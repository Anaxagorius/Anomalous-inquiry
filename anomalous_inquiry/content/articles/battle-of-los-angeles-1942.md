In the early hours of February 25, 1942 — just over ten weeks after the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor — the United States Army's anti-aircraft batteries opened fire over Los Angeles, California, in response to unidentified radar returns and visual observations of aerial objects over the city. The incident remains one of the most extensively documented and unexplained military UAP events of the Second World War era.

## Background: The Context of Fear

By February 1942, the Pacific Coast of the United States was in a state of heightened military alert. Pearl Harbor had occurred on December 7, 1941, and a Japanese submarine had shelled Fort Stevens, Oregon, on February 23, 1942 — the first attack on a continental U.S. military installation since the War of 1812. Civilian defence preparations, blackout protocols, and anti-aircraft units were on high readiness.

This context is essential to understanding the military response on February 25: an unidentified aerial contact over Los Angeles would have been assessed as a genuine threat. The decision to open fire was not reflexive but represented a considered, if rapid, command decision under realistic threat conditions.

## The Incident: Timeline and Events

### February 24: Initial Alerts

At approximately 7:18 PM on the evening of February 24, the Western Defense Command issued a Yellow Alert — warning of possible air attack — following intelligence reports of expected enemy activity near Los Angeles.

The alert was cancelled at 10:23 PM.

### February 25: The Engagement

At approximately 2:25 AM on February 25, 1942, radar operators at the Army's 37th Coast Artillery Brigade detected unidentified returns approaching from the sea. At 3:16 AM, a full air raid alarm was sounded across greater Los Angeles. A complete coastal blackout was ordered.

Between approximately 3:16 and 4:14 AM, anti-aircraft batteries of the 37th Coast Artillery Brigade fired approximately 1,440 rounds of 12.8-pound shells into the sky over Los Angeles. Searchlight beams swept the sky and converged on reported positions of the objects.

Thousands of civilian witnesses observed searchlight beams illuminating objects in the sky. Multiple civilians, military personnel, and police officers reported seeing structured aerial objects caught in the searchlights. Descriptions varied: some witnesses described a single large object; others reported multiple smaller ones.

No aircraft were shot down. No wreckage was recovered.

### Physical Consequences

The barrage, fired over a densely populated city, resulted in:

- Several deaths: contemporary reports cite three to five civilians killed by heart attacks attributed to the stress of the alert, and others killed by falling shell fragments
- Extensive property damage from falling shrapnel
- Significant disruption to war industry plants and civilian infrastructure

### The All Clear

The all-clear was sounded at 7:21 AM. The city of Los Angeles began assessing the events of the night.

## Official Explanations and Contradictions

### Navy Secretary Frank Knox

Later on February 25, Secretary of the Navy Frank Knox held a press conference and attributed the entire incident to "war nerves" — suggesting that no aircraft had been present and the episode represented a false alarm combined with mass anxiety.

### Army Secretary Henry Stimson

Army Secretary Henry Stimson offered a different explanation the same day, acknowledging that objects had indeed been tracked by radar and observed visually. Stimson suggested the objects might have been commercial aircraft, possibly being used in a deliberate psychological operation by the enemy, or perhaps a small number of unidentified civilian planes.

### Congressional Inquiry

The contradictory statements from the Navy and Army prompted a congressional inquiry. Members of Congress demanded clarification about whether enemy aircraft had been over Los Angeles. No conclusive official answer was provided.

### 1983 Office of Air Force History

In 1983, the Office of Air Force History attributed the incident to weather balloons, suggesting that radar operators had tracked meteorological balloons released in the area and that nervous gunners had opened fire without confirmed aerial threat.

The weather balloon explanation has been disputed by researchers, who note that it does not account for the visual observations of large structured objects caught in searchlights, the sustained multi-hour nature of the event, or the multiple consistent witness accounts of objects exhibiting controlled movement.

## Witness Accounts and Physical Evidence

### Military Personnel

Multiple anti-aircraft crews reported visually tracking objects in their searchlights and observing them move in ways inconsistent with balloons or known aircraft of the period. Some described objects that appeared to absorb fire without reaction.

Lieutenant William Gerstell of the 37th Coast Artillery stated that the objects were real and had been clearly observed by trained personnel.

### Civilian Witnesses

Hundreds of civilians reported observing objects caught in searchlights. The Los Angeles Times published front-page coverage on February 26 including a photograph — later widely reproduced — showing converging searchlight beams illuminating what appeared to be a large circular or ovoid object in the sky. The photograph has been the subject of subsequent debate about whether it shows an actual object or a convergence of searchlight beams.

### Radar Records

Radar records from the 37th Coast Artillery Brigade, reviewed under FOIA requests, confirmed that returns had been tracked approaching from the sea and moving over the metropolitan area. The nature of the radar targets — whether balloons, aircraft, or something else — has not been definitively established.

## Analysis and Assessments

### James McDonald

Atmospheric physicist and UAP researcher Dr James McDonald, in testimony before the United States Congress in 1968, cited the Battle of Los Angeles as one of several historical cases meriting serious scientific review. McDonald noted that the official explanations were mutually inconsistent and that the physical evidence — particularly the radar tracking and the sustained visual observations of structured objects — had not been adequately accounted for.

### Jacques Vallée

Vallée categorised the Battle of Los Angeles in his broader analysis of early UAP history, noting it as a case in which official explanations conflicted with each other and with the available evidence record, and in which the objects did not behave consistently with known aircraft or balloons.

### Contemporary Researchers

Researchers including Kevin Randle and others have noted that the incident's documentation — contemporary newspaper coverage, official military reports, congressional records, and the Air Force History account — provides an unusually rich archive, but one whose key facts remain in dispute. The phenomenon observed has not been explained to the satisfaction of all researchers.

## Photographic Evidence

The front-page photograph published in the Los Angeles Times on February 26, 1942, showing multiple searchlight beams converging on a bright region in the night sky, is the primary visual document of the incident. Analysis of the photograph has produced conflicting conclusions:

- Some researchers argue the bright region in the convergence of beams shows a large circular or disc-shaped object
- Sceptical analysts argue the bright area is simply the visual effect of multiple powerful searchlight beams meeting, with no object required to produce the image

The photograph is included in this record as a primary historical document. *Note: Its interpretation is contested and no definitive conclusion about what it shows has been established.*

## Classification

The Battle of Los Angeles is classified CE-1 under the Hynek scale: visual observation of unidentified aerial phenomena by multiple credible witnesses (including trained military personnel), supported by radar tracking, under sustained observation over an extended period. The event does not meet CE-2 criteria in the absence of confirmed physical effects directly attributable to the objects themselves.
