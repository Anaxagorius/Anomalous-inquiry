Between November 1989 and April 1991, Belgium experienced one of the most extensively documented mass UAP sighting events in modern history. Over this period, thousands of witnesses across the country reported large, silent, low-flying triangular objects bearing intense lights at each apex and a central spotlight. The Belgian Air Force conducted active radar tracking and launched F-16 intercept missions. Full investigation files were released to the public. The Belgian wave is frequently cited as the most institutionally transparent national response to a sustained UAP event on record.

## Overview and Scale

The Belgian wave involved:

- More than **13,500 reported witnesses** across Belgium
- Radar tracking by the Belgian Air Force and NATO systems
- Two **F-16 intercept sorties**, including achieved radar lock
- Full public release of investigation documentation by the Belgian Air Force
- Peer-reviewed technical analysis published in the Journal of Scientific Exploration
- A rare public acknowledgment by a national air force that the observed objects were "unidentified"

No conventional explanation has been established for the core events.

## The First Sightings: November 29, 1989

### Initial Reports Near Eupen

The wave began on the evening of Thursday, November 29, 1989, in the Eupen area of eastern Belgium, near the German border. Gendarmerie officers Heinrich Nicoll and Hubert Zwern, on patrol near Eupen, reported observing a large triangular object with lights at each corner and a central red light, hovering silently at low altitude. The object was estimated to be 30–40 metres in length.

Over the following hours, dozens of independent witnesses across the region reported similar or identical objects. The Belgian Gendarmerie logged approximately 143 separate testimonies from that first night alone.

### Physical Characteristics Reported

Across the November 29 sightings and subsequent reports, witnesses consistently described:

- **Triangular or delta-shaped platform**: Right-angled or equilateral triangle, with lights at each vertex
- **Three white spotlights** at the apices, and one central red or orange light
- **Absolute silence** or a very low sub-audible hum
- **Extremely low altitude**: Many reports of objects below 200 metres
- **Very slow movement**: Some objects reported stationary or hovering for extended periods
- **Rapid acceleration**: When the object departed, it did so instantly and at high speed
- **Scale**: Estimates varied, but most accounts described an object significantly larger than a commercial aircraft fuselage

## Sustained Activity: December 1989 – March 1990

The wave continued through December 1989 and January and February 1990. Sightings were reported across the whole of Belgium, including in and around Brussels. The Belgian Gendarmerie maintained a centralised logging system and received thousands of reports.

The objects appeared to favour low-altitude, slow-speed flight over populated areas, which facilitated observation but created puzzlement about any concealment purpose if the craft were of human origin.

## The F-16 Intercept Missions: March 30–31, 1990

### Background

By March 1990, the volume and consistency of reports prompted the Belgian Air Force to authorise active intercept attempts. This decision was notable: it represented an institutional acknowledgment that the observed phenomena were real aerial objects worth investigating, not mass illusion.

### The Intercept of March 30–31

On the night of March 30–31, 1990, the Belgian Air Force scrambled two F-16 Fighting Falcons from Beauvechain Air Base following multiple reports of triangular objects and confirmed radar tracking from ground stations.

The F-16s were guided to the target by ground-controlled intercept radar and their own onboard APG-66 radar systems.

**Radar Lock**: Both F-16 pilots achieved radar lock on an unidentified target on multiple occasions. On each occasion when lock was achieved, the target executed an immediate and extraordinarily rapid evasive manoeuvre.

**Performance Data**: Data recorded from the F-16 radar during the engagement, and subsequently analysed by the Belgian Air Force and independent researchers, showed the unidentified object accelerating from approximately 280 km/h to 1,800 km/h within seconds, while simultaneously descending from 3,000 metres to 1,700 metres in two seconds — a rate of descent approximately consistent with 40 g acceleration, far beyond any known aircraft or human physiology.

**Loss of Contact**: The target dropped off radar at very low altitude. The F-16 pilots did not achieve visual contact.

### Belgian Air Force Statement

In the days following the March 30–31 intercept, the Belgian Air Force held a press conference — an extraordinary act of public transparency — and described the incident in detail, including releasing the radar data and stating that the objects had been real, tracked, and unidentified. Colonel Wilfried De Brouwer, Chief of Operations of the Belgian Air Force, presented the technical findings and stated publicly that no conventional explanation accounted for the object's performance.

## Technical Analysis

### August Meessen

Professor August Meessen of the Catholic University of Louvain (UCL) conducted an in-depth technical analysis of the radar data from the March 30–31 intercept. His analysis, published in a special issue of the *Société Belge d'Études et de Recherches* in 1991 and in the *Journal of Scientific Exploration* in 2009, concluded:

- The radar returns were genuine: they could not be explained as anomalous propagation, ground clutter, or electronic countermeasure
- The accelerations recorded exceeded the capability of any known conventional aircraft or drone technology
- The evasive manoeuvres appeared to be reactive — the object's acceleration and direction change coincided precisely with acquisition of radar lock

Meessen's analysis remains one of the most technically detailed public assessments of a UAP-associated radar dataset.

### SOBEPS Investigation

The Société Belge d'Études et de Recherches sur les Phénomènes Spatiaux (SOBEPS), a Belgian civilian UAP research organisation, produced a two-volume investigation report covering the full wave: *Vague d'OVNI sur la Belgique* (Vol. 1, 1991; Vol. 2, 1993). The report included witness testimony, radar data analysis, photographic evidence assessment, and technical discussion.

The SOBEPS volumes remain the most comprehensive single documentation of the Belgian wave.

## Photographic Evidence

Several photographs purportedly showing the triangular objects were taken during the wave. The most widely reproduced — taken by an anonymous photographer in Petit-Rechain, Belgium, in 1990 — showed a triangular object with lights at each corner and a central glow.

In 2011, Patrick Maréchal publicly stated that he had fabricated the Petit-Rechain photograph using a model made of foam. His claim was accepted by most researchers as credible, and the photograph is now considered a hoax.

*Note: The existence of a fabricated photograph does not affect the evidential weight of the radar data, witness testimony, or the Air Force's analysis. Hoaxed photographs are unfortunately common in UAP cases and should be assessed independently of other evidence categories.*

No other photographs from the Belgian wave have been independently verified as depicting genuine anomalous objects.

## The NATO Connection

The Belgian Air Force coordinated with NATO during the wave, as Belgium hosts NATO infrastructure including the AWACS early warning radar fleet based at Geilenkirchen. NATO radar tracking is not publicly confirmed for specific engagements during the wave, and NATO has not publicly commented on whether its systems tracked the Belgian wave objects.

## Conventional Explanations Assessed

### B-2 Spirit or Advanced USAF Platform

Given the triangular shape and observed performance, some researchers and journalists speculated that the objects might be early or operational versions of the Northrop B-2 Spirit stealth bomber, or another classified USAF platform. The B-2 entered operational service in 1997; its configuration is broadly triangular.

The Belgian Air Force formally queried USAF whether any American aircraft were operating in Belgian airspace during the wave. The USAF denied the presence of any U.S. military aircraft in the area. This denial cannot be independently verified.

However, the B-2 and other known advanced aircraft cannot account for the observed characteristics: the objects' extremely low speed (as slow as hovering), absolute silence, the performance recorded in the March 30–31 radar data, and the scale of reports across a small, densely populated country.

### Other Conventional Explanations

Atmospheric phenomena, blimps, conventional aircraft, or drones do not match the performance characteristics of the core evidential record.

## Colonel Wilfried De Brouwer

Colonel Wilfried De Brouwer, Chief of Operations of the Belgian Air Force during the wave, became the public face of the Belgian military's response. He was unusual among serving military officers in that he continued to speak openly about the wave after his retirement, including contributing a chapter to Leslie Kean's *UFOs: Generals, Pilots, and Government Officials Go on the Record* (2010).

De Brouwer stated in Kean's book:

> "Hundreds of people saw these things. I know these sightings are real. The question is, what are they? I have no idea."

His willingness to engage publicly and honestly with the evidence record distinguishes the Belgian institutional response from most national-government handling of UAP cases.

## Legacy and Research

The Belgian wave is cited as a landmark case in UAP research for several reasons:

1. **Institutional transparency**: The Belgian Air Force's release of investigation files, press conference, and public statements are without parallel in military UAP disclosure
2. **Technical record**: The radar data from the March 30–31 intercept represents one of the best-documented UAP sensor records
3. **Witness volume and quality**: Over 13,500 witnesses including trained military and civilian personnel
4. **Cross-national corroboration**: Reports also came from adjacent regions of Germany, the Netherlands, and France during the wave period

The case is included in most serious academic treatments of the UAP phenomenon and is a reference point in discussions of the evidentiary standard required to treat the UAP question seriously.

## Classification

The Belgian wave is classified **CE-1 / CE-2** under the Hynek scale:

- **CE-1**: Visual observation of unidentified aerial phenomena at close range by multiple witnesses
- **CE-2**: Sensor effects — specifically, radar tracking with documented anomalous performance data representing associated physical instrument recording consistent with CE-2 criteria

No CE-3 elements (entity observation) are documented in the Belgian wave.

## Disclaimer

This article documents the Belgian UFO wave as a historical case record. Witness accounts, radar data, and official documentation are presented as recorded. The Petit-Rechain photograph is identified as a confirmed hoax and excluded from the evidential record. CE classification reflects reported characteristics and sensor data. No explanation, conclusion, or origin is asserted.
