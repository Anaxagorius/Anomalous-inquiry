Intentionality — the property of mental states of being "about" or "directed toward" something — is a foundational concept in the philosophy of mind and phenomenology. Within parapsychological research, intentionality has received attention both as a structural feature of psi experiences (anomalous cognition is typically experienced as awareness of something specific, not a diffuse state) and as a potential operative variable in experimental designs: many psi experiments place the participant in a state of directed intention toward a target. The relationship between conscious intentional states and psi effects — whether intention causes, mediates, or merely accompanies anomalous outcomes — is a central and unresolved question in theoretical parapsychology.

## Intentionality in Philosophy of Mind

Franz Brentano (1874) proposed intentionality as the "mark of the mental" — the feature that distinguishes mental states from physical states. Every mental act, Brentano argued, is directed toward an intentional object: believing, desiring, imagining, and perceiving are all about something. Edmund Husserl developed this insight into a systematic phenomenology, distinguishing the act of consciousness (noesis) from its intentional content (noema) and from the actual object that may or may not correspond to that content.

In contemporary philosophy of mind, intentionality remains central to debates between representationalism (which identifies mental content with representational states of the brain), phenomenological approaches (which locate intentionality in the first-person structure of lived experience), and externalism (which holds that the contents of mental states are partly constituted by the external world or social environment, not solely by internal states of the brain).

## Intention in Parapsychological Experiments

Experimental parapsychology has placed intentional states in a complex and ambiguous role. Most psi experiments require subjects to form an intention: to "send" a target image, to "receive" impressions from a remote target, or to influence a random process. Whether the intentional act is causally operative, merely coincident with the psi effect, or actually counterproductive has been the subject of theoretical debate.

### The Problem of Effort and Intention

A recurrent finding in parapsychological research — documented across forced-choice, free-response, and REG paradigms — is that conscious, effortful striving to perform psi does not reliably improve performance and may sometimes impair it. This is sometimes described as the "trying paradox" or related to the observed tendency for psi effects to be stronger under relaxed conditions (the Ganzfeld state, hypnagogic states, or meditative states) than under deliberate analytical attention.

Rex Stanford's PMIR model explicitly predicted that psi would be most evident when operating non-intentionally, in service of biological needs, without conscious awareness of the psi process. Some researchers have interpreted this as evidence that the relevant intentional state is not the deliberate volitional effort but a more primitive, pre-reflective directedness toward a goal — closer to Husserl's notion of passive intentional synthesis than to deliberate voluntary action.

### Directed Intention Research

William Braud and Marilyn Schlitz conducted a series of DMILS (distant mental interaction with living systems) experiments in the 1980s and 1990s in which a distant sender was asked to direct calm or activating attention toward a shielded receiver, with the receiver's electrodermal activity as the dependent measure. Their early studies reported significant sender–receiver correlations, and subsequent analyses examined whether the sender's subjective experience of successful intentional contact correlated with physiological outcomes. The results were suggestive but not consistent.

Braud later developed a broader framework for intentional influence in which he distinguished between "usual" intention (deliberate effort to cause a specific outcome) and a more non-attached, receptive mode of intentional engagement that he associated with greater experimental success. He connected this to contemplative traditions — particularly Buddhist and Christian mystical frameworks — in which non-grasping awareness is described as more conducive to effective intention than aggressive, outcome-focused effort.

### Retroactive Intention and Retrocausality

Several experimental designs have examined whether intention formed after an event can influence that event retroactively. Helmut Schmidt's pre-recorded random events studies presented participants with random binary sequences generated weeks or months before the experimental session, asking them to intend that a particular sequence of outcomes (e.g., more ones than zeros) was present. Statistically significant effects were reported, implying that either the random generation had been influenced retrocausally, or — on decision augmentation theory — that participants' selection of which pre-recorded sequences to use was guided by precognitive access to the sequence content.

Daryl Bem's 2011 paper in the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, which reported nine experiments on "retroactive facilitation" — effects of future stimuli on present responses — drew wide attention and controversy. Bem's designs were conventional-looking social psychology experiments adapted to detect retrocausal influence. The paper's publication in a mainstream journal triggered a large replication effort and was a significant catalyst for the replication crisis discussion in psychology.

## Phenomenological Accounts of Psi Experience

Phenomenological analysis of spontaneous psi cases and experimental session reports suggests that anomalous cognition, when experienced subjectively, shares the directedness characteristic of normal perception and imagination: the experiencer is typically aware of something — a specific place, person, or event — rather than experiencing a uniform information flood.

Charles Honorton noted that Ganzfeld subjects' verbal reports during sessions often described vivid hypnagogic imagery that appeared to track the target content without the subject being aware of the correspondence. This has been interpreted as consistent with a model in which anomalous information reaches awareness through imagery and sensory metaphor rather than through propositional thought — consistent with Frederic Myers' concept of the "subliminal self" as the primary locus of psi reception.

### Agency and Passivity

A tension runs through psi experience accounts between reports of passive reception — in which information appears to arrive without deliberate seeking — and reports of active agency, in which an experiencer felt that they directed their attention toward a target and retrieved information. This tension maps onto a longstanding philosophical debate about the relationship between passive affection and active constitution in intentional experience.

Some researchers have proposed that the most productive approach involves what Carlos Alvarado calls "psi-conducive states" — alterations of ordinary consciousness that loosen the strong intentional directedness of everyday cognition, making room for anomalous information to surface. This has motivated the development of ganzfeld, hypnotic, and meditative protocols.

## Intentionality and the Question of a Psi Agent

A philosophically central question is whether psi requires an agent — a person with conscious intentions — or can occur in the apparent absence of any intending mind. Poltergeist phenomena have been interpreted as cases where physical effects occur around a particular person (the "focus") who may be unaware of causing them. REG experiments have examined whether unattended machines show statistical anomalies in emotionally significant environments (the Global Consciousness Project). Both lines of investigation suggest that, if psi occurs, it may not require deliberate intention — which raises the question of what the relevant "intentional" structure is.

### The Global Consciousness Project

The Global Consciousness Project (Radin, Nelson, and colleagues) operates a network of random event generators at sites worldwide, analysing their output for correlated deviations during globally significant events (elections, disasters, commemorations). The project operates on the hypothesis that collective human attention — a distributed, non-deliberate form of shared intentional focus — produces detectable physical correlations. The project's results have been reported as statistically significant by its operators and contested on methodological grounds by critics. It represents the most ambitious attempt to operationalise collective intentionality as a psi variable.

## Assessment

The relationship between intentionality and psi is a genuinely complex theoretical and empirical question. Deliberate intention appears neither necessary nor sufficient for psi effects in the experimental literature; relaxed, non-grasping mental states are often associated with better performance than effortful striving. Phenomenological analysis of psi experience reveals the characteristic directedness of mental states, consistent with ordinary intentional structure, while also pointing to distinctive features (imagery, subliminal processing, spontaneous arising) that suggest anomalous information surfaces through non-deliberate cognitive channels. A satisfactory theoretical account of how intentional states relate to psi outcomes — whether as cause, correlate, or mere accompaniment — has not yet been achieved.
