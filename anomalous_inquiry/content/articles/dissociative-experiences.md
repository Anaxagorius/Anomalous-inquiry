Dissociation designates a spectrum of psychological states involving the partial or complete disruption of the ordinarily integrated functions of consciousness, memory, identity, emotion, perception, and behaviour. While clinically severe dissociation is associated with trauma-related pathology, a substantial range of dissociative experiences occur in the general population without clinical significance and are considered normal variants of consciousness. These non-pathological dissociative states have attracted sustained interest from parapsychology and consciousness research due to their structural overlaps with OBEs, trance states, and other anomalous experiences, and due to empirical associations between dissociative traits and anomalous experience reports.

## The Dissociation Spectrum

Dissociation exists along a continuum from everyday experiences to clinically significant disorders. The non-pathological range includes:

**Absorption** — a trait-level capacity for deep attentional engagement with internal or external stimuli, during which peripheral awareness is reduced. High absorption is associated with vivid imaginative engagement, intense aesthetic experience, and, experimentally, hypnotic susceptibility. It is measured by instruments including Tellegen's Absorption Scale.

**Depersonalisation** — transient states in which the self feels unreal, automated, or detached from one's own thoughts, feelings, or body; frequently described as watching oneself from the outside. Occurs commonly following sleep deprivation, meditation, extreme stress, and mild psychoactive use without reaching disorder threshold.

**Derealisation** — states in which the external environment appears unreal, dreamlike, or artificial. Commonly co-occurring with depersonalisation; triggered by the same contexts.

**Highway hypnosis and automatic behaviour** — loss of conscious attentive monitoring during routine activity (most commonly driving), with behaviour continuing competently while awareness seems absent or disengaged.

**Flow states** — characterised by effortless absorption, reduced self-consciousness, and altered time perception during skilled activity; described by Csikszentmihalyi as distinct from pathological dissociation but sharing the feature of attenuated reflective self-monitoring.

**Imaginative involvement** — the capacity to become deeply engaged with fiction, music, or mental imagery to the point of partial confusion between imagined and perceived reality.

## Measurement

The primary instruments used to assess dissociative experiences include:

- **Dissociative Experiences Scale (DES)** — Bernstein and Putnam (1986); 28-item self-report scale; the most widely used research instrument; distinguishes a DES-II sub-scale associated with pathological amnesia and depersonalisation from the broader absorption factor
- **Tellegen Absorption Scale (TAS)** — measuring the non-pathological absorption trait most associated with hypnotic susceptibility and paranormal experience
- **Cambridge Depersonalisation Scale** — specifically targeting transient depersonalisation phenomena
- **Multiscale Dissociation Inventory (MDI)**

## Dissociation and Anomalous Experience

A consistent empirical finding across multiple independent studies is a positive association between dissociative traits — particularly absorption — and reports of anomalous or paranormal experiences:

**Paranormal belief and experience:** Studies including those by Pekala, Kumar, and Cummings (1992) and subsequent replications have found that individuals scoring higher on dissociation measures report more frequent and vivid anomalous experiences including apparitions, telepathic impressions, OBEs, and premonitory dreams. The association is robust and replicates across cultures.

**OBEs and dissociation:** Out-of-body experiences are consistently associated with higher DES and TAS scores in survey populations. Whether this reflects a common neurological substrate, a heightened tendency to code unusual experiences in anomalous terms, or a genuine propensity for anomalous events in high-dissociators remains a methodologically open question.

**Mediumship:** Research with claimed mediums has documented elevated dissociation scores, particularly on absorption and imaginative involvement sub-scales. Studies by Roxburgh and Roe (2011) and others found that practising mediums score significantly higher than the general population on the DES, but within a range more consistent with non-pathological absorption than with trauma-related pathology.

**Psi performance in laboratory studies:** Several investigators have examined whether dissociative traits predict performance on controlled ESP tasks. Results are mixed, with some studies reporting that high absorbers or high DES scorers show better anomalous performance on free-response tasks such as the ganzfeld.

## Structural Parallels with Anomalous States

Non-pathological dissociative states share several functional characteristics with other anomalous experience contexts:

- Reduced dominance of the ordinary reflective self-monitoring mechanism
- Increased susceptibility to vivid, ego-syntonic imagery
- Loosened personal identity boundaries
- Altered time and body perception
- Enhanced internal perceptual prominence relative to external stimuli

These characteristics parallel the phenomenology of hypnagogic states, mediumistic trance, and ganzfeld conditions. The convergence has led some researchers to propose a unified "receiver" profile in which individuals with naturally higher dissociative and absorption traits may be more consistently associated with anomalous experience and possibly with anomalous performance in controlled conditions.

## Critical Perspectives

Mainstream psychological accounts of the dissociation-anomalous experience correlation attribute it to psychological factors without requiring anomalous causation:

- High dissociators may be more prone to source monitoring errors — misattributing internally generated imagery and thought to external or anomalous sources
- Absorption is associated with vivid mental imagery that may be more easily coded as externally perceived
- Dissociative boundary-attenuation may increase the likelihood of borderline perceptual experiences being interpreted as anomalous

These accounts predict the statistical correlation without requiring that the experiences involve genuine anomalous information acquisition. Parapsychological researchers have noted that such accounts do not in themselves resolve whether genuine anomalous content may additionally be present in a subset of such experiences.

## Assessment

The association between non-pathological dissociative traits and anomalous experience is one of the most replicable empirical findings at the intersection of parapsychology and personality psychology. Dissociation measures provide useful instruments for characterising experiencer populations. The mechanistic interpretation of this association — whether it reflects genuine anomalous receptivity, psychological artefact, or both — is an open empirical question that current designs have not yet resolved.
