Spiritualism — the belief that the spirits of the dead survive bodily death and can communicate with the living, typically through a human intermediary (medium) — emerged as a popular religious and social movement in the mid-nineteenth century and produced a distinctive investigative literature that forms part of the historical foundation of parapsychology. This article addresses the movement as an object of empirical investigation rather than as a religious or metaphysical system.

## Origins of the Movement

The popular spiritualist movement is conventionally dated to March 1848, when reports emerged from Hydesville, New York, of strange rapping sounds occurring in the home of John and Margaret Fox, interpreted by the family as communications from a deceased former tenant. Kate and Margaretta Fox, the teenage daughters, became the first widely publicised mediums of the era, communicating with alleged spirits through a coded rapping system.

The Fox case spread rapidly through American religious and social networks, generating imitators and a popular hunger for spirit communication. Within a decade, spiritualism had attracted millions of adherents in the United States and had crossed the Atlantic to Britain, where it found ready audiences in the Victorian culture of bereavement, religious uncertainty, and enthusiasm for scientific novelty.

## Investigative Motivations

The scientific investigation of spiritualism was motivated by several distinct concerns:

- **Fraud detection** — determining whether mediumistic phenomena were produced by trickery
- **Survival research** — assessing whether evidence of post-mortem survival could be obtained under conditions that excluded fraud
- **Physical phenomena** — examining claims of materialisation, levitation, telekinesis, and related physical effects under controlled conditions
- **Mental phenomena** — evaluating trance communications, apparitions, and apparent knowledge of unknown facts for evidence of genuine information acquisition

The spectrum of investigators ranged from hostile debunkers (seeking to expose fraud for moral and religious reasons) to credulous believers, with a smaller number attempting genuinely impartial empirical assessment. The results reflect this spectrum.

## Physical Mediumship: Notable Cases and Investigations

The Victorian period produced numerous individuals claiming to produce physical phenomena in mediumistic séances. Investigation of these claims produced mixed findings:

**Daniel Dunglas Home (1833–1886)** — the most extensively investigated physical medium of the period, Home was tested by numerous investigators including physicist William Crookes (1870–1873), who reported observing levitation, elongation of Home's body, materialisation of hands, and movement of objects without contact under conditions he described as rigorously controlled. Home was never formally exposed as a fraud despite sustained attempts; this absence of exposure has been interpreted both as evidence for genuine phenomena and as evidence of exceptionally skilled evasion. Magician Harry Houdini and others argued that the conditions described by Crookes were not actually fraud-proof.

**Eusapia Palladino (1854–1918)** — an Italian peasant medium whose séances included levitation of tables, materialised faces and hands, and apparent telekinesis. Palladino was repeatedly caught cheating in controlled conditions — using her freed foot or hand to produce effects — but was also reported to produce phenomena under conditions that her investigators claimed precluded ordinary explanation. The SPR conducted multiple series of investigations; the 1908 Naples sittings (by Feilding, Baggally, and Carrington) were reported as producing genuine unexplained effects even under careful control. The consensus interpretation in later parapsychology is that Palladino cheated when she could and produced genuine effects when adequately controlled — a position uncomfortable for both believers and sceptics.

**Rudi Schneider (1908–1957)** — an Austrian physical medium studied extensively in the 1920s and 1930s. Schneider was investigated by Harry Price and others using photographic and instrumental monitoring. Price published photographs purportedly showing Schneider releasing a controlled arm to manipulate objects — a charge Schneider disputed. The case illustrated the limitations of photographic evidence in séance conditions.

## Mental Mediumship: Notable Cases

**Leonora Piper (1859–1950)** — Mrs Piper was the medium most extensively studied by the SPR. Richard Hodgson, initially a determined debunker, became convinced of her genuineness after years of investigation including covert surveillance (to determine whether she gathered information by normal means) and proxy sittings (in which sitters were unknown to Piper in advance). The quantity and specificity of apparently correct information produced by Piper in trance states is extensively documented in the SPR proceedings. Her most discussed communicator was the ostensible spirit "Dr Phinuit," later replaced by the "Imperator Group."

**Gladys Osborne Leonard (1882–1968)** — studied by the SPR from approximately 1915 onward, Leonard was the primary medium through whom the "cross-correspondences" were investigated. These were independent communications from different mediums in different countries that appeared to interlock in ways that suggested coordination by a common intelligence (the ostensible communicating entities).

## The Fox Sisters' Confession

In 1888, Margaretta Fox publicly confessed that the original Hydesville rappings had been produced by cracking the toe and knee joints. She subsequently recanted the confession, but the episode permanently complicated the historical status of the founding case of the movement. Investigators who had already spent years examining the phenomena were divided between those who accepted the confession and those who attributed it to financial pressure or unreliability.

## The Investigative Legacy

The investigation of early spiritualism produced several lasting methodological contributions:

- Recognition that well-intentioned witnesses are unreliable under conditions designed to exploit inattention and expectation
- Early development of control procedures for séance conditions (holding hands, seating in positions of observation, photographic monitoring, use of physical restraints)
- The proxy and book test designs for eliminating cold reading and prior research as explanations for apparently veridical mediumistic information
- Documentation of a range of phenomena that established the research agenda for twentieth-century parapsychology

## Assessment

Early spiritualism, viewed as an object of empirical investigation, presents a complex evidential record. Fraud was widespread and well-documented across the movement. However, several specific cases — particularly mental mediumship cases investigated by the SPR under controlled conditions — produced results that have not been fully accounted for by fraud, prior research, cold reading, or chance coincidence. This archive documents the investigated record without endorsing spiritualist metaphysical conclusions.
