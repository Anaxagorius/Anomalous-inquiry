Environmental psi effects are a category of anomalous physical phenomena in which the output of physical systems — particularly random or stochastic processes — appears to show statistical deviations correlated with psychological states, collective human attention, or intentions directed at the environment. The category encompasses laboratory-based random event generator (REG) research, field investigations of haunted or anomalous locations, and ambitious proposals such as the Global Consciousness Project that attempt to detect correlated deviations in random systems associated with globally shared human attention.

## Conceptual Background

The hypothesis that mind can influence the physical environment beyond the ordinary neuromuscular pathways of the body has a long history in parapsychology under the label of psychokinesis (PK). Early PK research focused on macroscopic effects: the movement of objects, the bending of metals (Uri Geller-type phenomena), and the stopping of clocks. Modern environmental psi research is methodologically more rigorous, focusing on micro-level statistical deviations in physical systems rather than macroscopic events, which are far more susceptible to undetected fraud.

The shift from macro-PK to micro-PK research was substantially driven by Helmut Schmidt, who from the late 1960s onward developed electronic random event generators — devices whose output was determined by quantum-level radioactive decay events — as psi-proof targets for psychokinesis experiments. If the outcome of a quantum process cannot be predicted or physically influenced by any known means, a systematic deviation in its statistics correlated with human intention would constitute evidence of anomalous physical influence.

## Random Event Generator Research

### Schmidt's REG Studies

Helmut Schmidt's programme of REG research, conducted at the Mind Science Foundation and subsequently elsewhere, employed binary random output from radioactive decay sources. In a typical study, a participant observed a display showing which of four lamps (illuminated by random binary output) was active, and attempted to increase the frequency of one lamp over chance expectation of 25%. Cumulative results across a large number of sessions were reported as significantly above chance.

Schmidt developed the concept of "pre-recorded target studies" — in which the random sequence was generated and recorded before the experiment, and the participant directed intention toward the tape's outcome retrospectively. This design was intended to rule out conventional physical influence on the real-time generator and to test for retrocausal PK. Positive results from pre-recorded designs were taken by Schmidt as evidence that the anomalous effect was not a conventional energetic influence on the generator.

### The PEAR REG Programme

The Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research (PEAR) laboratory, under Robert Jahn and Brenda Dunne, conducted the most extensive REG research programme from 1979 to 2007. Using a Michelson random mechanical cascade (a device in which polystyrene balls fell through a pegboard to produce a Gaussian distribution) and electronic REGs, the PEAR programme accumulated approximately 12 million experimental trials across hundreds of participants. The operators (participants) were instructed to attempt to shift the distribution in a specified direction (high intention), in the opposite direction (low intention), or to provide a control baseline (neutral).

A comprehensive analysis published by Jahn and Dunne in 1987 (*Foundations of Physics*) found small but consistent deviations in the intended direction across the REG and cascade data, with an overall significance of p < 10⁻¹¹ for the electronic REG database. The effect size was very small (approximately 0.6 standard deviations per 10,000 bits in either direction from chance), implying that the effect would be undetectable in any individual session and only emerged as statistically robust across the aggregate database.

Replications by independent laboratories produced mixed results. A meta-analysis by Radin and Nelson (1989), covering 832 studies from 68 experimenters, reported a combined effect significantly above chance. A subsequent analysis by Bösch, Steinkamp, and Boller (2006) covering a similar database found a significant overall effect but identified a decline effect (positive results declining over time and in larger, more rigorous studies) that raised concerns about publication bias. Radin and Nelson contested this analysis.

### Experimenter and Operator Effects

A notable feature of the REG literature is the significant variation in effect sizes across operators (participants) and across experimenters. Some individuals consistently show much larger effects than the mean; others show null results; and a small proportion show results significantly below chance (termed "psi-missing"). The clustering of results around specific experimenters — some of whom consistently obtain significant positive effects across different participants and equipment — has been interpreted by proponents as evidence of an experimenter-level psi component and by critics as evidence of methodological variability.

## Field REG Research and the Global Consciousness Project

### Field REG Effects

The discovery that REG devices operating in the field — at public events, in groups of meditating individuals, and in emotionally resonant social settings — appeared to show anomalous deviations led to the development of field REG research. Roger Nelson, Jahn, and Dunne at PEAR reported deviations in REG output at group meditations, musical performances, and collective ceremonies. These reports were exploratory rather than formally pre-registered.

### The Global Consciousness Project

The Global Consciousness Project (GCP), initiated by Roger Nelson in 1998, extended field REG research to a global scale. The GCP operates a network of approximately 70 hardware random event generators at sites worldwide (including Europe, North America, Asia, and Australia), continuously recording their output. The output data are collected and archived centrally. For hypothesised "global events" — occasions of widespread shared human attention, including terrorist attacks, major sporting events, natural disasters, and commemorative occasions — the GCP analyses whether the network's aggregate output deviates from chance expectations in a pre-specified window around the event.

The GCP has reported cumulative significance over its operational history across hundreds of pre-specified events. As of reports through 2015, the cumulative result was approximately p = 10⁻⁴ over approximately 500 events. Proponents interpret this as evidence that global collective human consciousness produces measurable physical effects on random systems.

Critics have raised several methodological concerns:

- **Event selection**: The pre-specification of events is not fully independent of cultural prominence, and the definition of the relevant time window introduces analytic flexibility.
- **Multiple comparisons**: With approximately 70 generators and various analysis options, correction for multiple comparisons has been debated.
- **Network correlations**: Physical (atmospheric, seismic) correlations among geographically distributed REG devices could introduce spurious apparent effects; the GCP has addressed this to some degree but not exhaustively.
- **Effect mechanism**: No mechanism has been proposed by which collective human attention could physically influence geographically dispersed quantum devices simultaneously.

The GCP remains one of the most ambitious empirical projects in parapsychology but also one of the most contested, with disagreement extending to the basic statistical methodology as well as interpretation.

## Geomagnetic and Environmental Correlates of Psi

Several researchers have investigated whether environmental physical variables modulate psi performance or the incidence of spontaneous anomalous experiences:

### Geomagnetic Activity and Psi

Michael Persinger and colleagues systematically examined whether geomagnetic activity (measured by geomagnetic K-indices) correlated with the occurrence of spontaneous anomalous experiences including apparitions, telepathic impressions, and out-of-body experiences. They reported inverse correlations: anomalous experiences were more frequent during periods of geomagnetic calm and less frequent during geomagnetic storms. Persinger proposed that geomagnetic activity influenced temporal lobe function in ways that either facilitated or suppressed anomalous cognition.

A number of independent studies examined geomagnetic correlates of psi performance in laboratory experiments. Results have been mixed, with some positive correlations reported in forced-choice and free-response studies. No consistent pattern has emerged.

### Local Sidereal Time

James Spottiswoode (1997) reported an analysis of free-response anomalous cognition studies suggesting that psi performance correlated with local sidereal time (LST), with a peak in effect size approximately 13.5 hours LST — a time associated with reduced ambient galactic radio noise reaching the Earth. This finding has been replicated in some analyses of additional datasets and not replicated in others. No mechanism connecting sidereal time to anomalous cognition has been proposed.

## Distant Healing and Environmental Intention

Research on distant healing and intention-based effects on biological and physical systems extends the environmental psi framework beyond purely physical random systems. Studies by William Braud and Marilyn Schlitz examined whether human physiological systems (electrodermal activity) could be influenced by remote intention. Meta-analyses of this literature (e.g., by Astin, Harkness, and Ernst, 2000; Schmidt, Schneider, Utts, and Walach, 2004) report small but statistically significant effects. The direct healing intentions literature, reviewed by Levin (2011), reports similar small effects from randomised controlled trials, though methodological quality varies widely.

Whether anomalous physiological effects on living systems (DMILS, distant healing) constitute a form of environmental psi — anomalous influence on a physical system — or a separate phenomenon (anomalous information acquisition followed by physiological response on the part of the receiver) is a theoretical question not resolved by the data.

## Assessment

Environmental psi research provides one of the more methodologically rigorous empirical approaches to anomalous physical influence, because it uses physical systems — random quantum processes — that are in principle beyond normal sensory or physical influence, and it produces continuous, quantifiable, time-stamped output amenable to statistical analysis. The meta-analytic record across REG studies reports small but statistically robust effects that survive correction for publication bias under some analytical approaches. The principal unresolved issues are replication consistency across laboratories and experimenters, the contested meta-analytical conclusions regarding decline effects and publication bias, and the total absence of a physically plausible mechanism. The Global Consciousness Project remains the most ambitious attempt to scale this research to collective and environmental contexts, and its methodology and results continue to be debated.
