Experimental design in psi research faces a set of methodological challenges both shared with other areas of empirical inquiry and specific to the nature of the phenomena being studied. The history of parapsychology can be read in part as a succession of increasingly rigorous design iterations in response to the identification of methodological flaws, both by internal researchers and by external critics. Understanding the design standards that have evolved in the field is necessary for evaluating its evidential record.

## Core Design Requirements

Psi research shares the fundamental requirements of any experimental science: pre-specification of hypotheses, appropriate control conditions, randomisation, blinding, sample size determination, and independent replication. Where psi research has historically diverged from these standards, critics have identified the divergences as the more likely explanation for positive results. Where it has met them, the debate has shifted to interpretation.

### Randomisation

Many early psi studies used manually generated random sequences for target selection. Later analysis revealed that such sequences contain systematic patterns — humans tend to avoid immediate repetition and alternating patterns — which could introduce systematic biases exploitable by participants who use simple strategies. Subsequent protocols have required hardware random number generators (RNGs), quantum-based random sources, or cryptographic sequences verified by independent parties.

### Blinding

Blinding in psi experiments presents specific complications:

- **Single-blind** — the participant does not know which condition they are in; adequate for many designs but insufficient if the experimenter who interacts with participants knows the target condition
- **Double-blind** — neither participant nor the experimenter present during the session knows the target; required for most credible forced-choice and free-response designs
- **Triple-blind** — neither participant, session experimenter, nor evaluator knows the target at the time of the trial; required for free-response designs where subjective matching is involved

The autoganzfeld protocol developed by Honorton and colleagues represented a significant advance in blinding by automating target selection and sender presentation, eliminating experimenter access to target identity during trials.

### Sensory Leakage Controls

In free-response paradigms such as remote viewing, Ganzfeld, and mediumship research, the primary methodological concern is sensory leakage — the possibility that information about the target reaches the participant through ordinary sensory channels (direct communication, environmental cues, structural features of target pools, or post-session unblinding errors).

Design requirements to address sensory leakage include:

- Physical isolation of sender and receiver
- Verified acoustic and electromagnetic shielding
- Target selection after isolation has begun
- Blind judging conducted by an independent evaluator who has never seen the original session
- Independent audit of randomisation records

## Target Pool Design

In forced-choice and free-response paradigms, the construction of target pools is itself a methodological variable. Several biases in target construction have been identified:

- **Structural redundancy** — if targets in a four-alternative set share visual, semantic, or thematic features that systematically differ from alternatives, chance-level analysis does not apply
- **Preference biases** — certain target categories are preferentially attended to or described by participants regardless of target identity, inflating hit rates for those categories
- **Matching biases in blind judging** — judges who evaluate free-response material against target pools may apply systematic preferences in ranking

Standard practice requires that target pools be constructed with matched alternatives on relevant perceptual dimensions, and that alternative selection for judging be performed by a party with no access to session material.

## Statistical Standards

### Multiple Comparisons

Psi research has historically been susceptible to inflated Type I error through multiple dependent and independent comparisons without appropriate adjustment. Analyses that test many outcome measures and report only significant findings represent a form of post-hoc selective reporting even in otherwise well-designed studies. Preregistration — registering hypotheses and analysis plans before data collection — now addresses this problem, though adoption in parapsychology has been uneven.

### Effect Size and Power

Early psi research was often characterised by very large sample sizes pursued to achieve significance on small effects. Modern standards emphasise reporting effect sizes independently of statistical significance, and computing a priori power analyses to determine required sample sizes. An underpowered study that fails to replicate a small but real effect is uninformative; an overpowered study that reports significance for a trivially small effect may be misleading.

### Base Rate Analysis

In free-response paradigms, the expected hit rate under chance depends on the design. In a four-alternative forced-choice design, chance expectation is 25%. In open free-response designs, the expected match rate is not analytically specified and must be established empirically through control conditions or simulation-based permutation analysis.

## Replication Standards

The appropriate standard for claiming an experimental psi effect is independent replication in at least one laboratory not involved in the original study, using a pre-specified protocol and analysis plan. Several paradigms in the literature have met this standard to varying degrees (the Ganzfeld and presentiment paradigms are the most extensively replicated); many reported effects have not been independently tested.

## Pre-Registration

From the 2010s onward, pre-registration of psi studies on platforms such as the Open Science Framework has become increasingly common. Pre-registration does not eliminate methodological problems but does meaningfully constrain post-hoc analysis and selective reporting. Several high-profile pre-registered psi studies have reported null or marginal results, and several have reported positive findings; the pre-registered record is more mixed than the historical non-pre-registered record.

## The Role of Experimenter Effects

A design-specific challenge in psi research is the documented relationship between experimenter identity and experimental outcome — the so-called "experimenter effect." This phenomenon, addressed separately in this archive, complicates the use of replication as a straightforward validity check, since a failure to replicate might reflect differences in experimenter characteristics rather than the absence of an underlying effect.

## Assessment

Parapsychological experimental design has evolved substantially from the Rhine-era protocols toward rigorous modern standards. The evaluation of any specific finding requires examining the design generation and randomisation of targets, the degree and integrity of blinding at each stage, sensory leakage controls, the statistical analysis plan and its relationship to prior hypotheses, and the replication record. These criteria, applied consistently, permit meaningful discrimination within the literature rather than wholesale dismissal or acceptance.
