Experimenter effects refer to the systematic influence of the researcher conducting an experiment on the results obtained, beyond what can be attributed to differences in methodology, participant populations, or random variation. In mainstream psychology, experimenter effects have been documented since Robert Rosenthal's work in the 1960s — researchers who expect particular results tend to obtain them, even in studies of animal behaviour where demand characteristics are implausible. In parapsychology, experimenter effects present an additional layer of complexity because the experimenter's expectations and beliefs may interact with the very phenomena under study in ways that have no analogue in standard psychophysics.

## The Experimenter Effect in Context

In Rosenthal's classic series of studies, experimenters told that their rats were "maze-bright" or "maze-dull" consistently obtained results consistent with those labels, despite no actual genetic difference between the two groups. Rosenthal attributed this to subtle differences in how experimenters handled, timed, and recorded results when they expected superior performance. Subsequent work found analogous effects in classroom settings, psychotherapy outcomes, and interview studies.

In these contexts, the experimenter effect is explained by ordinary channels: unconscious body language, differential attention, or minor variations in procedure that systematically influence participant behaviour. The effect is real and important, but its mechanism is conventional.

## Parapsychological Experimenter Effects

In psi research, the experimenter effect takes a potentially more radical form. A pattern documented across multiple research programmes is that psi effects appear to be associated with specific experimenters in ways that cannot be fully attributed to identifiable procedural differences. Some experimenters consistently obtain above-chance results; others consistently obtain null results; and formal attempts to identify the procedural differences do not account for the discrepancy.

Key features of the parapsychological experimenter effect include:

- **Psi-conducive versus psi-inhibitory experimenters** — the concept, associated with Helmut Schmidt and later developed by Rex Stanford and others, holds that experimenters may actively facilitate or inhibit psi effects through mechanisms that include but may also transcend ordinary interpersonal channels
- **Experimenter belief as a predictor** — multiple studies have found that experimenter belief in psi is significantly associated with positive experimental outcomes across different experimental contexts (White, 1976; Kennedy and Taddonio, 1976)
- **Cross-laboratory divergence** — systematic attempts to replicate psi findings in new laboratories using the same protocol frequently obtain divergent results even when procedural adherence is verified; differences in experimenter personnel are the primary uncontrolled variable

## The "Psi of the Experimenter" Hypothesis

Some theorists within parapsychology have proposed that experimenters are not merely passive conduits for psi effects but may themselves be sources of psi influence on experimental outcomes. Under this hypothesis, a psi-believing experimenter who unconsciously "wants" a positive outcome may exert direct anomalous influence on the outcome — either on the biological or physical target, or on the randomisation process — producing effects that masquerade as participant psi.

This hypothesis — sometimes called the "psi of the experimenter" or "super-psi" interpretation — is difficult to test directly, since it makes essentially the same predictions as a genuine participant-psi effect. It does make one distinctive prediction: negative results obtained by sceptical experimenters should be explainable by inhibitory psi from those experimenters rather than by the absence of participant psi, making replication failure unfalsifiable in principle.

Critics have noted that this interpretive extension makes parapsychology's core claims unfalsifiable: any null result can be attributed to the experimenter's psi-inhibitory influence. Proponents argue that this is not a defect specific to parapsychology but reflects a genuine structural feature of studying phenomena that may be influenced by consciousness at the measurement stage.

## Experimental Documentation

The most systematic documentation of the experimenter effect in parapsychology comes from several sources:

**Rhine and associates** — Early Duke laboratory records showed that certain staff members consistently obtained significant results while others did not, under otherwise similar conditions. Rhine initially attributed this to differences in rapport with participants.

**The sheep–goat meta-analysis (Lawrence, 1993)** — Beyond the participant sheep–goat effect, Lawrence's meta-analysis found that experimenter belief interacted significantly with experimental outcome, with believing experimenters obtaining more positive results even from identical participant pools.

**Wiseman and Schlitz studies (1997, 1999)** — In a directly designed test of the experimenter effect, Caroline Watt and colleagues arranged for Marilyn Schlitz (a psi believer) and Richard Wiseman (a noted sceptic) to run sessions of the same DMILS protocol with the same participant population. Results obtained by Schlitz were consistently significant; results obtained by Wiseman were consistently null. A third collaborative study also found the same pattern. Since participants were drawn from the same population and the protocol was identical, the experimenter is the primary remaining variable.

## Implications for Replication

The experimenter effect has profound implications for psi replication standards. If the identity of the experimenter is a genuine causal variable in psi research, then replication by a different experimenter using the same protocol is not a straightforward test of the original finding — it is a test of whether the effect generalises to a new experimenter. Failure to replicate by a new experimenter may indicate that the original finding was an artifact of the original experimenter's procedural idiosyncrasies, or that it was a genuine psi effect that does not generalise across experimenter conditions.

Distinguishing these possibilities requires designs that directly manipulate experimenter variables while holding all other factors constant — such as the Wiseman–Schlitz collaborative paradigm — and preregistration of both protocol and experimenter characteristics.

## Assessment

The experimenter effect is one of the most robust and methodologically challenging findings in psi research. It is documented with independent evidence across multiple paradigms and has been demonstrated in directly controlled comparisons. It complicates the use of independent replication as a straightforward validity criterion, and it introduces the possibility — philosophically uncomfortable for both advocates and sceptics — that the experimenter's mental state is not merely a confound to be controlled but a variable potentially causally implicated in experimental outcomes.
