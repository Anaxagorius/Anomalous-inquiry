Extended consciousness models are theoretical frameworks that propose conscious experience, cognitive processing, or mind–environment interaction extends beyond the strict boundaries of skull and skin. Within mainstream cognitive science, "extended mind" and "embodied cognition" theories argue that cognitive processes routinely incorporate environmental props, bodily states, and social structures in ways that dissolve the sharp brain–world boundary assumed in classical computationalism. Within parapsychology, extended consciousness models go further, proposing that mind may sometimes extend across spatial, temporal, or individual boundaries in ways that current physics does not fully explain.

## Extended Mind Theory in Cognitive Science

The extended mind hypothesis, introduced by Andy Clark and David Chalmers in a 1998 paper in *Analysis*, proposed a parity principle: if an external process plays the same functional role as an internal cognitive process, it deserves to be counted as part of the cognitive system, regardless of its location in or outside the biological body. Their canonical example — Otto, who relies on a notebook to store information that Inga stores in her biological memory — argued that the notebook is literally part of Otto's cognitive system, not merely a tool he uses.

Embodied cognition, enactivism (Varela, Thompson, Rosch), and 4E cognition (embodied, embedded, enacted, extended) collectively reject the classical view of the brain as an isolated information-processing engine and emphasise the constitutive role of bodily, social, and environmental factors in shaping cognitive processes.

These mainstream extended mind frameworks do not entail anomalous or parapsychological phenomena. However, they provide a conceptual precedent for the boundary-dissolution that more radical extended consciousness theories require, and they have influenced how some parapsychologists frame their hypotheses.

## Extension to Parapsychological Phenomena

### Psi as Extended Cognition

Some parapsychologists have proposed that anomalous cognition phenomena — remote viewing, telepathy, precognition — can be reframed within an extended mind architecture. On this view, psi is not a mysterious additional faculty but an extreme case of the organism's cognitive reach: the boundary of the cognitive system simply extends further than ordinarily supposed, encompassing information from spatially distant or temporally displaced sources.

This framing has the advantage of connecting psi research to mainstream cognitive science discourse and of avoiding the language of supernatural causation. Its disadvantage is that it does not, by itself, explain *how* such extension could occur within physical systems as currently understood. Critics note that mainstream extended mind theory invokes causal coupling through known physical mechanisms (visual scanning of the notebook, motor action on tools), while psi would require coupling mechanisms that are not known.

### Dean Radin's "Entangled Minds"

Dean Radin, in *Entangled Minds* (2006), proposed that quantum entanglement may provide a physical basis for non-local correlations between minds. He argued that if quantum entanglement can produce correlations between spatially separated physical systems without energy transfer, analogous correlations between biological systems — including human minds — might occur via quantum channels. Radin cited the growing body of psi experimental data as empirical evidence that such correlations exist. The analogy is widely contested: quantum entanglement does not transmit information (the no-communication theorem), and maintaining macroscopic biological entanglement over time presents formidable decoherence problems.

### Rupert Sheldrake's Morphic Fields

Rupert Sheldrake proposed the concept of morphic resonance and morphic fields as a mechanism by which habitual patterns of form and behaviour are transmitted across space and time within species. Applied to mind, the morphic field hypothesis suggests that individual minds participate in extended field structures shared with other members of species, social groups, or families. Sheldrake has claimed empirical support from experiments on telephone telepathy, the "sense of being stared at," and other phenomena. His work is not accepted within mainstream biology or neuroscience and has been extensively criticised on methodological and theoretical grounds.

### Intersubjective Extended Consciousness

Phenomenological traditions — particularly the work of Edmund Husserl, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, and Thomas Fuchs — have argued that individual consciousness is constitutively intersubjective: it is structured by shared social and bodily practices that are not reducible to the processes of any individual brain. This does not entail anomalous psi effects, but it has been invoked by some researchers as a framework within which shared or collective conscious experiences (collective apparitions, shared NDE elements, mass anomalous events) might be understood without entirely supernatural framing.

## Collective Phenomena and Group Mind Hypotheses

Certain anomalous phenomena appear to involve more than one person simultaneously. Collective apparitions — in which multiple witnesses independently report seeing the same figure — and shared or contagious anomalous experiences challenge purely individual models of anomalous perception. Extended consciousness frameworks have been invoked to accommodate such cases, with varying degrees of theoretical elaboration.

William Roll's concept of the "psi field" and subsequent investigators' work on "group psi" propose that the boundaries of a psi effect need not correspond to any one individual. PMIR theory (Stanford) similarly allows for psi to operate across individuals sharing a social environment. These proposals remain at the level of theoretical speculation with limited experimental testing.

## The Global Workspace and Expanded Boundaries

Bernard Baars' global workspace theory of consciousness proposes that consciousness corresponds to the broadcasting of information from a central workspace to widely distributed cognitive processes. Extended consciousness theorists have noted that if the global workspace were extended — through unknown mechanisms — to incorporate information from outside the body, this would provide a framework for anomalous cognition within an otherwise standard cognitive architecture. This idea is more a theoretical possibility than a developed model.

## Relationship to Quantum Biology

Since the 2000s, evidence has accumulated for quantum coherence playing functional roles in biological systems — most prominently in photosynthesis, avian magnetic navigation, and possibly olfaction. These findings have given some credibility to the idea that quantum effects are not purely microscopic and that evolution may have harnessed quantum phenomena for biological function. Extended consciousness theorists have cited this development as indirect support for the possibility that quantum non-local effects might be relevant to consciousness, though the gap between quantum coherence in chlorophyll complexes and non-local mind effects remains enormous.

## Critical Perspectives

The mainstream scientific community largely treats extended consciousness models as either category errors (conflating causal coupling with something more mysterious) or as unfalsifiable. The specific quantum mind hypotheses are criticised for misrepresenting quantum mechanics — entanglement does not support information transfer; macroscopic quantum coherence in the brain has not been demonstrated. Sheldrake's morphic resonance lacks a proposed physical mechanism and has not generated independently testable novel predictions.

Parapsychologists sympathetic to extended consciousness models acknowledge these problems but point to the experimental evidence base as providing empirical motivation for theoretical exploration beyond standard frameworks. If the data are taken seriously, they argue, some extension of the standard cognitive model is required.

## Assessment

Extended consciousness models range from well-established positions in mainstream cognitive science (extended mind, embodied cognition) to highly speculative proposals (quantum entanglement of minds, morphic fields) that are not accepted within relevant scientific disciplines. Their value within parapsychology lies primarily in providing theoretical vocabulary that connects anomalous cognition phenomena to broader debates about the nature and boundaries of mind, rather than in providing mechanistic explanations. The experimental evidence from anomalous cognition research remains the necessary empirical starting point; theoretical frameworks for interpreting that evidence remain provisional and contested.
