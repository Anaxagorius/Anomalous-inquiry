The file drawer effect — also called publication bias — refers to the systematic tendency for statistically significant positive findings to be published while null or negative results remain unpublished in the metaphorical "file drawer." When this occurs across a body of research, the published literature gives a misleadingly positive impression of the true effect, because the visible sample of studies is not representative of all studies conducted.

The term was introduced by Robert Rosenthal in 1979 in the context of a general discussion of publication bias, but the concept is closely associated with parapsychology research because it was explicitly raised as a critique of Rhine-era psi studies and has remained central to the methodological debate in the field.

## The Mechanism of Bias

In any research programme where investigators choose whether to submit results for publication, and where journals preferentially accept significant results, the following dynamic emerges:

1. Many researchers independently conduct studies on a phenomenon
2. Investigators with significant positive results submit for publication; those with null results may not
3. Journals are more likely to accept significant positive results
4. The published literature accumulates a biased sample of studies
5. Meta-analyses of the published literature yield inflated effect sizes and combined significance levels

Even without deliberate concealment or fraud, this process produces systematic distortion. The bias is exacerbated in fields where a theoretical a priori case for the effect is weak (and where null results are therefore less interesting to both investigators and journals) and where effect sizes are small (since small true effects require large samples to reliably detect, and underpowered studies with true effects will frequently produce null results that are discarded).

## The Rosenthal Fail-Safe N

Rosenthal proposed a method for estimating the severity of publication bias using what he termed the "fail-safe N" (Rosenthal, 1979): the number of unpublished null-result studies that would be required to reduce a significant combined p-value in a meta-analysis to non-significance. If the fail-safe N is small — a few dozen studies — then the published effect might plausibly be an artifact of file-drawer bias. If it is very large — several hundred or several thousand studies — the effect is more robust to the bias, because it would require an implausibly large unpublished literature to explain.

Applications of the fail-safe N to major psi meta-analyses have generally returned large values. Bem and Honorton's 1994 autoganzfeld meta-analysis, for instance, would require several hundred null studies to nullify its combined effect — more than would plausibly exist given the size of the Ganzfeld research community at the time. However, critics have noted that the fail-safe N is not a perfect method, since it assumes the unpublished studies are distributed around zero rather than around some intermediate value, and does not account for between-study heterogeneity.

## Funnel Plot Asymmetry

A graphical method for detecting publication bias involves plotting study effect sizes against their standard errors or sample sizes in a "funnel plot." In an unbiased literature, this plot should be approximately symmetric around the true effect — small studies scatter widely due to sampling variation, large studies cluster near the true value, and the shape approximates an inverted funnel.

Asymmetry in funnel plots — where small studies cluster toward the positive end — is a signature of publication bias, since negative small-study results are under-represented. Funnel plot analysis has been applied to several psi meta-analyses with mixed results: some paradigms show asymmetry consistent with publication bias, others do not. The method is not definitive, since asymmetry can also arise from genuine effect heterogeneity (small studies may study higher-ability participants or use stronger protocols) rather than publication bias.

## Trim-and-Fill Analysis

The trim-and-fill method (Duval and Tweedie, 2000) is a more formal approach to publication bias correction. It estimates the number of "missing" studies implied by funnel plot asymmetry, imputes their results, and recalculates the meta-analytic effect on the augmented dataset. Applied to several psi meta-analyses, the trim-and-fill method reduces but typically does not eliminate significant effects, though the corrected effects are smaller than the uncorrected estimates.

## The File Drawer in Parapsychology: Scope Estimates

Several investigators have attempted to estimate the actual scope of unpublished psi research. The Parapsychological Association (PA) has maintained an archives programme. Rhine's laboratory maintained records of exploratory and non-significant studies not submitted for publication. Assessment of these sources suggests that the file drawer exists but that its scope is unlikely to account for the combined effects reported in major meta-analyses, particularly where the fail-safe N calculation suggests several hundred or more null studies would be required.

However, these estimates are inherently uncertain. The population of investigators who have conducted informal psi tests without reporting them cannot be fully enumerated, and experimenter reluctance to publish null results may be particularly high in a field where negative findings carry no particular disciplinary prestige.

## Pre-Registration as a Structural Response

The most direct structural response to the file-drawer problem is pre-registration of studies before data collection begins, combined with registered report mechanisms in journals that commit to publication conditional on study quality rather than results. Under this system, all registered studies are identifiable regardless of outcome, and the file-drawer effect is structurally prevented for the registered component of the literature.

Adoption of these practices in parapsychology has been increasing since approximately 2012. The Parapsychological Association has encouraged pre-registration, and several psi journals have adopted registered report formats.

## Relationship to Other Biases

The file-drawer effect does not operate in isolation. It interacts with:

- **Selective outcome reporting** — even within published studies, only significant outcomes may be highlighted while non-significant secondary measures are omitted
- **Flexible analysis** — analytic choices made after seeing data can inflate significance without suppressing the study entirely
- **Citation bias** — significant studies receive more citations and are better known, shaping the qualitative literature review independently of formal meta-analytic procedures

These compound biases mean that correcting for file-drawer effects alone, even if possible, would not fully characterise the extent of inflation in published effects.

## Assessment

The file-drawer effect is a genuine and acknowledged problem in psi research, as it is in psychology and medicine more broadly. It provides a legitimate alternative explanation for meta-analytic significance in paradigms where the fail-safe N is relatively small or funnel plot asymmetry is evident. For paradigms where fail-safe N estimates require implausibly large unpublished literatures, the argument is weaker but not eliminable. Structural solutions through pre-registration represent the appropriate response, and their continued adoption will progressively improve the interpretability of the emerging research record.
