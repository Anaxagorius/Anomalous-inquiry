The Gimbal and GoFast videos are two of three declassified infrared sensor recordings officially released by the U.S. Department of Defense in April 2020. Together with the Nimitz FLIR1 footage, they represent the core of officially confirmed multi-sensor UAP evidence from U.S. Navy operations. Both videos were recorded in 2015 by F/A-18 Super Hornet crews operating from carrier strike groups in the Atlantic Ocean.

## The Gimbal Video

### Background

The Gimbal video was recorded on January 20, 2015, off the southeastern coast of the United States. The footage was captured using the Advanced Targeting Forward-Looking Infrared (ATFLIR) pod aboard an F/A-18F. The object is tracked against a cloud layer background.

### Characteristics of the Object

The Gimbal footage shows an object moving across the sensor field that, at a certain point, appears to rotate — banking from horizontal to a counter-clockwise orientation in a way that conventional aircraft do not. The cockpit audio includes the comment "Look at that thing, dude! It's rotating!" as this motion occurs.

The object displays no identifiable thermal exhaust plume, no visible propulsion system, and no wings or control surfaces in the infrared imagery. Its thermal signature differs from that of conventional aircraft.

### The Gimbal Derotation Debate

The most technically contested element of the Gimbal footage concerns whether the apparent rotation of the object represents actual rotation of the target or an artefact of the ATFLIR pod's gimbal derotation mechanism.

The ATFLIR pod contains a rotating gimbal that compensates for aircraft roll. When tracking in certain azimuth sectors, the gimbal derotation can produce an apparent rotation of the image background and nearby objects. Analyst Mick West has published detailed analysis arguing the apparent rotation is consistent with this artefact.

Subsequent analysis by aerospace engineer and former AATIP investigator Luis Elizondo, the Scientific Coalition for UAP Studies, and others maintains that the object's rotation and thermal profile are not adequately explained by the derotation artefact. The debate remains unresolved in the open literature.

### Official Status

The DoD confirmed the video's authenticity in September 2019 after a leaked version circulated in the press. The April 2020 official release confirmed it was recorded by Navy personnel and that it "remains unexplained."

## The GoFast Video

### Background

The GoFast video was also recorded in 2015 by Navy F/A-18 crews in the Atlantic. It shows a small object tracking at high speed across the ocean surface.

### Apparent vs. Actual Speed

The GoFast footage generated significant public attention due to the object's apparent high speed — estimated by some commentators at hundreds or thousands of miles per hour based on visual impression. However, rigorous analysis by several teams, including the Scientific Coalition for UAP Studies, significantly moderated this estimate.

The apparent speed of the object results from the parallax effect between the low-altitude, fast-moving object and the distant ocean surface. Calculating actual object speed requires accounting for:
- Aircraft altitude and velocity at time of recording
- Object altitude (estimated from the ATFLIR targeting data)
- Angular velocity of the sensor track

When these factors are properly incorporated, the object's actual velocity is estimated at approximately 40–50 mph — anomalously slow for an aircraft at low altitude over the ocean, rather than exceptionally fast. An object exhibiting stable, level flight at very low altitude over open ocean at approximately 40 mph, with no visible rotor, wing, or control surface, remains anomalous but in a different way than initial impressions suggested.

### Physical Characteristics

The GoFast object in infrared does not display a thermal exhaust plume and presents as a point source against the ocean background. Its signature is consistent with a small, cool object.

## Context: The 2014–2015 Atlantic Encounters

The Gimbal and GoFast videos are part of a broader pattern of UAP encounters reported by East Coast naval aviation units between approximately 2014 and 2015. Commander (now retired) Ryan Graves of the VFA-11 Red Rippers has testified publicly that his squadron encountered anomalous objects on a near-daily basis during training operations in restricted airspace off Virginia Beach during this period.

Graves has described objects that appeared stationary against strong headwinds, manoeuvred in unexpected ways, and persisted in airspace despite no flight plan filings. Several near-misses were reported by pilots within the squadron. Graves testified before the House Oversight Committee in July 2023, calling for a formal, systematic investigation and expressing concern about aviation safety.

## Significance

The Gimbal and GoFast footage — together with the Nimitz FLIR1 video — constitute the current evidential foundation for officially confirmed, sensor-recorded UAP. Their significance lies not in definitive identification of the objects as extraordinary, but in:

1. Their official authentication by the U.S. Department of Defense
2. The failure of standard explanations to fully account for the observations
3. The corroboration of broader patterns of naval UAP encounter testimony

The videos are insufficient, individually, to establish the nature of the objects. They are sufficient to establish that the Navy's own sensor systems recorded objects the DoD has not been able to identify.
