The United States government has conducted formal investigations of unidentified aerial phenomena since the late 1940s. The history of these programmes reflects shifting institutional priorities — from public reassurance and Cold War intelligence assessment in the early decades, to renewed defence and intelligence focus in the contemporary period.

## Early Air Force Programmes (1947–1969)

### Project Sign (1947–1949)

Project Sign was the U.S. Air Force's first formal UAP investigation programme, established in December 1947 in the wake of the Kenneth Arnold sighting and the Roswell incident. Sign was staffed at Wright-Patterson Air Force Base and tasked with assessing whether unidentified flying objects posed a threat to national security.

In 1948, Sign analysts produced an "Estimate of the Situation" — a classified document concluding that a significant fraction of observed UFOs were likely of extraterrestrial origin. The document was rejected by Air Force Chief of Staff General Hoyt Vandenberg, who ordered it destroyed and directed Sign toward conventional explanations. The original document has never been recovered; its existence is attested by Captain Edward Ruppelt and other Sign personnel.

### Project Grudge (1949–1951)

Project Sign was renamed Project Grudge in February 1949. Under Grudge, the programme's analytical posture shifted decisively toward debunking — a mandate to find conventional explanations for all UAP reports rather than to assess them openly. The Grudge final report, published in 1949, concluded that UFOs were the product of misidentification, psychological factors, or hoax, a conclusion that researchers have argued was predetermined.

### Project Blue Book (1952–1969)

Project Blue Book was established in March 1952 as the Air Force's primary ongoing UAP investigation unit. Over 17 years of operation, Blue Book investigated 12,618 reported sightings. Of these, 701 remained classified as "unidentified" at the programme's closure — the Air Force's own determination that no conventional explanation had been identified.

J. Allen Hynek served as Blue Book's scientific consultant throughout its existence. Captain Edward Ruppelt, who directed Blue Book from 1952 to 1953, described an intellectually serious investigation culture during his tenure that he believed was subsequently corrupted by institutional pressure.

The Condon Report, commissioned by the Air Force and produced by the University of Colorado under Edward Condon, concluded in 1969 that further investigation of UAP was unlikely to produce useful scientific data. Blue Book was disbanded in December 1969. Hynek publicly disputed the Condon Report's methodology and conclusions.

The Blue Book archive — approximately 130,000 pages — was declassified and is available at the National Archives and through the National Archives Catalogue.

## Modern Programmes (2007–Present)

### Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP) (2007–2012)

AATIP was a classified programme funded within the U.S. Senate by Senator Harry Reid at the request of aerospace entrepreneur Robert Bigelow. The programme was administered by Luis Elizondo within the Office of the Under Secretary of Defense for Intelligence.

AATIP produced research on UAP encounters, contracted studies on exotic aerospace concepts through Bigelow Aerospace Advanced Space Studies (BAASS), and collected and analysed sensor data from military UAP encounters including the Nimitz cases. The programme's funding of approximately $22 million ran from 2007 to 2012.

Luis Elizondo resigned from the DoD in 2017, citing obstruction of his work, and subsequently joined the To the Stars Academy of Arts and Sciences (TTSA), through which he assisted in the public release of the Nimitz, Gimbal, and GoFast videos.

### UAP Task Force (UAPTF) (2020–2022)

The UAP Task Force was established within the Office of Naval Intelligence in August 2020, following a provision in the FY2020 Intelligence Authorization Act directing the DNI and SECDEF to report on UAP to the congressional intelligence committees.

The UAPTF produced the "Preliminary Assessment: Unidentified Aerial Phenomena" in June 2021 — the first official, publicly released analysis of UAP by a U.S. government body since the Blue Book era. The report assessed 144 UAP reports from U.S. government sources between 2004 and 2021, concluded that 143 could not be identified, and categorised potential explanations including airborne clutter, natural atmospheric phenomena, USG or industry developmental programmes, foreign adversary systems, and "other."

### All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) (2022–Present)

The All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office was established in July 2022 within the Office of the Secretary of Defense, superseding the UAPTF. AARO has a broader mandate than its predecessors, covering not only aerial UAP but objects across all domains — air, sea, space, and the interfaces between them.

AARO is directed by statute to:
- Develop and maintain a UAP reporting framework for DoD and Intelligence Community personnel
- Coordinate with NASA and other agencies
- Report annually to Congress
- Provide a secure mechanism for current and former government personnel to report UAP-related information

AARO has produced annual reports to Congress and established a public-facing website for reporting. As of 2024, AARO's historical review has attracted criticism from some whistleblowers and congressional oversight members who contend that AARO has not adequately pursued evidence of advanced programmes relating to UAP.

### NASA UAP Study (2022–2023)

NASA commissioned an independent study team in June 2022 to assess the UAP phenomenon from a scientific data perspective. The team of 16 independent experts, chaired by astrophysicist David Spergel, published its report in September 2023.

The NASA report concluded that the current data on UAP is insufficient to reach firm scientific conclusions, and emphasised the need for:
- Standardised, calibrated observational data
- Reduction of the observational stigma that suppresses reporting
- Integration of NASA's remote sensing capabilities into UAP data collection

The report did not assess any cases as definitively anomalous but argued that the scientific community should treat the subject seriously rather than dismissing it without investigation.

## International Programmes

**UK Ministry of Defence UFO Desk (1950–2009)**: The UK MoD maintained a small UAP investigation unit that received and assessed public UFO reports. Its files were released in tranches to the National Archives between 2008 and 2013, covering approximately 4,500 pages of documentation.

**GEIPAN (France)**: The Groupe d'Études et d'Informations sur les Phénomènes Aérospatiaux Non-identifiés, operating under the French space agency CNES since 1977, is one of the few national UAP investigation bodies that has operated continuously with public transparency. GEIPAN publishes its case database online and employs a four-tier classification system. Approximately 3% of its cases are classified as "Type D" — unexplained after thorough investigation.

**COMETA Report (1999)**: A privately produced French study by a group of retired senior military officers, scientists, and defence officials concluded that a significant residual of UAP cases was "unexplained" and recommended that the extraterrestrial hypothesis be taken seriously as a working hypothesis. The report has no official government status but is notable for the seniority of its authors.

**Brazilian Air Force**: Brazil has been among the most transparent national air forces on UAP matters, conducting open investigations (including Operation Saucer in 1977) and periodically releasing investigation files.

**Chilean CEFAA**: Chile's Committee for Studies of Anomalous Aerial Phenomena operates under the Civil Aeronautics Directorate and has produced several carefully documented analyses of pilot UAP encounters, including the 2014 Chilean Navy helicopter FLIR case.

## Congressional Engagement

Since 2019, U.S. congressional interest in UAP has increased substantially:
- The FY2020 Intelligence Authorization Act mandated the UAPTF report
- The FY2022 NDAA created AARO and mandated historical records review
- The FY2023 NDAA included provisions for whistleblower protection related to UAP
- Public hearings before the House Armed Services Committee (2022) and House Oversight Committee (2023) featured testimony from military pilots and whistleblowers

The 2023 Oversight hearing included testimony from former intelligence officer David Grusch, who alleged that the U.S. government possesses non-human technology and biological material of non-human origin, claims that AARO has not substantiated in its public reporting.
