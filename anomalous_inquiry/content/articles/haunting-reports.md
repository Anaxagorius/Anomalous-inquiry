Haunting reports describe recurring anomalous experiences associated with a specific location over an extended period, typically reported by multiple independent witnesses and persisting across changes of occupancy. They are distinguished from poltergeist phenomena (which centre on a person rather than a place) and from one-time apparitional encounters, though these categories overlap and are not always clearly separable in the case literature. The experiences reported in haunting cases include visual apparitions, auditory phenomena (voices, footsteps, music), olfactory impressions, cold spots, sensations of presence, and occasionally anomalous physical effects.

## Defining Characteristics

Several features distinguish haunting reports from other anomalous experiences:

- **Location specificity**: Phenomena recur in the same place, are reported by successive occupants unfamiliar with the site's history, and do not typically follow residents who move away.
- **Multi-witness character**: In many well-documented cases, independent witnesses who have not communicated with each other report convergently similar experiences — a figure in a particular location, a sound in a specific room.
- **Historical connection**: Apparitional figures, when identified, are often linked to former inhabitants or traumatic events associated with the location, though cases exist where no such connection can be established.
- **Persistence over time**: Haunting phenomena may recur over decades or centuries, as long as the location remains standing.

## Historical Background

Reports of haunted locations appear in ancient Roman sources (Pliny the Younger's account of a haunted house in Athens is frequently cited). Systematic collection of haunting cases began with the SPR in the 1880s. Eleanor Sidgwick's extensive survey of spontaneous cases, and her critical analysis in "Notes on the Evidence, Collectively and Individually, for Phantasms of the Dead" (1885), applied evidential standards notably more rigorous than previous accounts. The SPR's *Census of Hallucinations* (1894), based on responses from 17,000 individuals, provided a large database on spontaneous apparitional and haunting experiences.

In the twentieth century, G.N.M. Tyrrell's *Apparitions* (1943) remains a systematic theoretical treatment. More recently, investigations by members of the Society for Psychical Research, the Koestler Parapsychology Unit at the University of Edinburgh, and independent investigators have contributed both case documentation and experimental approaches to haunted locations.

## Typology of Hauntings

### Crisis and Grief Apparitions

Many spontaneous apparitional cases involve a person seeing or hearing a recently deceased individual, often at the time of death or shortly after. These are distinguished from haunting phenomena by their one-time or brief character and their connection to a personal relationship rather than a location. They are discussed in the apparitional experiences literature and only peripherally in haunting research.

### Residual or "Stone Tape" Hauntings

The "stone tape" hypothesis (popularised by T.C. Lethbridge and subsequently by Nigel Kneale's 1972 television drama of the same name) proposes that strong emotional experiences imprint information on the physical fabric of a location — stone, water, soil — which can later be replayed under appropriate conditions, generating a sensory impression in sensitive individuals. On this model, the apparitional figure is not a conscious entity but a recorded trace, analogous to a playback of stored information.

The hypothesis is speculative and lacks a known physical mechanism, but it has influenced investigation methodology: proponents seek correlations between apparitional phenomena and geological, electromagnetic, or geochemical features of locations.

### Intelligent or Interactive Hauntings

In contrast to residual hauntings, some reports describe figures or presences that appear to respond to witnesses, exhibit apparent intentionality, and convey novel information — including, in some cases, information later verified as accurate and unknown to the percipient at the time. Such cases shade into survival-of-consciousness evidence and are among the most evidentially significant in the case literature, as they potentially exclude simple memory or expectation as explanations.

## Field Investigation Methods

Modern haunting investigations employ a range of instruments and methodologies:

- **Video and audio recording**: Continuous surveillance aimed at capturing anomalous phenomena; voice recorder anomalies ("electronic voice phenomena," EVP) are recorded and analysed, though their interpretation is contested.
- **Electromagnetic field (EMF) measurement**: Based on the hypothesis that electromagnetic anomalies may correlate with reported phenomena; EMF meters are widely used but the causal relevance of EMF anomalies to subjective experiences is not established.
- **Infrasound measurement**: Low-frequency sound below the threshold of hearing (below ~20 Hz) has been proposed to induce disorientation, unease, or visual anomalies; Vic Tandy's work at Coventry linked an infrasound source to reported anomalous experiences in a laboratory, providing a possible normal-explanation mechanism for some cases.
- **Temperature monitoring**: Reports of cold spots are common; systematic temperature mapping sometimes identifies thermal anomalies but their connection to anomalous experiences is not demonstrated.
- **Psychological assessment of witnesses**: Factors including temporal lobe sensitivity, fantasy proneness, and prior expectations have been examined as moderating variables.

Christopher French and colleagues at Goldsmiths University developed an environmental experimental paradigm in which subjects were exposed to allegedly haunted and non-haunted areas of buildings (including Hampton Court and Edinburgh vaults), assessing whether anomalous experiences correlated with specific locations or with general suggestion.

## The Edinburgh Vaults Research

A systematic investigation of reported hauntings in the South Bridge Vaults in Edinburgh was conducted by Richard Wiseman, Emma Greening, and Matthew Smith (2003). They mapped self-reported anomalous experiences across vault chambers. Experiences were found to cluster in specific chambers — consistent with a spatial haunting phenomenon — but correlated with a combination of draft, lighting, and magnetic field variation that could account for the subjective reports through purely physical channels. The authors concluded that environmental factors could explain the location-specific clustering of experiences without invoking anomalous causation.

Critics of this design noted that environmentally sensitive locations might also be genuinely haunted, and that the presence of a physical correlate does not disprove a psi component.

## Explanatory Frameworks

### Normal Explanation Hypotheses

A range of well-established mechanisms can generate haunting-type experiences without anomalous causation:

- **Infrasound**: As noted, infrasound can induce unease and perceptual anomalies.
- **Carbon monoxide**: Toxic levels of CO produce headaches, disorientation, and hallucinations; "haunted house" investigations have occasionally identified CO leaks as the cause of reported experiences.
- **Geomagnetic fields**: Michael Persinger proposed that temporal lobe stimulation by fluctuating geomagnetic fields can produce sensed presences, out-of-body sensations, and complex hallucinations. His "God helmet" laboratory simulations of this effect have been disputed on methodological grounds.
- **Expectancy and suggestion**: Prior knowledge that a location is "haunted" substantially increases the likelihood of anomalous experiences in susceptible individuals.
- **Unusual optical and acoustic properties**: Architectural features of old buildings can produce visual illusions and anomalous acoustic reflections.

### Anomalous Cognition Hypothesis

If hauntings sometimes involve genuine anomalous information about the history of a location — accessed by percipients with no prior knowledge through psi — they would represent a form of place-mediated anomalous cognition rather than a survival phenomenon. This model does not require any continuing conscious entity; it requires only that the location serves as a source of information accessible through paranormal means. G.W. Lambert proposed "geophysical" correlates of haunting phenomena (underground water, subsidence, seismic activity) as physical causal factors; Lethbridge proposed information storage in geological materials.

### Survival Hypothesis

The most traditional interpretation of hauntings holds that at least some cases involve the continuing presence of the consciousness or some aspect of the personality of a deceased individual, interacting with the physical environment and with sensitive percipients. The evidential basis for this interpretation — as opposed to the residual recording or psi-trace hypothesis — rests on interactive, apparently intentional apparitional behaviour and on the small subset of cases involving veridical unknown information communicated through the apparitional encounter.

## Assessment

Haunting phenomena present a complex evidential picture. Many reported haunting experiences are explicable through well-known psychological and environmental mechanisms, and the investigative record includes numerous cases resolved by normal explanation. A residue of cases — particularly those involving multi-witness independent convergence, verified veridical information, and effects not attributable to identified physical causes — is more difficult to dismiss entirely. The theoretical framework best supported by the best cases remains unclear: whether they represent residual information recorded in the physical environment, genuine psi-mediated information access, or — in the most demanding interpretation — the continuing agency of deceased individuals are questions the current evidence cannot resolve definitively.
