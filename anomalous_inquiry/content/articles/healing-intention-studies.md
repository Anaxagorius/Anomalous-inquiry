Healing intention studies examine whether a person's focused mental intent to promote recovery or wellbeing in another individual produces measurable effects beyond what can be attributed to suggestion, expectation, or known physical contact. The research literature spans controlled laboratory experiments using biological assay systems, randomised clinical trials of distant healing modalities, and meta-analyses attempting to integrate findings across methodologically diverse studies. This archive treats the subject in a non-clinical framing, focusing on the anomalous-phenomena literature rather than the evidence base for specific therapeutic practices.

## Conceptual Distinctions

Several overlapping terms appear in the literature and are not always used consistently:

- **Distant healing intention (DHI)** — a general term for any attempt to influence another's health from a distance through mental processes, irrespective of theoretical framework
- **Intercessory prayer** — intent expressed through prayer, typically within religious frameworks; the most extensively studied DHI modality in randomised trial designs
- **Therapeutic touch and related modalities** — hands-on or near-body practices in which practitioners report an intent to transmit healing through a postulated energy field; typically studied within integrative medicine
- **DMILS (distant mental interaction with living systems)** — laboratory paradigms measuring physiological outcomes in an isolated biological system when a remote individual focuses healing or calming intent; see separate entry

The distinction between healing intention research (studied as anomalous phenomena) and evidence-based integrative medicine is methodologically important. The anomalous-phenomena literature is specifically concerned with whether intent produces effects *at a distance* absent known physical channels.

## Laboratory Models

To avoid the confounds associated with human clinical studies (expectation, social interaction, placebo mechanisms), a line of research has examined healing intention effects on non-human biological systems:

- **Cell cultures** — William Braud and colleagues (Southwest Research Institute) examined effects of focused intent on the lysis rate of human red blood cells in hypotonic solution; a series of studies in the 1980s and early 1990s reported small but statistically significant reductions in lysis rate when a remote participant focused protective intent on the cell preparation
- **Bacteria and yeast** — studies by Grad and others examined growth rates of bacterial and fungal cultures under conditions of healing intent directed by selected participants; results have been inconsistent across laboratories
- **Wound healing in animals** — Bernard Grad (McGill University) conducted controlled experiments in the 1960s examining wound healing rates in mice held by Oskar Estebany, a practitioner claiming healing ability, compared to untreated or sham-treated controls; results were reported as significantly accelerated in the healing condition, though methodological limitations were acknowledged

## Randomised Clinical Trials: Intercessory Prayer

The most controversial and extensively studied human-subjects paradigm is the randomised trial of intercessory prayer for hospitalised patients. Key studies include:

**Byrd (1988)** — A study of 393 patients admitted to the coronary care unit at San Francisco General Hospital, randomised to intercessory prayer by Christian prayer groups or control. Reported significant differences on several medical outcome measures (fewer complications, less antibiotic use, fewer intubations). The study was influential but criticised for methodological limitations in blinding and outcome selection.

**Harris et al. (1999)** — A double-blind randomised trial of 990 CCU patients in which neither patients, staff, nor investigators knew which patients were assigned to the prayer condition. Reported a modest but statistically significant improvement on a composite outcome score in the prayer group.

**STEP (Study of the Therapeutic Effects of Intercessory Prayer, 2006)** — The largest such study to date, involving 1,802 patients undergoing coronary artery bypass graft surgery. Found no significant difference in complications for patients who received prayer compared to those who did not. Notably, patients who were told they would definitely receive prayer had *higher* complication rates than those who were uncertain — an outcome attributed by the investigators to possible performance anxiety.

The STEP result introduced a significant methodological complication: awareness of receiving prayer may itself constitute an intervention with measurable psychosomatic effects, making clean separation of the anomalous element from the expectation element difficult.

## Meta-Analytic Status

A 2007 Cochrane review by Roberts, Ahmed, and Hall examined 10 randomised trials of intercessory prayer (totalling over 7,600 patients) and found no clear evidence of benefit or harm. The review noted substantial heterogeneity across studies in populations, outcome measures, and prayer protocols, limiting the interpretive value of pooled analyses.

A broader 2003 review by Astin, Harkness, and Ernst of 23 randomised controlled trials of distant healing — including prayer, mental healing, and therapeutic touch — found that 57% reported statistically significant effects. The authors acknowledged methodological concerns across the literature but concluded that the evidence merited continued investigation.

## Methodological Challenges

The field faces several interlocking methodological problems:

- **Intention definition and fidelity** — there is no consensus method for verifying that an experimental healer maintained the intended mental state during sessions
- **Distance as a variable** — most studies treat distance as binary (present or absent) rather than examining it systematically; some healing traditions assert that distance is irrelevant to effect
- **Selection of practitioners** — studies vary widely in whether they use self-identified healers, trained practitioners, or prayer volunteers, making the participant variable difficult to characterise
- **Biological endpoints** — the choice of outcome measure (self-report, clinical event, physiological assay) substantially affects results and interpretability

## Assessment

The healing intention literature presents a mixed evidential picture. Several laboratory studies using biological assay systems have reported significant effects under controlled conditions, but replication is inconsistent. Large randomised clinical trials have produced contradictory results, with the most rigorous showing no effect on primary outcomes. The subject remains unresolved and warrants continued investigation under tightly controlled conditions.
