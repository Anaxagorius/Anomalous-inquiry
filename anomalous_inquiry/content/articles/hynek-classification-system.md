The Hynek Classification System is the foundational taxonomy used to categorise Unidentified Aerial Phenomena (UAP) reports by proximity and type of interaction. Developed by astronomer and U.S. Air Force scientific consultant J. Allen Hynek, it was formally introduced in his 1972 book *The UFO Experience: A Scientific Inquiry* and remains the most widely cited classification framework in the field.

## Background

J. Allen Hynek (1910–1986) served as the scientific consultant for Project Blue Book from 1952 to 1969. Initially sceptical, Hynek became increasingly persuaded that a subset of cases resisted conventional explanation and that the subject warranted serious scientific investigation. His classification system emerged from direct engagement with thousands of case files and was designed to provide systematic distinctions useful for evidential and analytical purposes.

## Core Categories

Hynek divided reports into two primary groups: **Nocturnal Lights**, **Daylight Discs**, and **Radar Cases** for more distant encounters, and **Close Encounters** for sightings within approximately 500 feet of the object.

### Distant Encounter Categories

**Nocturnal Lights (NL)**: The most common report type. Anomalous lights observed in the night sky, exhibiting behaviour inconsistent with known aircraft, satellites, or meteors — including sudden direction changes, hovering, and unusual colour shifts.

**Daylight Discs (DD)**: Objects observed in sufficient daylight to permit shape characterisation. The "disc" descriptor is historical and does not constrain the actual morphology — objects in this category include spheres, cylinders, and other forms.

**Radar Cases (RV)**: Objects tracked by radar, subdivided into:
- Radar-only detections (no visual confirmation)
- Radar-visual correlation (simultaneous confirmation by both radar and direct observation — the highest evidential value)

### Close Encounters

**CE-1 (Close Encounter of the First Kind)**: A sighting of an unidentified object within approximately 150 metres (~500 feet), with sufficient proximity to observe physical detail, but without reported physical interaction between the object and the environment.

**CE-2 (Close Encounter of the Second Kind)**: A close encounter accompanied by physical effects on the environment, including:
- Electromagnetic interference (vehicle failure, compass anomalies)
- Physiological effects on witnesses (skin burns, temporary blindness, nausea)
- Ground trace evidence (scorched vegetation, depressions, radiation anomalies)
- Effects on animals (behavioural disturbance, injury)

**CE-3 (Close Encounter of the Third Kind)**: A close encounter at which animated entities or occupants are reported in or around the object. The famous 1977 Spielberg film title derives from this classification.

## Post-Hynek Expansions

Subsequent researchers extended the system to account for encounter types not fully captured by Hynek's original scheme:

**CE-4 (Close Encounter of the Fourth Kind)**: Cases in which the witness reports being taken aboard an object and subjected to examination or interaction — the abduction narrative. This category was not used by Hynek himself and remains contested on methodological grounds, as the evidence base is primarily based on hypnotic regression and memory reports of uncertain reliability.

**CE-5 (Close Encounter of the Fifth Kind)**: Human-initiated contact, including bilateral communication with non-human intelligence. This category, associated with researcher Steven Greer, is considered highly speculative by mainstream researchers and is included here for taxonomic completeness rather than as an endorsed analytical category.

**CE-6 and CE-7**: Additional extensions proposed by various researchers to cover death or serious injury of witnesses, or reproduction-related claims, respectively. These are not in widespread analytical use.

## Applications and Limitations

The Hynek system provides a useful first-order sorting tool for case analysis and database construction. Its primary value is in distinguishing the evidential strength of different encounter types: radar-visual CE-2 cases with multiple trained witnesses and physical trace evidence represent the upper tier of documentation quality, while single-witness nocturnal lights represent the lower tier.

The system does not imply any hypothesis about the origin or nature of the phenomena it classifies. CE-1 through CE-3 classification is assigned on the basis of reported characteristics, not on determination of identity.

Limitations of the framework include:
- It was designed for aerial phenomena and maps imperfectly onto underwater or near-space cases
- The 500-foot proximity threshold is not always quantifiable from witness testimony
- The entity criterion for CE-3 does not distinguish between animated mechanical objects, animals, and reported humanoid figures

Modern analytical frameworks such as those developed by the Scientific Coalition for UAP Studies (SCU) and AARO supplement the Hynek system with more granular sensor and physical-evidence taxonomies.
