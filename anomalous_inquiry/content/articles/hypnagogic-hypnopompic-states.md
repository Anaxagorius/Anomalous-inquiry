Hypnagogic and hypnopompic states are transitional phases of consciousness occurring at the boundaries between waking and sleep. The hypnagogic state occurs during the descent into sleep; the hypnopompic state occurs during emergence from sleep into waking. Both are characterised by a distinctive range of perceptual, sensory, and cognitive phenomena that distinguish them qualitatively from both ordinary wakefulness and full dreaming sleep. These states have attracted sustained interest from parapsychology due to their historical association with spontaneous anomalous experiences and their structural parallels with deliberately induced altered states.

## Phenomenological Features

### Hypnagogic State

The hypnagogic state begins at the onset of drowsiness and proceeds through progressive dissolution of the waking attentional framework. Characteristic phenomena include:

- **Hypnagogic imagery** — vivid visual imagery appearing with closed eyes, ranging from simple phosphene-like patterns and geometric forms to complex figurative scenes and faces; often described as appearing "before" the inner eye with a distinctly external quality
- **Hypnagogic hallucinations** — auditory, tactile, kinetic, and sometimes olfactory hallucinations; auditory examples include hearing one's name called, brief music, or spoken phrases
- **Sleep starts (hypnic jerks)** — sudden myoclonic contractions frequently accompanied by vivid falling imagery
- **Proprioceptive alterations** — sensations of floating, sinking, rotation, or expansion of the body
- **Thought dissolution** — progressive loss of coherent logical chains, with intrusion of loosely associative or involuntary imagery and speech fragments
- **Ego boundary attenuation** — reduction in the ordinary felt separation between self and environment

### Hypnopompic State

The hypnopompic state mirrors hypnagogic phenomenology but occurs during the reverse transition. It is commonly associated with:

- **Hypnopompic hallucinations** — persistent imagery from the dream state bleeding into awakening awareness; includes visual apparitions (frequently figures at the bedside, shadow presences, or animals in the room), heard voices, and tactile sensations
- **Sleep paralysis** — temporary inability to move or speak during the hypnopompic transition, resulting from persistence of REM atonia into wakefulness; frequently accompanied by a strong sense of presence, pressure on the chest, and intense anxiety; cross-culturally interpreted in terms of attacking entities, demons, or intruders

## Neurological and Physiological Basis

Both states correspond to EEG patterns intermediate between waking alpha rhythms and the theta activity characteristic of Stage 1 NREM sleep. The hypnagogic descent is associated with progressive alpha attenuation and theta emergence; the hypnopompic phase involves the reverse. In both states, reduced activation of prefrontal circuits produces diminished critical monitoring and increased susceptibility to internally generated imagery.

Sleep paralysis involves persistence of brainstem-mediated REM atonia into wakefulness — a dissociation between cortical arousal and motor inhibition maintained by glycinergic pathways in the spinal cord. The accompanying hallucinations are now understood to represent intrusion of REM-like imagery generation into a partially wakeful brain state, explaining the vivid and often threatening character of the perceived presences.

## Parapsychological Relevance

The hypnagogic and hypnopompic states are among the most frequently cited contexts for spontaneous anomalous experiences in both case collections and epidemiological surveys. Their relevance includes:

**Crisis apparitions:** A significant proportion of reported apparitional cases — in which a percipient reportedly sees or hears a person who is concurrently dying or in acute crisis at a distance — occur during hypnagogic or hypnopompic transitions. The SPR's 1886 *Phantasms of the Living* collection and subsequent census studies noted that transitional sleep states were disproportionately represented among crisis apparition reports.

**OBE induction context:** The hypnagogic-to-sleep transition is the most commonly reported natural precursor to spontaneous OBEs. Several deliberate OBE induction methods (including the Wake-Back-To-Bed technique and Monroe Institute's Hemi-Sync approaches) exploit this transition explicitly.

**Receptive states model:** The hypnagogic state's attenuation of focused attention and reduction of internal cognitive noise has led some parapsychologists to classify it as a structurally psi-conducive state, analogous to the ganzfeld condition. Honorton's noise-reduction model of psi explicitly includes hypnagogic and related states as optimal reception conditions.

**Historical context:** Many historical reports of visionary and mediumistic experiences include phenomenological features consistent with hypnagogic or hypnopompic states, including immobility (atonia), vivid visual and auditory phenomena of external quality, and a strong felt presence. This overlap has been used both by sceptics (as a naturalistic explanation of claimed anomalous encounters) and by researchers investigating the possible convergence of neurological mechanism and genuine anomalous content.

## Sleep Paralysis and Entity Encounters

Sleep paralysis with hypnopompic hallucinations constitutes one of the most cross-culturally consistent anomalous experiences documented in the anthropological and psychological literature. Cultural interpretations include:

- **Old Hag** (Newfoundland) — a crushing supernatural figure
- **Kanashibari** (Japan) — binding by supernatural forces
- **Djinn attacks** (Islamic traditions)
- **Succubus/incubus** encounters (Western medieval tradition)
- **Grey alien abduction experiences** — researchers including David Hufford and Susan Clancy have proposed that sleep paralysis with hypnopompic hallucination may underlie a significant proportion of reported alien contact experiences, particularly the characteristic paralysis, sense of presence, and non-human entities

The phenomenological consistency across cultures — including the characteristic paralysis, oppressive presence, chest pressure, and terror — represents one of the more compelling cases for a shared neurological substrate driving diverse cultural interpretations.

## Assessment

Hypnagogic and hypnopompic states are well-characterised neurologically and represent a major intersection between mainstream sleep science and parapsychological case study collections. The neurological mechanisms producing their phenomena are established. The extent to which their anomalous associations (including reported veridical content in crisis apparitions and OBEs triggered from these states) reflect genuine anomalous information acquisition rather than neurologically explicable confabulation remains an open empirical question.
