In March 1967, a series of incidents at Malmstrom Air Force Base in Montana — a Strategic Air Command installation housing Minuteman ICBM launch facilities — resulted in the simultaneous offline shutdown of multiple nuclear missile systems. Several USAF personnel reported observing unidentified aerial objects over and near the launch facilities at the time of the shutdowns. The Malmstrom incidents represent one of the most detailed and officially documented cases of alleged UAP activity near nuclear weapons infrastructure.

## Background: Malmstrom AFB and the Minuteman Programme

Malmstrom AFB, located near Great Falls, Montana, was a primary Strategic Air Command (SAC) installation housing two wings of Minuteman I intercontinental ballistic missiles. The Minuteman system was dispersed across dozens of underground launch facilities (LFs) connected to underground Launch Control Centers (LCCs) staffed around the clock by two-person Strategic Missile Crews.

Each flight typically consisted of ten LFs connected to one LCC. The missiles were maintained in continuous alert status and were subject to strict technical control protocols. The electronics of the Minuteman guidance and control systems were housed in hardened underground silos with substantial electromagnetic shielding.

## The Echo Flight Incident: March 16, 1967

### The Shutdown

Between approximately 6:00 and 8:30 AM on March 16, 1967, all ten Minuteman missiles at Echo Flight, Malmstrom AFB, went to "No-Go" status — meaning they were no longer in launch-ready condition. The sequence in which the missiles went offline was rapid: multiple missiles dropped status within seconds of each other, sequentially.

The Event Sequence Reports filed at the time confirmed the No-Go status. The cause was subsequently attributed by Air Force investigation to a "spurious signal" transmitted via a logic coupler in the LF guidance and control system. The technical investigation did not establish the source of the spurious signal.

### The UAP Report: Captain Robert Salas

The missile crew on duty at the Echo Flight LCC at the time of the shutdown was Captain Robert Salas and Captain Fred Mywald (some accounts identify Mywald as the Crew Commander). According to Salas's subsequently published testimony:

Shortly before the missiles began going offline, Salas received a phone call from a topside security guard reporting that the guard and other personnel had observed a glowing red or orange object — described as oval or disc-shaped, roughly 30 feet in diameter — hovering silently above the main gate of the LCC facility. A second call, minutes later, reported that the object was now directly overhead.

Salas states that he woke Captain Mywald to report the security alerts, and that within minutes of doing so, the missiles began dropping to No-Go status in rapid succession.

Salas has described the coincidence of the UAP report and the missile shutdowns as clearly connected in his assessment, though he has acknowledged that he could not observe the objects himself from the underground LCC.

### The Security Personnel Reports

Several security police personnel assigned to Echo Flight have corroborated the presence of unidentified aerial objects above the facility on the morning of March 16. These include reported observations by:

- The flight line security team at the main gate
- Personnel at individual LF sites in the vicinity who also reported unusual lights

Complete security logs from that morning have not been publicly released. Salas and subsequent researchers have noted that some records appear to have been classified or removed from the accessible archive.

## The Oscar Flight Incident: March 1967

Within weeks of the Echo Flight incident, a similar — though less precisely documented — incident was reported at Oscar Flight, also at Malmstrom. Multiple LF systems reportedly went offline simultaneously, again coinciding with reports from security personnel of unidentified lights or objects in the vicinity.

Researcher Robert Hastings, who has conducted the most extensive investigation of UAP incidents at nuclear weapons facilities, has documented testimony from several Oscar Flight security personnel who reported unusual aerial phenomena during this period.

## Official Investigation and Records

### Air Force Investigation

The Air Force conducted a technical investigation into the Echo Flight shutdown. The investigation determined the cause as a "spurious signal" in the logic coupler but did not conclusively identify the origin of the signal. The investigation did not formally address the UAP reports by security personnel.

### Declassified Documents

A number of documents relating to the Malmstrom incidents have been declassified or obtained through FOIA requests:

- SAC Event Sequence Reports confirming the No-Go status of Echo Flight missiles on March 16, 1967
- Unit histories for the 341st Strategic Missile Wing referencing unusual equipment anomalies in March 1967
- Internal communication suggesting that the simultaneous shutdown of ten missiles in a single flight was "not consistent with normal system behaviour"

Hastings's research has produced a substantial documented record of these materials, presented in his 2008 study.

### Pentagon Response

In 2010, the U.S. Air Force held a press conference in response to public claims by Salas and other former missile officers that UAP had interfered with nuclear missiles. The Air Force spokesperson stated that the agency had found no evidence that UAP had interfered with U.S. strategic nuclear weapons. Salas and Hastings disputed this characterisation, noting that the Air Force's statement did not address the specific documented incidents they had cited.

## Robert Hastings' Research

Robert Hastings is a civilian investigative journalist who has spent four decades documenting alleged UAP incidents at nuclear weapons facilities worldwide. His 2008 book *UFOs and Nukes: Extraordinary Encounters at Nuclear Weapons Sites* is the primary academic and investigative reference for the Malmstrom incidents and related cases.

Hastings has interviewed dozens of former USAF personnel — including launch officers, security police, and maintenance crews — who reported UAP activity at or near nuclear missile and bomber installations. His case catalogue spans multiple Cold War-era incidents at Malmstrom, Minot AFB, F.E. Warren AFB, and UK and Soviet nuclear sites.

In September 2010, Hastings and Salas organised a press conference at the National Press Club in Washington, D.C., at which seven former USAF officers presented their accounts publicly. The officers testified under oath to UAP encounters at nuclear weapons facilities. The event received significant media coverage.

## Other Malmstrom Personnel Testimony

Beyond Salas, several other individuals who served at Malmstrom in 1967 have given testimony relating to UAP activity near nuclear facilities:

**Colonel (Ret.) Walter Figel** — Figel was the Deputy Crew Commander at Echo Flight on March 16, 1967. In interviews with Hastings (and later with other researchers), Figel confirmed that a security guard had reported a UFO over the LF at the time the missiles went offline. Figel's account has evolved across different interviews; he has described uncertainty about some details while confirming the core sequence of events.

**Former security personnel** — Multiple individuals who served as security police at Malmstrom during 1966–1967 have described observations of unusual lights and objects near LFs during this period, some of which they were instructed not to document or discuss.

## Sceptical Assessments

**Proximity vs. causation**: Sceptics note that the fact of UAP reports coinciding with a missile shutdown does not establish causation. The 341st Missile Wing experienced numerous technical anomalies during 1966–1967 related to the reliability of the early Minuteman systems.

**Chain of custody of testimony**: Salas did not publish his account until decades after the event. Some researchers have noted apparent variations in how Salas has described details of the incident across different accounts.

**Classified records**: The incomplete record — with key security logs unavailable — makes independent verification of the full sequence of events impossible from open sources.

These caveats do not negate the documented technical shutdown or the Air Force's own acknowledgment of the No-Go event. They apply specifically to the claimed connection between the shutdown and UAP activity.

## Broader Pattern: UAP and Nuclear Installations

The Malmstrom incidents are the most extensively documented instances of a broader reported pattern. Hastings's research catalogue includes:

- UAP sightings near nuclear bomber bases (Minot AFB, Barksdale AFB)
- Reports of UAP over nuclear submarine bases
- Soviet-era accounts of UAP interference with strategic missile systems, including reports investigated by the Soviet military and described in declassified Russian sources

The pattern — if it is a pattern — has been the subject of serious discussion in both military and academic UAP research contexts. The question of whether any intelligence or technology of unknown origin has shown interest in nuclear weapons systems remains unresolved.

## Classification

The Malmstrom AFB incidents are classified CE-2 under the Hynek scale: reported close-range observation of unidentified aerial phenomena (CE-1) combined with associated physical effects on mechanical or electronic systems (the missile shutdowns). The CE-2 classification reflects the coincidence of UAP reports with system malfunctions; it does not assert that the UAP caused the malfunctions.

## Disclaimer

This article documents the Malmstrom AFB missile incidents and associated UAP reports as a historical case record. Witness testimony is presented as reported. Technical documentation is presented as recorded in declassified official records. The CE-2 classification and analysis of the connection between UAP activity and the missile shutdowns represent the best available interpretation of the record, not a concluded finding. Classification reflects reported proximity and effects, not explanation, origin, or intent.
