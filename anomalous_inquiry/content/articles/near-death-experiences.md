A near-death experience (NDE) is a reported cluster of conscious experiences occurring in individuals who come close to biological death — typically through cardiac arrest, severe trauma, or clinical procedures — and subsequently survive. NDEs represent one of the most extensively studied phenomena within the scientific literature on consciousness and survival, with prospective studies published in peer-reviewed medical journals and a dedicated professional organisation, the International Association for Near-Death Studies (IANDS), founded in 1981.

## Phenomenological Features

The term "near-death experience" was introduced by physician Raymond Moody in his 1975 book *Life After Life*, which compiled accounts from over 150 individuals resuscitated from clinical death or near-death. Moody identified a recurrent phenomenological pattern that has since been confirmed by many independent researchers across cultural contexts:

- Sense of leaving the body and perceiving it from an external vantage point (out-of-body experience; OBE)
- Movement through a dark tunnel toward a region of intense light
- Encounter with deceased relatives or beings of light
- Life review — rapid panoramic recall of life events with associated emotional and perspective-taking dimensions
- Encounter with a boundary or limit beyond which the experiencer does not pass
- Return to the body, often described as reluctant
- Profound and lasting changes in values, attitudes toward death, and psychophysiology

## Prospective Clinical Studies

The methodological gold standard in NDE research consists of prospective studies in which patients admitted to cardiac resuscitation units are enrolled before any NDE occurs, with data collected systematically at follow-up. Key prospective studies include:

**Pim van Lommel et al. (2001)** — *The Lancet*. A Dutch study of 344 cardiac arrest survivors found that 18% reported an NDE. The study examined variables including depth of unconsciousness, duration of cardiac arrest, and prior awareness of NDEs as predictors, and found none reliably correlated with who reported an experience. The publication in a high-impact medical journal was a landmark in the field.

**Sam Parnia et al. (2001)** — *Resuscitation*. A UK prospective study of 63 cardiac arrest survivors, with 4 meeting criteria for full NDE. Parnia's subsequent AWARE (AWAreness during REsuscitation) study incorporated visual targets placed above resuscitation equipment to test OBE veridical claims.

**Bruce Greyson (2003)** — Greyson, then at the University of Virginia, produced the Greyson NDE Scale, the most widely used standardised instrument for measuring NDE depth and component features, enabling cross-study comparisons.

## Veridical Out-of-Body Perceptions

A subset of NDE reports include apparently accurate descriptions of events occurring in the environment while the patient was unconscious and in cardiac arrest — the period during which cerebral activity is absent or minimal. These veridical OBE claims are the most scientifically significant, as they would, if verified, be inconsistent with current models of consciousness as entirely brain-dependent.

Notable examples include:
- **Pam Reynolds** (1991) — a neurosurgery patient reported detailed observations of the operating theatre and equipment during a period of hypothermic cardiac arrest with electroencephalographic silence; her account included specific details verified by the surgical team
- **Maria's shoe case** — an often-cited early case in which a patient reported seeing a worn tennis shoe on a specific ledge of the hospital roof during her OBE; the shoe was subsequently found in the location described (reported by social worker Kimberly Clark Sharp, 1984)

The methodological challenge is that such accounts are generally documented retrospectively, and systematic prospective target protocols (such as AWARE) have not yet produced a confirmed veridical OBE case.

## Shared Death Experiences

A related phenomenon, the shared death experience (SDE), involves reports from individuals present at or near the death of another person who report anomalous perceptions — visual apparitions, geometric light phenomena, a sensation of accompanying the dying person partway into another realm — without themselves being close to death. SDEs were documented by Raymond Moody beginning in the 1990s and have been reported by hospice workers, nurses, and family members in multiple independent series.

## Neurological and Psychological Accounts

Mainstream neurological models have proposed several candidate mechanisms for NDE phenomenology:

- Cerebral anoxia producing disinhibition of cortical networks
- REM-intrusion at the point of unconsciousness
- Endogenous ketamine or dimethyltryptamine (DMT) release
- Temporal lobe hyperactivation

Proponents note that such accounts describe potential neural correlates but do not address the accuracy of veridical OBE claims or account for the occurrence of coherent conscious experiences during periods of verified cerebral inactivity.

## Assessment

The NDE literature is the most clinically integrated component of survival research, with prospective studies in peer-reviewed medical and neuroscience journals. The phenomenological record is extensive and cross-culturally consistent. The core empirical challenge — demonstrating accurate perception during verified unconsciousness — has not yet been resolved.
