Between November 10–14, 2004, pilots and crew of the USS Nimitz carrier strike group reported a series of encounters with unidentified aerial phenomena off the coast of southern California. These incidents are among the most thoroughly documented modern UAP cases, supported by radar data, cockpit video, and first-hand testimony from multiple trained military observers.

## The FLIR1 Video

Footage from the forward-looking infrared (FLIR) camera of an F/A-18F Super Hornet, recorded on November 14, 2004, was officially released by the Department of Defense in April 2020. The video shows an object exhibiting rapid acceleration inconsistent with known aircraft performance characteristics.

## Witness Testimony

Commander David Fravor (USN, Ret.) and Lieutenant Commander Jim Slaight reported a direct visual observation of an approximately 40-foot, white, oblong object exhibiting erratic motion patterns and instantaneous acceleration during a training exercise.

Fravor described the object as having no visible propulsion, no wings, no tail, and no exhaust plume.

## Radar and Sensor Correlation

The encounters were tracked on multiple independent sensor platforms:

- **SPY-1 radar** aboard the USS Princeton (CG-59) tracked anomalous objects for approximately two weeks prior to the visual encounter
- **AN/APG-73 airborne radar** aboard Fravor's F/A-18 achieved a brief target lock before the object rapidly repositioned
- **FLIR infrared** recorded the November 14 encounter

## Official Acknowledgment

The DoD's 2020 release of the Nimitz footage confirmed its authenticity. The UAP Task Force report of 2021 and the subsequent AARO reports have not provided a definitive explanation for the Nimitz encounters.

## Classification

This event meets criteria for CE1 classification under the Hynek scale: a close encounter with an anomalous aerial object, observed by multiple trained witnesses at close range, with associated physical sensor corroboration.
