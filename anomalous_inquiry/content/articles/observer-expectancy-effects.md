Observer expectancy effects — also called demand characteristics, observer bias, or expectancy effects — refer to the influence that an observer's beliefs, hopes, or expectations about the outcome of an experiment can have on the results obtained, through subtle and often unconscious mechanisms. In psi research, this problem has two distinct but related dimensions: the effect of the participant's expectations on their performance, and the effect of the experimenter's expectations on outcomes — the latter forming part of the broader "experimenter effect" documented in the parapsychological literature and addressed in a separate article.

## The Basic Mechanism

In any human-subjects experiment, participants are sensitive to contextual cues about what is expected of them. These cues may include:

- The framing and wording of experimental instructions
- The manner of the experimenter
- The physical environment of the experiment
- Prior knowledge about the purpose of the study
- Beliefs about one's own abilities

When these factors systematically favour one direction of response, they can produce artificial associations between participant responses and experimental outcomes that mimic genuine effects. In psi research, where the target response (correct identification of a hidden target, production of an anomalous physiological shift) is difficult to verify through ordinary means, the scope for expectancy-driven distortion is substantial.

## Demand Characteristics

Martin Orne introduced the concept of "demand characteristics" in 1962 to describe the ensemble of cues in an experimental setting that convey to participants the hypothesis being tested and thereby invite responses consistent with that hypothesis. In psi research, a participant who knows that the experimenter believes in psi, or who is motivated by a desire to demonstrate ability, may engage in subtly different behaviours than one who is neutral or sceptical — not through fraud, but through the influence of expectation on attention, criterion setting, and response style.

Several features of psi research are susceptible to demand characteristics:

- **Free-response paradigms** — In tasks where participants generate open descriptions (as in remote viewing or Ganzfeld), demand characteristics may affect what types of content are produced, in ways that interact with typical target content
- **Volunteer selection** — Participants who elect to participate in psi studies are not a random sample; they are likely to believe in psi and to be motivated to perform well, creating a self-selection confound
- **Experimenter demeanour** — Positive feedback during a session (warmth, encouragement) has been associated with higher hit rates in some studies, though this may reflect genuine motivation effects rather than demand characteristics per se

## The Sheep–Goat Effect

One of the most replicated findings in the parapsychological literature concerns the relationship between participant belief in psi and experimental performance. The "sheep–goat effect" — first systematically documented by Gertrude Schmeidler in the 1940s — refers to the consistent pattern in which participants who believe in psi (sheep) score above chance and those who disbelieve (goats) score at or below chance.

A 1993 meta-analysis by Lawrence across 73 studies involving over 4,500 participants found a highly significant overall association (z = 8.17, p < 10⁻¹⁵) between psi belief and experimental performance. This finding is itself ambiguous: it could reflect genuine psi ability facilitated by a conducive belief state, or it could reflect differential susceptibility to demand characteristics among believers — with believers and disbelievers alike producing responses shaped by their expectations rather than by a genuine signal.

Crucially, the sheep–goat effect also poses a theoretical puzzle for the demand-characteristics explanation: if both believers and disbelievers show systematically directional effects (above chance versus below chance), a simple demand-characteristics account needs to explain not merely inflated scores among believers but actively depressed scores among disbelievers — which is not the typical prediction of demand-characteristic models.

## Expectancy in Physiological Paradigms

Observer expectancy effects also operate in physiological psi paradigms, though the mechanisms differ from purely behavioural outcomes. In DMILS (distant mental interaction with living systems) studies and presentiment experiments, physiological measures such as skin conductance or cardiovascular responses are recorded. Participants cannot directly control these measures through compliance with demand characteristics, but expectation can influence arousal levels, attentional state, and the magnitude of orienting responses, all of which affect autonomic measures.

Blinding the participant to the experimental hypothesis — or ideally to their own physiological recording — reduces but does not eliminate this confound, since arousal associated with participation in a psi experiment may itself differ between believers and non-believers, or between participants who are given more versus less information about the study purpose.

## Expectancy Effects in Blind Judging

A specific form of observer expectancy affects the blind judging procedures used in free-response paradigms. Even when judges are formally blinded to the correct target, they may carry implicit preferences for certain target categories, cultural images, or perceptual schemas that interact with the target pool construction in non-random ways. This has been documented in studies where the same judge produces systematically different matching patterns across different target pools, suggesting that judge-specific expectancies or aesthetics interact with the matching task.

## Design Controls

Appropriate experimental design addresses observer expectancy effects through several mechanisms:

- **Deception about study purpose** — participants may be told the study concerns attention, relaxation, or another neutral construct rather than psi; this reduces motivated responding but raises ethical questions about informed consent
- **Non-human biological assay systems** — using bacteria, cell cultures, or other non-human targets removes participant expectancy as a factor, though experimenter expectancy remains
- **Double and triple blinding** — removing knowledge of the target condition from all parties with any interaction role limits the channels through which expectancy can influence outcomes
- **Between-subjects design** — assigning sheep and goat conditions between participants rather than measuring beliefs after the study reduces post-hoc attribution bias

## Assessment

Observer expectancy effects represent a real and important source of potential confound in psi research, particularly in human-subjects paradigms where participants are motivated and self-selected. The sheep–goat effect is itself a robust finding that does not reduce trivially to demand characteristics but introduces its own interpretive complications. Well-designed studies address expectancy through blinding and target selection procedures, but complete elimination of all expectancy channels in human-subjects psi research is methodologically challenging.
