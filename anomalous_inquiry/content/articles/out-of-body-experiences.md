An out-of-body experience (OBE) is a reported phenomenon in which an individual's centre of conscious awareness appears to be located outside the physical body, typically perceiving the body and its surroundings from an elevated or displaced vantage point. OBEs occur spontaneously during sleep transitions, anaesthesia, extreme physical stress, sensory deprivation, and near-death crises, and can in some cases be induced deliberately through specific techniques. They represent one of the most frequently reported anomalous experiences in general population surveys, with prevalence estimates ranging from 5% to 35% depending on methodology and definition.

## Phenomenological Profile

The core OBE is characterised by a sense of spatial dislocation — the experiencer's viewpoint appears to be detached from the physical body, usually elevated above it or positioned elsewhere in the environment. Accompanying features frequently reported include:

- Perception of the physical body from an external perspective, often described as looking down at oneself
- Heightened clarity and vividness of perception relative to ordinary experience
- A sense of movement — floating, gliding, or intentional travel
- Partial or full perception of a secondary "vehicle" or energetic body
- Occasionally, encounter with other presences or environments not corresponding to physical space
- Termination by a sudden return to the physical body, sometimes described as a jolt or snap

The experience is almost universally reported as distinct from dreaming — percipients typically emphasise the lucidity and apparent reality of the state.

## Prevalence and Triggers

Population surveys consistently indicate that OBEs are a common experience across cultures and historical periods. A frequently cited survey by Blackmore (1982) found approximately 12% of a UK general sample reported at least one OBE. Cross-cultural analyses suggest broadly similar prevalence across Western and non-Western populations.

Common reported triggers include:

- **Sleep transitions** (hypnagogic or hypnopompic states) — the most frequently reported context
- **Near-death events** — cardiac arrest, severe trauma, surgical anaesthesia
- **Extreme physical stress** — mountain climbing, high-altitude dehydration, combat
- **Sensory deprivation** — flotation tanks, isolation chambers
- **Psychedelic substances** — particularly ketamine and high-dose dissociatives
- **Deliberate induction** — body-awareness techniques, visualisation, sleep paralysis exploitation

## Laboratory Research

The primary scientific interest in OBEs concerns veridical perception — reports of accurately perceiving events in the physical environment during the experience. Such claims, if verified under controlled conditions, would constitute evidence of extrasensory perception independent of the physical sensory apparatus.

**Charles Tart's early studies (1960s–1970s)** placed hidden target numbers in locations only accessible from an elevated OBE vantage point. Subject "Miss Z" reported the correct five-digit number in one controlled session; methodological questions about that specific trial have been raised by critics.

**Keith Harary's experiments at the Psychical Research Foundation** included a series in which Harary's attempts to project to a distant location and interact with a cat produced measurable reductions in the cat's distress vocalisations during target versus control periods, though replication was not achieved.

**The AWARE studies (Parnia et al., 2008–2014)** represent the most rigorous prospective attempt to test veridical OBE claims. Visual targets — images placed on elevated shelves visible only from above — were installed in cardiac resuscitation units. Of 2,060 cardiac arrest events, one case of accurate OBE perception was reported, though the target card for that patient had not been deployed. No confirmed veridical OBE perception of the study targets was documented in the published phase-one results.

## Induced OBEs and Sleep Research

Neurological research has demonstrated that sensations closely resembling OBEs can be induced through direct stimulation of the right temporoparietal junction (TPJ). Work by Blanke et al. (2002) showed that electrical stimulation of this region in an epilepsy patient produced immediate sensations of floating above the body and viewing it from above. This finding has been interpreted by mainstream neuroscience as evidence that OBE phenomenology is generated by specific neural processes rather than actual spatial displacement of consciousness.

Sleep research identifies the hypnagogic and hypnopompic transitions as the most common natural context for spontaneous OBEs. During these transitions, elements of REM sleep physiology — including atonia and vivid imagery generation — may overlap with partial wakefulness, producing conditions conducive to the experience.

## Theoretical Accounts

The dominant neuroscientific account holds that OBEs result from a temporary disruption of multisensory body-ownership integration, particularly involving the TPJ and related parietal circuits. On this view the experience is an internally generated perceptual construct.

Parapsychological and dualist accounts propose that OBEs represent genuine spatial displacement of a conscious entity separable from the physical body — variously termed the "astral body," "etheric body," or simply a non-physical locus of consciousness. This interpretation is supported by proponents who argue that neuroscientific accounts explain the form of the experience but not the occasional veridical content.

Intermediate positions have been proposed by researchers including Susan Blackmore, who argues that OBEs are internally modelled experiences generated by a self-model that decouples from sensory input under specific conditions, without requiring literal spatial displacement.

## Assessment

OBEs are among the best-documented anomalous experiences in the scientific literature and represent a central challenge for consciousness science. The phenomenological record is extensive and cross-culturally stable. Controlled laboratory investigations of veridical OBE perception have not yet produced results sufficient to resolve the question of whether any extrasensory perceptual component accompanies the experience. The neurological evidence for TPJ involvement is robust but addresses the mechanism of the experience's phenomenology rather than its ultimate ontological status.
