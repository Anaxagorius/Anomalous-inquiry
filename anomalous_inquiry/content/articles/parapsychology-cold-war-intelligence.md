The Cold War produced, on both sides of the Iron Curtain, state-funded programmes investigating anomalous human capabilities for potential intelligence and military applications. The most extensively documented of these is the United States remote viewing programme described in the separate Stargate article. This article provides broader context for the Cold War intelligence framing of parapsychological research, including Soviet programmes, the competitive intelligence dynamic that drove US funding, and the legacy of this period for the field.

## The Soviet and Eastern Bloc Research Programmes

Reports of Soviet interest in parapsychological research circulated in Western intelligence circles from the late 1950s onward. The primary source was a 1960 book, *Telepathy: A Soviet Riddle*, by Czech journalist and emigré Milan Ryzl, followed by a 1970 popular account, *Psychic Discoveries Behind the Iron Curtain*, by Sheila Ostrander and Lynn Schroeder, which described an extensive range of claimed Soviet research into telepathy, psychokinesis, bioenergetics, and related phenomena.

The Ostrander–Schroeder book was widely read in both popular and intelligence communities and contributed substantially to the competitive concern that motivated US programme funding. Its claims were largely anecdotal and unverified, but the hypothesis that the Soviet Union was seriously investigating anomalous human capabilities for military application was treated as a genuine intelligence concern.

Documented Soviet research appears to have concentrated in several centres:

- **The Popov Society** — the All-Union Scientific and Technical Society named for physicist Alexander Popov had a bioinformation section that hosted research presentations on bioenergetics and telepathy
- **Leningrad research** — several Soviet physicists, including Eduard Naumov and others, conducted and published work on telepathy and psychokinesis; Naumov was subsequently imprisoned, reportedly partly due to contacts with Western researchers
- **Nina Kulagina** — the most extensively filmed Soviet subject claimed for psychokinetic ability; filmed demonstrations of apparent object movement and apparent influence on biological systems were reported by multiple researchers and obtained by Western intelligence services; the genuineness of Kulagina's demonstrations has been contested

The actual scale and scientific quality of Soviet psi research is difficult to assess. Some Soviet researchers later acknowledged that the field in the USSR was characterised by the same mixture of genuine investigation and pseudoscience found elsewhere, and that the competitive concern generated in Western intelligence circles probably overstated the coherence and resources of the Soviet programme.

## The US Intelligence Motivation

The CIA's initial funding of remote viewing research at Stanford Research Institute (SRI) in 1972 was explicitly motivated by the concern that the Soviet Union was operationally exploiting anomalous human capabilities and that the US could not afford to concede a potential intelligence advantage without investigation. The funding memo authored by CIA analyst Kit Green cited the Soviet programme reports and argued for a controlled assessment of whether similar effects could be reproduced under US laboratory conditions.

This intelligence motivation shaped the research in several important ways:

- **Results orientation** — unlike academic parapsychology, which could afford extended programmes of exploratory research, the intelligence-funded programme needed to produce operationally useful results on a defined schedule
- **Classification** — much of the programme's work was classified, preventing publication and independent external review; this also meant that negative results were not publicly available for the research community's use
- **Talent search** — the programme was explicitly interested in identifying individuals with high demonstrated remote viewing ability, leading to the recruitment of Ingo Swann, Pat Price, and others
- **Theoretical agnosticism** — the operational focus meant that the programme was relatively uninterested in theoretical mechanism; the question was whether the effect was real and useful, not how it worked

## Key Intelligence Applications

Several declassified documents describe specific intelligence applications of remote viewing during the operational phase of the programme (approximately 1978–1995):

- Location of a Soviet Tupolev Tu-95 aircraft downed in northern Africa (1976); remote viewer Pat Price reportedly identified the location prior to its confirmation by satellite imagery
- Descriptions of a Soviet submarine under construction at a classified facility (1979; viewer was Joseph McMoneagle)
- Location attempts of American hostages in Iran (1979–1981)
- Tasking against suspected Soviet armaments sites

The operational record is difficult to assess objectively: declassified programme documents include both positive assessments of utility and sceptical evaluations. The 1995 AIR (American Institutes for Research) review, which assessed the SAIC-phase laboratory data as statistically significant, was less positive about operational utility, concluding that the results were not sufficiently reliable for intelligence decisions.

## Eastern European and Chinese Programmes

Research into anomalous human capabilities was also documented in other Cold War contexts:

**China** — Research into alleged "extraordinary human functions" (EHF) — including claimed perception of hidden text through non-visual means — attracted substantial state interest in China from the late 1970s onward. Reports of children able to identify text by touch or by holding paper to non-visual body sites circulated and attracted laboratory investigation by physicists. The Chinese government funded research, then suppressed it at various periods; several claims were exposed as fraud but the programme continued in modified form. The political and institutional dynamics of Chinese EHF research differed markedly from the Western intelligence context.

**Israel** — Uri Geller, the Israeli psychic whose demonstrations of metal bending and ESP attracted both popular and scientific attention in the early 1970s, was investigated at SRI by Targ and Puthoff in 1972–1973. The published SRI paper on Geller appeared in *Nature* in 1974 and constituted one of the rare instances of anomalous phenomena research in that journal. Geller's status as a potential intelligence asset is documented in partially declassified materials.

## Legacy and Declassification

The declassification of the Stargate programme documents in 1995 represented a significant event in the history of parapsychology. It confirmed that serious government resources had been committed to anomalous cognition research for over two decades, produced a substantial body of laboratory data (the SAIC dataset in particular), and made available for the first time a systematic operational record.

The legacy of the Cold War intelligence context for parapsychology as a field is mixed. The intelligence funding provided resources and institutional support unavailable through academic channels, and the SAIC laboratory data are among the most methodologically rigorous in the field. At the same time, the association of psi research with intelligence applications contributed to its framing as either militarily dangerous or credulously naive in the popular press, and the classified character of much of the work excluded normal scientific scrutiny for decades.

## Assessment

The Cold War intelligence context produced the most well-resourced and in some respects most methodologically rigorous psi research programme on record. It also embedded that research in a set of institutional constraints — classification, operational pressure, talent-selection — that limit its straightforward application to basic science questions. The declassified record constitutes an important historical and empirical resource for the evaluation of anomalous cognition claims.
