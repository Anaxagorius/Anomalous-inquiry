Poltergeist phenomena (from the German *Poltergeist*, "noisy ghost") are a class of reported anomalous physical disturbances characterised by recurrent, apparently spontaneous movement of objects, unexplained sounds, fires, and occasionally direct physical contact with individuals. Unlike conventional haunting reports, which are typically associated with a specific location, poltergeist activity is frequently associated with a specific person — the "focus" — and tends to follow that person if they move. The term Recurrent Spontaneous Psychokinesis (RSPK), introduced by William Roll, reflects the contemporary parapsychological hypothesis that the phenomena originate in an unconscious psychokinetic process centred on the focus individual rather than in a disembodied entity.

## Phenomenological Profile

The range of reported poltergeist phenomena includes:

- **Object movement**: Objects thrown, displaced, or arriving from apparently no source; typical trajectories are curved or anomalous rather than linear, suggesting that ordinary physical projection is not the cause
- **Auditory phenomena**: Rappings, knockings, banging sounds, sometimes in apparent response to questions; sounds with no identified physical source
- **Apport phenomena**: Objects appearing from unknown locations or disappearing and reappearing elsewhere
- **Fire-starting**: Spontaneous ignition or heat without identified incendiary cause; documented in several historical and contemporary cases
- **Water manifestations**: Spontaneous dripping, puddles, or localised moisture without a physical source
- **Physical contact**: Pushing, slapping, scratching, or biting of the focus individual or other witnesses, occasionally leaving marks

The phenomena are typically intermittent, unpredictable in individual occurrence, and concentrated in bursts that may last weeks to months before declining. Many cases resolve spontaneously.

## Historical Overview

The historical record of poltergeist-type disturbances extends back several centuries. The Drummer of Tedworth (1661), investigated by Joseph Glanvill and reported in *Saducismus Triumphatus* (1681), is among the earliest documented cases to receive systematic investigation by a named observer. The Epworth Poltergeist (1716–17), centred on the household of the Rev. Samuel Wesley and his daughter Hetty, is documented in family correspondence. The Bell Witch case (Adams, Tennessee, 1817–21) is the most extensively documented early American poltergeist account.

Systematic investigation accelerated in the late nineteenth century with the founding of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in 1882. The SPR's *Phantasms of the Living* (Gurney, Myers, Podmore, 1886) and subsequent *Census of Hallucinations* (1894) gathered poltergeist and related case data using more structured methods than earlier accounts. Frank Podmore was a persistent sceptic, attributing most poltergeist cases to fraudulent stone-throwing or mischief by juveniles — a hypothesis that has remained a standard alternative explanation.

## The RSPK Hypothesis

William Roll, beginning with his investigations of the Miami poltergeist case in 1967 and systematised in *The Poltergeist* (1972), developed the RSPK framework. Roll's central observations were:

1. In the majority of well-investigated cases, phenomena were centred on a specific individual (the "focus"), typically an adolescent or young adult, often female, frequently in a state of psychological distress.
2. The focus was rarely directly responsible for the phenomena as a conscious agent; in many cases the focus was among the witnesses and could not physically account for effects.
3. The onset of poltergeist activity frequently coincided with periods of interpersonal conflict, psychological stress, or suppressed hostility.
4. The activity typically declined and ceased without permanent resolution of the physical disturbance.

Roll hypothesised that the focus individual was the unconscious source of a psychokinetic process — anomalous physical influence originating in an unconscious mental state and manifesting as apparently external physical effects. He noted that several focus individuals showed elevated scores on measures of neurological lability (EEG abnormalities, dissociative tendencies) and proposed a relationship between certain altered neurological states and RSPK susceptibility.

## Notable Investigated Cases

### The Enfield Poltergeist (1977–78)

The Enfield case, investigated by Maurice Grosse and Guy Lyon Playfair of the SPR, became one of the most extensively documented modern poltergeist cases. Centred on an 11-year-old girl, Janet Hodgson, in a council house in North London, reported phenomena included furniture movement, levitation of the focus child, object projection, fires, water pools, and a distinctive deep voice that produced extended communications reportedly attributable to a previous occupant of the house. Grosse spent over 200 nights at the property over 18 months. Multiple independent witnesses — police officers, journalists, and neighbours — reported seeing objects move. Some incidents were documented on film and audio recording. The case has been extensively discussed, with sceptics citing opportunity for hoax by the children and proponents citing the range and variety of witnessed phenomena.

### The Rosenheim Poltergeist (1967–68)

The Rosenheim case, centred on Annemarie Schneider, a 19-year-old secretary at a law office in Rosenheim, Bavaria, is notable for involving anomalous effects on electrical and telephone equipment. Investigators included physicists Hans Bender (University of Freiburg) and F.W. Karger and G. Zicha from the Max Planck Institute. Registered anomalies included unexplained fluctuations in electrical supply, swinging of hanging light fixtures, spontaneous rotation of pictures on walls, and telephone malfunctions generating telephone calls that had not been dialled. Phenomena ceased when Schneider left the employment. The physicists could not identify a conventional explanation for the electrical anomalies, which were recorded by installed monitoring equipment.

### The Miami Poltergeist (1967)

Roll's most extensively documented case involved a warehouse in Miami, Florida. Approximately 224 incidents of object movement were recorded over an eight-week period, centred on Julio Vasquez, a 19-year-old shipping clerk. Roll and J.G. Pratt analysed the spatial and temporal distribution of incidents and found a distance-dependent pattern: incident frequency declined with distance from Vasquez, consistent with a field-like influence centred on the focus. This analysis provided one of the few attempts to extract quantitative parameters from poltergeist phenomena.

## Psychological Profile of the Focus

Roll and subsequent investigators have noted several recurring features of poltergeist focus individuals:

- Adolescent or young adult (though cases involving adults and children also occur)
- Psychological distress, suppressed hostility, or interpersonal conflict at the time of onset
- In some cases, neurological atypicality, dissociative tendencies, or subclinical seizure activity
- Relative lack of awareness of being the source of the phenomena

Nandor Fodor proposed a psychoanalytic interpretation in the 1940s, suggesting poltergeist phenomena were projections of unconscious hostility or sexual tension onto the physical environment. This psychodynamic framing has been superseded by Roll's more empirically grounded framework, but the association of RSPK with psychological conflict at onset is widely acknowledged.

## Sceptical Perspectives

Sceptical analyses of poltergeist cases emphasise several points:

- **Opportunity for hoax**: The majority of cases involve juveniles in domestic settings where phenomena cannot be continuously observed. Many cases, when subjected to close surveillance (including concealed cameras), show reduction or cessation of phenomena.
- **Mischievous behaviour**: Several focus individuals have been caught producing phenomena fraudulently, including in the Enfield case. Proponents typically argue that caught hoaxing does not invalidate all reported incidents, particularly those witnessed by multiple independent observers.
- **Confirmation bias in witnesses**: Households already alarmed by unusual events may misinterpret ordinary occurrences as further manifestations.
- **Psychological need for explanation**: The attribution of unusual domestic events to a poltergeist may reflect a narrative impulse that amplifies and organises ambiguous experiences into a coherent account.

Joe Nickell and other scientific sceptics have argued that all poltergeist cases, properly investigated, reduce to a combination of fraud, misperception, and psychological need. No poltergeist case has been conclusively demonstrated to involve genuine anomalous physical effects under controlled conditions.

## Assessment

The poltergeist phenomenon presents a distinctive investigative challenge: the phenomena are recurrent but unpredictable, occur primarily in uncontrolled domestic settings, and centre on individuals who have both opportunity and potential psychological motivation for hoax. The history of the field includes cases that were not adequately controlled and cases where hoax was later demonstrated. At the same time, some cases — particularly Rosenheim, where physical anomalies were recorded by instrumentation — are not fully accounted for by hoax or misperception alone. The RSPK hypothesis provides a coherent theoretical framework but remains speculative. Rigorous prospective investigation of active cases with systematic controls, continuous monitoring, and psychological assessment of the focus individual would be required to advance beyond the current evidential situation.
