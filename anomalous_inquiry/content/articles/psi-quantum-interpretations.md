Quantum mechanics has attracted sustained attention from parapsychologists and theorists seeking a physical basis for psi phenomena. The appeal is understandable: quantum theory permits non-local correlations (entanglement), probabilistic outcomes sensitive to observation (the measurement problem), and — in some interpretations — an irreducible role for observers or information in the description of physical reality. These features have been repeatedly, if controversially, invoked as potential physical scaffolding for phenomena such as telepathy, psychokinesis, and precognition. This article surveys the principal quantum-based proposals, their scientific status, and the objections they face.

## Why Quantum Mechanics Attracts Psi Theorists

Classical physics — Newtonian mechanics and Maxwellian electrodynamics — describes a deterministic, local, and observer-independent world. Within this framework, the occurrence of genuine information transfer without physical signals, or of mind acting on matter without physical force, is simply inconsistent with the foundational equations. Quantum mechanics, by contrast, exhibits three features that appear, at least superficially, to leave more room:

1. **Non-locality**: Bell's theorem (1964) and subsequent experiments (Aspect et al., 1982; Hensen et al., 2015) demonstrate that entangled quantum systems exhibit statistical correlations that cannot be explained by any local hidden variable theory. The correlations are real and experimentally confirmed.

2. **The measurement problem and the role of observation**: In standard quantum formalism, a quantum system evolves as a superposition of states until measurement, at which point a definite outcome is realised. The precise role of the measuring apparatus — and whether "observer" in any sense implies consciousness — is among the most contested questions in the foundations of physics.

3. **Indeterminism**: At the quantum level, individual outcome events are irreducibly probabilistic. If a psi-capable mind could influence which outcome from a quantum superposition is realised, it could in principle influence macroscopic events without violating conservation laws — because no energy would need to be injected, merely the selection among already-possible outcomes.

## Major Quantum Psi Proposals

### Observational Theories

Evan Harris Walker (1975) and Helmut Schmidt proposed that consciousness is connected to quantum state reduction, and that this connection provides the mechanism for both ESP and psychokinesis. Walker's model assigned a specific role to conscious observation in collapsing quantum wavefunctions and proposed that the timing and direction of collapse could be influenced by conscious intent — providing a mechanism for micro-PK effects on random number generators. Schmidt, who conducted the foundational random event generator (REG) research at the Mind Science Foundation, proposed that the quantum events in his REGs were not "collapsed" until consciously observed, allowing for retroactive psychokinetic influence.

Brian Josephson (Nobel Laureate in Physics, 1973) has argued that quantum mechanics and biology are inadequately integrated in current science, and that psi phenomena may involve quantum processes not yet incorporated into standard biological models. Josephson has cited the anomalous cognition data as serious empirical evidence, placing him in a minority position within the physics community.

### Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Orch OR)

Roger Penrose and Stuart Hameroff developed the Orchestrated Objective Reduction hypothesis in the 1990s, proposing that quantum computations in neural microtubules underlie conscious experience, and that objective reduction of the quantum state — a proposed physical process beyond standard unitary evolution — produces moments of conscious experience. Hameroff has extended the hypothesis to suggest that quantum non-locality in microtubule networks could support some forms of anomalous cognition.

The scientific reception of Orch OR has been largely negative. The primary objection, articulated by Tegmark (2000), is that thermal decoherence times in neural tissue are approximately ten orders of magnitude too short for the quantum coherence required by the model to be maintained. Proponents have disputed the precise decoherence calculations, pointing to emerging quantum biology evidence as indirect support, but the consensus position in neuroscience and physics remains that quantum coherence plays no significant role in neural computation.

### Transactional Interpretation and Retrocausality

John Cramer's transactional interpretation of quantum mechanics (1986) describes quantum interactions as arising from a "handshake" between offer waves (propagating forward in time) and confirmation waves (propagating backward in time). This formalism is time-symmetric and explicitly accommodates retrocausal effects. Some parapsychologists — particularly those working on precognition and presentiment — have cited the transactional interpretation as providing a physical framework within which retrocausal psi effects would not be inherently impossible.

The transactional interpretation is a serious proposal within the foundations of quantum mechanics, though not the dominant view. Its relevance to psi rests on the further assumption that macroscopic biological retrocausal effects could arise from the quantum substrate — a step that requires additional theoretical development and is not established.

### Weak Quantum Theory

Harald Walach and colleagues proposed "weak quantum theory" — a generalisation of quantum formalism in which the mathematical structure of quantum mechanics is applied to systems beyond the microphysical domain. In their framework, entanglement-like correlations could arise between arbitrary systems (including minds) that are appropriately coupled, providing a formal basis for psi correlations without requiring quantum mechanics to operate literally at the level of neural biology.

Critics have noted that weak quantum theory achieves its generality by removing the physical constraints that make standard quantum mechanics empirically precise; the resulting framework is too unconstrained to generate falsifiable novel predictions beyond those it was designed to accommodate.

### Decision Augmentation Theory

Edwin May, Jessica Utts, and James Spottiswoode proposed decision augmentation theory (DAT) as an empirically grounded alternative to energy or signal models of psi. DAT holds that psi effects — particularly in random event generator experiments — arise not from direct physical influence on quantum processes but from precognitive access to future information that guides the experimenter's (or participant's) decisions about when to initiate a trial or which targets to select. On this model, no violation of physical conservation laws occurs; instead, the psi capacity is a form of prospective information acquisition that biases experimental sampling.

DAT has been developed into a set of testable predictions about the statistical signatures of genuine PK versus DAT-consistent effects, and the collaboration between May and Utts represents one of the more rigorous statistical frameworks within the psi–physics intersection.

## The No-Communication Theorem and Its Implications

A fundamental constraint on quantum non-locality is the no-communication theorem: quantum entanglement cannot be used to transmit information faster than light. The correlations produced by entangled particles are real but cannot be exploited by either party to send a message to the other. This is a theorem within standard quantum mechanics, not a merely practical limitation.

This theorem poses a direct problem for quantum psi models that invoke entanglement as a mechanism for telepathic information transfer. If entanglement cannot carry information even in physics, it is unclear how biological entanglement would support anomalous information acquisition. Some theorists distinguish between information and correlation, arguing that anomalous cognition might involve non-local correlations rather than information transfer in the strict Shannon sense — but this distinction has not been developed into a precise theoretical framework.

## Reception Within Physics

The quantum psi proposals are regarded with scepticism or dismissal by the mainstream physics and neuroscience communities. The principal objections are:

- **Decoherence**: Macroscopic quantum coherence does not survive in warm, wet biological systems on timescales relevant to cognition.
- **Misapplication of quantum formalism**: Many quantum psi models apply quantum concepts loosely, invoking "non-locality" or "entanglement" as metaphors rather than within the mathematical framework of quantum mechanics.
- **No-communication theorem**: Standard quantum non-locality cannot support information transfer.
- **Lack of falsifiability**: Most quantum psi proposals do not generate predictions beyond the psi phenomena they were designed to explain.

Some physicists — including Josephson and, more recently, Henry Stapp — have argued that the dismissal of quantum mind and psi connections within the physics community reflects dogmatic materialism rather than principled scientific assessment. This position remains a minority view.

## Assessment

Quantum mechanics provides a physically rigorous framework in which the universe is demonstrably non-local at the microphysical level, probabilistic at the fundamental level, and contested regarding the role of observation and measurement. These features have motivated a wide variety of proposals linking quantum theory to psi phenomena. None of the existing proposals has achieved scientific acceptance; the principal problems are the decoherence barrier, the no-communication theorem, and the tendency to invoke quantum language metaphorically rather than formally. The experimental data from anomalous cognition research, if taken seriously, motivates the search for physical frameworks that can accommodate such effects; quantum mechanics, as currently understood, does not straightforwardly provide one, though its foundations remain genuinely unsettled and theoretically active.
