Remote viewing (RV) is a structured protocol for attempting to perceive information about a geographically distant or otherwise shielded target by means other than known sensory channels. From 1972 to 1995, the United States government funded a series of classified research and operational programmes at Stanford Research Institute (SRI) and later at Science Applications International Corporation (SAIC) to assess the potential intelligence utility of remote viewing. The programmes were collectively known under a succession of code names — SCANATE, GONDOLA WISH, GRILL FLAME, CENTER LANE, SUN STREAK, and finally STARGATE.

## Origins at SRI

In 1972, physicists Russell Targ and Harold Puthoff at SRI International (formerly Stanford Research Institute) began conducting formal laboratory tests of alleged psychic individuals. Initial work with Ingo Swann and later Uri Geller focused on perception of shielded targets and remote object descriptions. The CIA funded early phases under contract, motivated by reports of Soviet research into anomalous human capabilities.

The core remote viewing protocol required a percipient, working under controlled conditions, to generate a free-form verbal or written-and-sketched description of a target — typically a physical location selected by a separate outbound agent who had travelled to the site. Comparisons between descriptions and target pools were made by blind judges.

## Targ and Puthoff Results

In a series of 51 outdoor remote viewing trials published in *Nature* in 1974, Targ and Puthoff reported that independent blind judges matched viewer transcripts to the correct target location significantly above chance. The publication was notable for the prestige of the journal and the controversy it generated. The authors reported precautions against conventional explanation but acknowledged that the methodology would benefit from further independent replication.

## Operational Use

By the late 1970s and early 1980s, the programme had transitioned from basic research to operational application, with military remote viewers tasked with intelligence objectives including locating hostages, describing foreign military installations, and tracking individuals. The operational record — much of it still partially classified — included both claimed successes and notable failures.

Key figures in the operational programme included remote viewers Ingo Swann, Pat Price, Joe McMoneagle, and others. McMoneagle received the Legion of Merit in 1984 partly in recognition of his contributions to intelligence gathering.

## The SAIC Phase and Declassification

A later phase of research conducted at SAIC in the late 1980s and early 1990s included more rigorous controls and was reviewed by an independent scientific panel that included Jessica Utts (University of California, Davis) and Ray Hyman (University of Oregon). In 1995, following a congressional directive, the CIA commissioned the American Institutes for Research (AIR) to evaluate the programme. The AIR report, authored by Utts and Hyman, acknowledged statistically significant anomalous effects in the SAIC laboratory data but disagreed on their interpretation and operational utility.

Utts concluded: "Using the standards applied to any other area of science, it is concluded that psychic functioning has been well-established." Hyman maintained that methodological issues precluded firm conclusions.

The full programme was declassified and its existence publicly acknowledged in November 1995.

## Coordinate Remote Viewing Protocol

SRI developed a structured training method — Coordinate Remote Viewing (CRV) — systematised by Ingo Swann. CRV breaks the perceptual process into discrete stages, moving from gestalt sensory impressions to dimensional data, structural descriptions, and analytical material. The structured nature of CRV was intended to reduce analytical overlay (front-loading of conscious inference).

## Assessment

The Stargate programme represents the most extensively documented government-funded anomalous cognition research programme on record. The declassified SAIC data — including 455 trials — showed effect sizes statistically inconsistent with chance. Whether these effects were sufficient for reliable operational application and whether they were attributable to genuine anomalous cognition remains the subject of ongoing research and disagreement.
