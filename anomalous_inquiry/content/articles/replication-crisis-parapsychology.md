The replication crisis — the widespread failure of originally reported findings to reproduce in independent studies — has been a prominent concern across experimental psychology, medicine, and the social sciences since at least the early 2010s. Parapsychology has its own longstanding version of this problem, with features that are in some respects distinctive and in others representative of the broader crisis in empirical science.

## Replication in Parapsychology: A Historical View

Inconsistency of replication has characterised psi research since J.B. Rhine's initial card-guessing experiments at Duke in the 1930s. Rhine's own laboratory reported highly significant results that proved difficult to replicate by independent groups even when those groups attempted to follow similar protocols. Early critics attributed this to methodological flaws in Rhine's laboratory; Rhine and colleagues argued that differences in experimenter attitude, participant selection, and environmental conditions might account for the discrepancy.

This early debate established a pattern that has persisted: psi effects reported by one laboratory or researcher fail to replicate in independent studies, producing a literature where the overall meta-analytic effect appears significant but individual replications are inconsistently positive. Whether this pattern reflects genuine effect heterogeneity, selective reporting, experimenter-specific factors, or the absence of a real effect has remained unresolved.

## The Magnitude of the Replication Problem

Quantitative assessments of replication rates in parapsychology are complicated by the absence of a consensus registry of attempted replications. However, several features of the literature have been documented:

- **Ganzfeld replications** — The Ganzfeld paradigm, one of the more rigorously conducted psi paradigms, has shown inconsistent replication. The 1999 Milton and Wiseman meta-analysis of 30 independent Ganzfeld studies found a non-significant effect, in contrast to the earlier significant effect from autoganzfeld studies. Subsequent reanalysis suggested the non-significant result reflected inclusion of methodologically deviant studies; re-running the analysis on protocol-conforming studies produced a borderline significant effect. The debate remains unresolved.
- **Micro-PK with RNGs** — The PEAR laboratory (Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research) reported cumulative significant shifts in output of random event generators across a 25-year programme (1979–2007). Independent replication attempts have produced inconsistent results; a formal multi-site replication under the auspices of the Psychophysical Research Laboratories found non-significant effects.
- **Presentiment studies** — Dean Radin's presentiment paradigm has been independently replicated in several laboratories (Bierman, Scholte; Spottiswoode, May) with positive results, and in others with null results, yielding an overall positive but inconsistent picture in the meta-analytic record.
- **Bem's "Feeling the Future" (2011)** — The publication in *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* of nine apparent retroactive influence experiments by Daryl Bem prompted numerous independent replication attempts. A 2016 meta-analysis of replication attempts found a significant combined effect (Bem et al.), but a large pre-registered replication by Galak, LeBoeuf, Nelson, and Simmons (2012) reported null results across seven studies.

## Comparison to the Mainstream Replication Crisis

The broader replication crisis, catalysed in part by the Open Science Collaboration's 2015 finding that only approximately 36% of psychological findings replicated at the same significance level in the original direction, has made the previously unusual features of the parapsychological literature appear less exotic. Psi researchers have noted that the replication rates in social psychology are not obviously superior to those in parapsychology, despite the absence of any anomalous theoretical assumption.

This comparison cuts in two directions. It may indicate that the criteria applied to parapsychology — that failure to replicate is evidence against genuine effects — should be applied with equal force across experimental psychology and medicine, which would substantially weaken mainstream social psychology as well as parapsychology. Or it may indicate that both bodies of literature are affected by the same methodological pathologies, and that neither should be treated as establishing robust findings without systematic replication improvement.

## Paradigmatic Lock-In and Protocol Drift

A specific problem in the parapsychological replication literature is protocol drift — the tendency for nominally "replication" studies to deviate substantially from the original design in ways that affect the likelihood of replicating an effect. In the Ganzfeld literature, Milton and Wiseman's 1999 review documented substantial procedural heterogeneity across studies that were represented as Ganzfeld replications. This raises the question of whether what is being measured is a replication of a specific protocol or a loose family of similar procedures.

Protocol drift is not unique to parapsychology — it is a documented problem in pharmacological and clinical trial replication efforts — but it interacts with the theoretical uncertainty in psi research to make replication records even harder to interpret.

## Pre-Registration as a Partial Response

Since approximately 2012, the adoption of pre-registration — specifying hypotheses, protocols, and analysis plans before data collection begins and depositing them in a public registry — has been advocated as a partial response to the replication crisis in both mainstream psychology and parapsychology. Pre-registered psi studies have been conducted and registered on the Open Science Framework and similar platforms.

The evidence from pre-registered psi studies is more mixed than the historical literature, consistent with the expectation that pre-registration removes some positive-result inflation. However, several pre-registered studies have reported positive findings, suggesting that the effect is not entirely attributable to flexible analysis.

## The Role of Theoretical Frameworks

A distinctive feature of the parapsychology replication problem is that there is no agreed theoretical framework that would specify under what conditions effects should replicate. In pharmacology, a pharmacokinetic model makes specific predictions about dose–response relationships and patient populations in which effects should reproduce. No equivalent model exists for psi effects, leaving replication attempts unable to pre-specify which protocol modifications should be expected to preserve or eliminate the effect.

## Assessment

The replication problem in parapsychology is real, longstanding, and not yet resolved by any combination of methodological improvements. The spread of the replication crisis to mainstream experimental science has contextualised but not dissolved this problem. The most productive approach in the current research environment is likely to be formal adversarial collaboration designs, pre-registered multi-site studies with agreed protocols, and systematic analysis of the variables that predict positive versus null outcomes within the existing literature.
