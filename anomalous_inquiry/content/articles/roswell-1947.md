In early July 1947, something fell near Roswell, New Mexico. What followed — the initial Army Air Force press release announcing a "flying disc," the rapid retraction, and decades of conflicting official statements — constitutes one of the most documented controversies in the history of anomalous phenomena.

## Initial Reports

On July 8, 1947, Public Information Officer Walter Haut issued a press release on behalf of Colonel William Blanchard at Roswell Army Air Field stating that personnel had recovered the remains of a "flying disc." The statement was transmitted nationally by wire services.

Within hours, a second, contradicting statement from General Roger Ramey at Fort Worth Army Air Field asserted that the debris was a conventional weather balloon.

## Physical Evidence

Witnesses who handled or observed the debris before its transfer described materials with unusual properties:

- Foil-like material that returned to its original shape after being crumpled
- Structural members bearing unfamiliar symbols
- Extreme light weight relative to apparent strength

Mac Brazel, the rancher who first discovered the field of debris, described the material as unlike anything he had previously encountered.

## Documentary Record

The Roswell incident generated an unusually dense paper trail. FOIA requests beginning in the 1970s produced thousands of pages of records, though significant gaps remain. A 1994 Air Force report attributed the debris to Project Mogul, a classified high-altitude balloon program designed to monitor Soviet nuclear tests.

## Assessment

This archive documents the Roswell incident without adjudicating competing explanations. The official record contains demonstrable inconsistencies. The physical nature of the recovered material remains disputed.
