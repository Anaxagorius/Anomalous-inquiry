The evidential case for psi phenomena rests substantially on statistical arguments: that hit rates, effect sizes, or physiological measurements in psi experiments differ from chance at levels that cannot be attributed to sampling variation alone. Conversely, sceptical arguments also rely on statistical reasoning — that effect sizes are inflated by selective reporting, that significance thresholds are set post-hoc, or that meta-analytic aggregation conceals heterogeneity. Understanding the statistical dimensions of this debate is essential for evaluating the literature.

## The Basic Statistical Picture

Psi effects, where they are reported, are characteristically small. In the Ganzfeld paradigm, the reported mean hit rate across the literature is approximately 30–32% against a 25% chance baseline — an absolute excess of 5–7 percentage points. In forced-choice precognition studies, effect sizes in the range of d = 0.1 to d = 0.3 are typical. In micro-PK experiments with random event generators (as in the PEAR laboratory), reported mean shifts from chance are fractions of a standard deviation.

These effect sizes are not dramatically different from those reported in well-accepted areas of psychology and medicine. The question of statistical significance — and what it implies — is therefore central to both the psi advocate's and critic's positions.

## Statistical Significance and Its Limits

A finding is statistically significant at the p < 0.05 threshold if random chance would produce data at least as extreme only 5% of the time under the null hypothesis. In psi research, many findings are reported at much more extreme thresholds — p < 0.001, p < 10⁻⁵, or lower in large meta-analyses. However, statistical significance is neither sufficient for the claim of anomalous effect nor for its dismissal:

- **Publication bias** — if only significant results are published, then any collection of independent studies drawn from the literature will show systematically inflated effect sizes and significance levels (see the separate article on the file-drawer effect)
- **p-hacking** — selective analysis of multiple dependent and independent variables, stopping rules adjusted to significance, or other analytic flexibility can produce nominally significant results from null data
- **Base rate of genuine effects** — in a domain where prior probability of a true effect is genuinely uncertain, a p-value does not directly convey the probability that the null hypothesis is false (this is the base-rate fallacy in Fisherian inference)

These problems are not unique to psi research; they are general concerns in empirical science identified prominently in the replication crisis literature.

## Effect Size Metrics

Effect size measures the practical magnitude of an effect independently of sample size. Common metrics in the psi literature include:

- **Cohen's d** — standardised mean difference; values of 0.2 are conventionally described as small, 0.5 as medium, 0.8 as large
- **r** — Pearson correlation coefficient or its equivalent; many meta-analyses in psi research report mean effect sizes in the range r = 0.1 to r = 0.3
- **Odds ratio** — used in forced-choice and binary outcome designs
- **Stouffer z and Fisher combined p** — aggregate significance measures used in meta-analyses

A finding with a large sample that achieves high statistical significance but a very small effect size (r = 0.01, d = 0.05) may be statistically unambiguous but theoretically and practically minimal. Conversely, a finding with a larger effect size in a smaller sample may fail to reach conventional significance while representing a more impressive deviation from chance.

## The Meta-Analytic Approach

Because individual psi studies often have limited statistical power, meta-analysis — the quantitative aggregation of results across multiple independent studies — has become the primary evidential vehicle in parapsychology. Key meta-analyses in the field include:

- **Honorton (1985)** — 28 Ganzfeld studies, combined hit rate significantly above chance
- **Bem and Honorton (1994)** — 11 autoganzfeld studies, z = 6.60
- **Radin and Nelson (1989)** — 832 micro-PK studies with RNGs, combined effect size consistent across methodologically screened subsets
- **Storm, Tressoldi, and Di Risio (2010)** — 108 free-response studies, mean d = 0.65
- **Mossbridge, Tressoldi, and Utts (2012)** — 26 presentiment studies, combined p < 10⁻¹¹

Critics have examined these meta-analyses for signs of publication bias using funnel plot asymmetry, trim-and-fill corrections, and the fail-safe N calculation (the number of null studies that would be required to reduce the combined effect to non-significance). In several cases, the fail-safe N required to nullify the reported effect is implausibly large given the size of the research community.

## The Decline Effect

An empirical anomaly noted within psi research is the "decline effect": the tendency for effect sizes to decrease over time in a given paradigm, with initial studies showing larger effects than later replications. This pattern has been observed in the Ganzfeld literature and in Rhine-era forced-choice work. Several explanations have been proposed:

- **Regression to the mean** — initial high-effect studies are positively selected for publication or attention; subsequent independent replications regress toward the true (lower) population effect
- **Methodological improvement** — later studies with tighter controls eliminate variance that was confounded with the effect in earlier, less rigorous designs
- **Selection of participants** — early studies may have used unrepresentative samples of particularly psi-conducive individuals
- **The experimenter effect** — changes in experimenter variables across the research programme affect outcomes (see separate article)

The decline effect is not specific to psi research; it has been documented in pharmacological and social psychology literatures as well.

## Bayesian Approaches

A growing body of parapsychological literature has adopted Bayesian statistical frameworks, which require explicit specification of prior probability distributions and compute posterior probabilities given the data. Bayesian analyses of major psi meta-analyses yield different conclusions depending on the prior. Sceptical priors (assigning very low a priori probability to genuine psi effects) produce low posterior probabilities even for large observed effects. More neutral priors yield intermediate conclusions. This demonstrates that the statistical case for psi cannot be separated from prior assumptions about theoretical plausibility.

## Assessment

The statistical record in psi research is non-trivial. Several paradigms show meta-analytic effects that, by the standards of applied statistics, would be considered significant if they appeared in other research domains. The key unresolved questions are not primarily statistical — they concern the degree to which publication bias, flexible analysis, and undisclosed methodological heterogeneity account for reported effects. These questions are empirically addressable through preregistration, independent data audits, and adversarial collaboration designs, several of which are underway or completed.
