On the night of September 18–19, 1976, Iranian Air Force F-4 Phantom II fighters attempted to intercept an unidentified aerial object over Tehran. The encounters involved two aircraft, multiple radar contacts, reported avionics and weapons system malfunctions, and visual observations by both pilots and ground personnel. The case is among the most thoroughly documented military aviation UAP encounters in the historical record.

## Initial Reports and Scramble

At approximately 12:30 a.m. on September 19, the Imperial Iranian Air Force command at Shahrokhi Air Force Base received reports of a bright, rapidly moving aerial object from civilian residents of northern Tehran. The duty officer, General Yousefi, initially dismissed the reports but subsequently stepped outside and confirmed the presence of a luminous object himself.

An F-4 Phantom was scrambled under General Yousefi's direct order. As the aircraft approached to within 25 nautical miles of the object, its instruments malfunctioned and communications were lost, forcing the pilot to abort the intercept. Systems were restored as the aircraft turned back.

A second F-4 was scrambled, piloted by Lieutenant Jafari (front seat) and Lieutenant Shokri (rear seat/WSO). The aircraft acquired the object on radar — a return comparable to the size of a Boeing 707 tanker. As the second fighter approached, the object accelerated and maintained a consistent distance ahead.

## The Principal Encounter

As the chase continued northwest of Tehran, a smaller object separated from the primary UAP and approached the F-4 at high speed. Jafari attempted to lock his AIM-9 Sidewinder missiles, but the aircraft's weapons system failed — according to Jafari, entirely without prior fault indication. He initiated a hard break turn to evade what appeared to be an incoming object. The smaller object circled the fighter and returned to the primary UAP.

A second smaller object then detached and descended rapidly toward the ground. The pilots expected an impact explosion, but observed only a bright illumination of a dry lake bed in the area of Shahrabad. No explosion or impact damage was found on subsequent ground inspection.

As the F-4 continued to track the primary object, it experienced a communications blackout on its UHF radio channel and its Internavigation System (INS) failed.

A third aircraft, a C-130, and a civilian airliner subsequently also observed the primary object in the same area.

## Official Documentation

The case is documented in a U.S. Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA) report dated September 19, 1976, produced by the U.S. Defence Attaché in Tehran. The report was distributed to the White House, the Secretary of State, the NSA, the JCS, and other high-level recipients.

The DIA evaluation at the bottom of the report states:

> "An outstanding report. This case is a classic which meets all the criteria necessary for a valid study of the UFO phenomenon:
> a) The object was seen by multiple witnesses from different locations;
> b) The credibility of many of the witnesses was high (experienced pilots);
> c) Visual sightings were confirmed by radar;
> d) The phenomena occurred over a significant period of time;
> e) There were electromagnetic effects;
> f) There were physical effects in the witnesses themselves."

This internal official evaluation makes the Tehran case exceptional in the declassified government record: a U.S. intelligence assessment explicitly characterising a UAP case as significant, credible, and unresolved.

## Characteristics of Note

The Tehran encounter displays several characteristics frequently reported in high-quality UAP cases:

- **Radar-visual correlation**: Independent radar confirmation of a visually observed object
- **Performance beyond known aircraft**: The object maintained distance ahead of the pursuing F-4 regardless of the fighter's speed, then accelerated further when pressed
- **Electromagnetic effects on aircraft systems**: Weapons, navigation, and communications failures temporally correlated with proximity to the object
- **Apparent reactive behaviour**: The object appeared to respond dynamically to the intercept attempt
- **Multiple independent witnesses**: Ground personnel, two F-4 crews, and civilian airline crew

## Classification

The Tehran case qualifies as CE-2 under the Hynek classification: a close encounter with documented electromagnetic effects on the intercepting aircraft. The apparent detachment and intercept by a subordinate object adds a dimension not captured by standard CE categories.
