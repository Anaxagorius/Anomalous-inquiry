Trance designates a broad class of altered states of consciousness characterised by reduced responsiveness to ordinary environmental stimuli, focused or absorbed attention, partial or complete suspension of reflective self-monitoring, and altered identity or agency. Trance states occur across a spectrum from light hypnotic absorption to deep mediumistic or shamanic dissociation, and have been central to religious, healing, and divinatory practices across virtually every known culture. Within parapsychology and consciousness research, they are of interest both as potential psi-conducive states and as the reported medium through which anomalous information is accessed by mediums, shamans, and oracles.

## Classification and Varieties

Trance is not a single uniform state but a family of related conditions sharing certain functional characteristics. Researchers have distinguished several principal forms:

**Hypnotic trance** — produced through standardised induction procedures involving focused attention, relaxation, and suggestion. Hypnotic trance ranges from light suggestibility to deep somnambulism; its phenomenological hallmarks include reduced critical evaluation, heightened response to suggestions, selective amnesia, and in deep states, capacity for complex post-hypnotic behaviour and reported age regression or past-life recall.

**Mediumistic trance** — reported by trance mediums, in which ordinary waking identity is temporarily supplanted or overlaid by ostensibly external communicating entities. Historically distinguished between light trance (maintaining partial waking awareness) and deep trance (complete amnesia for trance content post-emergence). Associated with the spiritualist tradition and formal survival research.

**Shamanic trance** — deliberately induced altered states used by practitioners across indigenous traditions to navigate non-ordinary realities for purposes of healing, divination, or community mediation. Induced through rhythmic percussion, breathwork, fasting, and psychoactive substances.

**Possession trance** — distinguished by anthropologists from shamanic trance by the inward direction of the entity (the practitioner is entered by the spirit rather than journeying outward); characteristic of traditions including Haitian Vodou, Brazilian Umbanda, and Afro-Cuban Santería.

## Trance Mediumship and Parapsychological Research

Trance mediumship — in which an individual enters an apparent trance and provides communications attributed to deceased persons — was the primary experimental paradigm in the early decades of psychical research following the founding of the SPR in 1882.

**Early investigations:** The SPR conducted extensive investigations of prominent trance mediums including Mrs Leonora Piper (Boston), Mrs Gladys Osborne Leonard (London), and Eusapia Palladino. Mrs Piper in particular underwent decades of investigation by researchers including Richard Hodgson, William James, and Oliver Lodge, including periods of covert surveillance to rule out cold reading and prior research. The investigators concluded that the volume and specificity of accurate communications was not explicable by normal means.

**Cross-correspondences:** From approximately 1901 to 1932, a series of complex literary scripts were received through several independent mediums in different countries, containing fragmentary references that cohered into intelligible classical allusions only when collated across mediums. The cross-correspondences were regarded by investigators including Eleanor Sidgwick and Gerald Balfour as specifically designed to rule out telepathy from the living as an explanation.

**Modern proxy sittings and double-blind studies:** Contemporary survival research has employed proxy sitter designs — in which the experimenter rather than the emotionally involved bereaved sitter attends, preventing cues from grief responses — and triple-blind protocols in which mediums receive minimal target information. Researchers including Gary Schwartz (University of Arizona) have reported statistically significant above-chance accuracy in such conditions, with replication attempted by Beischel and Schwartz (2007) in the Windbridge protocol. The methodology and interpretation of these studies remain actively debated.

## Hypnosis and Anomalous Phenomena

The relationship between hypnosis and parapsychological phenomena has a complex research history:

**Psi in hypnosis:** Several investigators have reported that hypnotic induction increases performance on ESP tasks relative to waking baseline, though meta-analytic results are mixed. A 1994 meta-analysis by Stanford and Stein found a small positive association. The hypothesis is that hypnosis reduces analytic interference with anomalous signal reception, consistent with the noise-reduction model of psi.

**Past-life regression:** Under deep hypnosis, some subjects produce detailed narratives of putative previous lives, including names, locations, and historical details they claim not to have known consciously. Ian Stevenson and colleagues investigated a subset of these cases against historical records; the methodological challenge is distinguishing genuine anomalous information from cryptomnesia — unconscious recall of material encountered and forgotten.

**Age regression:** Hypnotic age regression produces apparently convincing childlike behaviour and reports, but controlled studies have not confirmed access to genuinely earlier memory traces not available in waking recall.

## Neurological Correlates

Neuroimaging studies of hypnotic trance have documented increased activity in fronto-parietal networks associated with sustained attention and reduced activity in the default mode network regions associated with reflective self-monitoring. Highly hypnotisable individuals show distinct resting-state connectivity patterns, particularly involving the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and salience networks.

EEG studies of deep mediumistic trance have produced reports of anomalous patterns — including theta hypersynchrony and reductions in beta activity inconsistent with task performance complexity — but the existing literature is limited and methodologically variable.

## Trance in Cross-Cultural Context

The anthropological literature documents trance states as near-universal features of human ritual life. Claude Lévi-Strauss and subsequent scholars noted structural parallels between shamanistic healing and psychotherapeutic processes. Michael Harner's foundational work in core shamanism documented consistent induction techniques and experiential phenomenology across unrelated indigenous traditions, including the characteristic "lower world" and "upper world" journeys reported by practitioners.

The universality of trance practices has been interpreted by some researchers as evidence for a common neurological substrate producing broadly consistent phenomenological features across cultural contexts, and by others as indicating access to a shared non-ordinary reality whose existence is independent of any particular cultural framing.

## Assessment

Trance states represent one of the oldest and most culturally widespread categories of altered experience and one of the earliest subjects of systematic parapsychological investigation. The research record on trance mediumship includes some of the most detailed case documentation in psychical research. Laboratory investigation of psi under hypnotic conditions has produced inconsistent results. The neurological correlates of various trance states are being progressively characterised. The central interpretive question — whether the anomalous information reported in trance states reflects genuine extrasensory or survival-related acquisition — has not been resolved by available evidence.
