Systematic UAP research depends on the integration of multiple independent data streams. No single observational channel provides sufficient basis for confident case assessment; the strongest cases in the literature are those in which several independent sources converge on anomalous characteristics that resist conventional explanation.

## Primary Observational Data

### Pilot Reports

Aviator testimony constitutes one of the most valuable data sources in the UAP literature. Pilots — particularly military aviators — are trained observers, familiar with the full range of conventional aircraft types, atmospheric optical phenomena, and sensor limitations. Their professional incentive is generally to avoid rather than invite non-conventional explanations.

Both civilian and military pilot reports have formed the basis for significant investigations, including the Project Blue Book files, the French GEIPAN database, and the more recent documentation associated with the USS Nimitz and other naval encounters.

The underreporting problem is significant: survey data and testimony from organisations like NARCAP and Ryan Graves's Americans for Safe Aerospace consistently indicate that pilots experience anomalous observations at substantially higher rates than those formally filed, due to fear of professional consequences and institutional stigma.

### Air Traffic Control Recordings

ATC audio and radar data provide contemporaneous documentation independent of witness testimony. In high-quality cases, ATC recordings capture the moment an unidentified return is first noted, controller reactions, and communications with aircraft in the vicinity. The 1952 Washington D.C. incidents and several European cases include ATC documentation.

### Naval Sensor Systems

The combat information systems aboard naval vessels — including SPY-1 phased-array radar, passive sonar, and electro-optical tracking systems — represent the highest-density military sensor environments routinely available for UAP analysis. The 2004 Nimitz encounters are notable for involving the USS Princeton's extended radar tracking of anomalous objects over approximately two weeks prior to the aerial intercept.

### Ground-Based Radar

Civilian and military air traffic radar provides the most complete persistent coverage of airspace. Radar-only UAP detections — returns without visual confirmation — constitute a significant fraction of documented cases. Ground-based radar coverage is geographically patchy, altitude-limited, and subject to anomalous propagation artefacts, but remains the primary instrument for establishing objective flight trajectory data.

### Satellite Telemetry

Satellite-based observations of UAP remain poorly documented in the open literature, due partly to classification and partly to the difficulty of interpreting imagery taken from orbital altitudes. Where satellite data has been referenced in connection with UAP cases, it typically appears in the form of ambiguous thermal or optical anomalies in existing surveillance imagery rather than purpose-acquired observations.

### Acoustic Data

The absence of expected acoustic signatures — notably, the absence of sonic booms from objects estimated to be moving at hypersonic velocity — is itself informative. Acoustic monitoring networks (including those maintained for nuclear treaty verification) have occasionally been cited in connection with UAP events. Direct acoustic capture of UAP-related phenomena remains rare in the documented literature.

## Documentation and Analysis Methods

### Case File Analysis

The primary analytical method in historical UAP research has been the structured review of individual case files: initial report forms, investigator interview records, physical evidence documentation, and ancillary data. The Project Blue Book archive, the NICAP database, and the GEIPAN records represent the major structured repositories.

Case file analysis is limited by data quality at the collection stage. Standardised reporting forms with consistent fields are essential for cross-case comparison; the historical record is characterised by inconsistent documentation.

### Witness Triangulation

Where multiple independent witnesses are present, their accounts can be analysed for consistency and used to reconstruct object position and trajectory. The geometric constraints of multi-witness reports can sometimes establish minimum velocity or angular size independent of individual testimony reliability.

### Event Reconstruction

For cases with sufficient data, 3D trajectory reconstruction can be attempted using radar tracks, photogrammetric analysis of photographs or video, and geometric triangulation from witness positions. The Scientific Coalition for UAP Studies has published several detailed reconstructions of naval UAP cases using this methodology.

### Declassified Document Review

FOIA releases, mandatory declassification reviews, and voluntary government disclosures have produced large volumes of relevant documentation. The Black Vault archive and the National Archives provide access to historical UAP-related government records. Primary source review is essential for establishing what was actually known, investigated, and concluded by official programmes.

### Photogrammetry

Quantitative analysis of photographs and video can extract angular size, estimated distance and velocity (where reference objects are available), and morphological characteristics. Photogrammetric analysis is complicated by the frequent absence of calibrated reference objects in UAP imagery, and by the difficulty of establishing camera parameters for legacy photography.

### Sensor Artefact Analysis

A core methodological responsibility in UAP analysis is ruling out sensor artefacts before attributing anomalous characteristics to the target. Known artefact types include:
- **Radar**: Anomalous propagation, sea clutter, velocity aliasing, ground returns
- **FLIR/infrared**: Parallax effects from target-background relative motion, focus instability, background thermal gradients
- **Optical**: Lens flare, defocus halos, camera gimbal lag

The "Gimbal" video released by the DoD generated substantial post-publication analysis regarding whether its apparent rotation could be attributed to the gimbal derotation mechanism of the ATFLIR pod — illustrating the importance of sensor artefact characterisation in UAP case analysis.

## Data Standardisation Challenges

A persistent problem in UAP research is the lack of consistent data collection standards across cases, organisations, and time periods. Contemporary initiatives — including AARO's standardised reporting system and the NASA UAP study's recommendations — aim to establish:
- Minimum required data fields for a reportable case
- Calibrated, sensor-platform-specific artefact characterisation
- Multi-source data fusion protocols
- Standardised confidence ratings for anomaly classification

Without such standards, cross-case comparison and statistical analysis remain limited by data heterogeneity.

## Methodological Disclaimer

UAP analysis documents unidentified phenomena — it does not resolve their origin or nature. A finding that an object resists conventional explanation after thorough analysis represents a genuine anomaly in the evidential sense; it does not constitute evidence for any particular hypothesis. Rigorous methodology requires that both the anomaly and the residual conventional hypothesis space be characterised explicitly.
