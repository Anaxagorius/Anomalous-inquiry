UAP sightings have not been uniformly distributed across time. The documented history of the phenomenon is characterised by distinct periods of elevated activity — commonly called "waves" or "flaps" in the research literature — separated by relatively quieter intervals. These waves appear to correlate with geopolitical conditions, technological change, and shifts in reporting culture, though the causal relationships remain unclear. This article surveys the major historical periods from the 1940s to the present.

## 1940s: Foo Fighters and the Post-War Surge

### The Foo Fighter Phenomenon (1944–1945)

The earliest coherent UAP wave of the modern era was the "Foo Fighter" phenomenon reported by Allied and Axis aircrews during the final phase of the Second World War. Bomber and fighter crews operating over Europe and the Pacific reported small, fast-moving luminous objects — spheres, balls of fire, and structured discs — that pursued aircraft without attacking. The reports originated independently from U.S., British, German, and Japanese sources, which effectively ruled out a single-nation experimental programme.

The term "Foo Fighter" originated from Allied usage and was drawn from a contemporary cartoon strip. The objects remain unexplained; contemporary hypotheses including plasma vortices, St. Elmo's Fire, and ball lightning do not account for all reported characteristics, particularly the apparent directed movement alongside aircraft.

### The 1947 Wave

The summer of 1947 produced the most consequential UAP event cluster of the decade. Kenneth Arnold's June 24 sighting near Mount Rainier, Washington — nine crescent-shaped objects moving in formation at estimated speeds far exceeding contemporary aircraft — triggered a national reporting cascade. Within weeks, hundreds of sightings were reported across the United States and internationally.

The July 1947 Roswell incident — in which the Army Air Force announced, then quickly retracted, the recovery of a "flying disc" — added a layer of official mystery that persisted for decades. The Air Force's response to this wave produced Project Sign in December 1947, the first formal governmental UAP investigation.

## 1950s: The Contactee Period and Institutional Scepticism

### The Contactee Movement

The early 1950s saw the emergence of the "contactee" phenomenon: individuals claiming sustained personal contact with extraterrestrial beings. George Adamski's 1953 account *Flying Saucers Have Landed* (co-authored with Desmond Leslie) described repeated encounters with benevolent beings from Venus and set the template for subsequent contactee narratives.

Unlike the pilot and military witnesses reporting anomalous objects, contactees typically reported personal relationships with human-appearing entities offering philosophical and spiritual teachings. The scientific and investigative community — including NICAP under Donald Keyhoe — largely rejected contactee claims as incompatible with the physical evidence base. However, the contactee movement significantly shaped public perception of UAP during this decade and attracted large audiences to speaking tours and books.

### Official Investigation and the Robertson Panel (1953)

In January 1953, the CIA convened the Robertson Panel — a scientific advisory committee under H.P. Robertson of the California Institute of Technology — to assess the national security implications of UFO reports. The panel reviewed approximately 75 cases over four days and concluded that UAP posed no direct threat but that the volume of public reports created a security risk by potentially overwhelming communication channels during a genuine attack. The panel recommended a debunking programme to reduce public interest.

The Robertson Panel recommendations exerted a lasting influence on Air Force and CIA posture toward UAP investigation, orienting official programmes toward debunking rather than analysis throughout the 1950s.

## 1960s: Mass Sighting Waves and Scientific Controversy

### The Early 1960s and Witness Credibility

The early 1960s produced a series of high-quality UAP cases that attracted scientific attention. The 1961 report by Betty and Barney Hill of an encounter near Lincoln, New Hampshire — including a period of missing time and subsequent hypnotic recall of an apparent abduction — was the first widely documented abduction narrative and became a reference case for subsequent encounters of this type.

The 1964 Socorro case — police officer Lonnie Zamora's observation of an egg-shaped craft with humanoid figures near Socorro, New Mexico — was investigated by Project Blue Book and J. Allen Hynek and remained officially classified as "unidentified." Physical trace evidence was documented contemporaneously.

### The 1966–1967 Wave and Swamp Gas

A major sighting wave across the United States in 1966–1967 forced a public response. A cluster of highly reported sightings in Michigan in March 1966 prompted the Air Force to dispatch J. Allen Hynek, who offered a provisional "swamp gas" explanation to the press. The response drew widespread ridicule and increased congressional pressure for a more credible investigation.

This pressure led to the Air Force commissioning the University of Colorado to conduct an independent scientific study, resulting in the Condon Report of 1969, which concluded that continued investigation was unlikely to produce scientific knowledge. Hynek publicly disputed the report's conclusions, and Blue Book was disbanded in December 1969.

## 1970s: The Abduction Narrative Era

### Emergence of Abduction Reports

The 1970s saw the systematic emergence and public attention to abduction narratives — cases in which witnesses reported being involuntarily taken aboard craft and subjected to examination or interaction. The 1975 Travis Walton case in Arizona, in which a logger disappeared for five days and was sought by law enforcement, attracted wide national attention. The polygraph testing of Walton and his fellow logging crew members was widely reported.

Researcher Budd Hopkins began collecting and publishing abduction cases in the late 1970s, culminating in *Missing Time* (1981) and *Intruders* (1987), which argued that alien abduction was a widespread and under-reported phenomenon. Hopkins's work was characterised by extensive use of hypnotic regression to recover suppressed memories — a methodology later subjected to significant scientific criticism.

### International Cases

The 1970s also produced major non-U.S. cases. The Frederick Valentich disappearance over Bass Strait, Australia in 1978 — in which a pilot reported an unidentified aircraft above him before his radio transmission cut off and he was never found — remains one of the most puzzling aviation UAP cases. The Trans-en-Provence case in France (1981, though reported in the 1970s cluster) produced ground traces that were analysed by GEPAN, yielding anomalous soil chemistry results.

## 1980s: Military Base Incidents and Official Encounters

### Rendlesham Forest (1980)

The December 1980 Rendlesham Forest incident — a series of encounters by USAF personnel at twin NATO bases in Suffolk, England — represents one of the most extensively documented military UAP cases in the literature. The Halt Memorandum, written by Deputy Base Commander Lieutenant Colonel Charles Halt and sent to the UK Ministry of Defence, documented the encounters and constitutes a rare piece of official military documentation of a UAP event by a named, senior officer.

### Cash-Landrum and Other CE-2 Cases

The Cash-Landrum incident of December 1980 in Texas — three witnesses reporting severe burns and health consequences after a close encounter with a large flame-emitting craft escorted by military helicopters — was among the most medically documented CE-2 cases of the decade.

The 1980s also saw a series of reports of UAP activity near nuclear missile installations, particularly within the United States and United Kingdom. Robert Hastings's research identified dozens of military personnel reporting UAP incursions at nuclear weapons storage and launch facilities, some associated with reported equipment anomalies.

## 1990s: Black Triangle Reports

The 1990s were characterised by a sharp increase in reports of large, silent, low-flying triangular or delta-shaped objects. The Belgium Black Triangle wave of 1989–1990 was the most extensively documented instance: over 13,000 witnesses, Belgian Air Force radar tracking and F-16 intercepts, and the full official release of investigation files.

Similar triangular objects were widely reported across the United States and United Kingdom throughout the 1990s. The Phoenix Lights event of March 13, 1997 — a massive V-shaped formation observed by thousands across Arizona — was the decade's most prominent U.S. case. The British author and researcher David Marler catalogued over 1,000 triangular UAP cases in his 2013 study *Triangular UFOs: An Estimate of the Situation*.

The triangular category generated sustained speculation about connections to classified aerospace programmes, particularly given reports of delta-wing test vehicles from facilities including the Nellis Test Range. No classified platform has been publicly acknowledged that accounts for all reported characteristics.

## 2000s: The Disclosure Activism Era

### The Disclosure Project (2001)

In May 2001, researcher Steven Greer organised a press conference at the National Press Club in Washington, D.C., at which over 20 individuals — including former military and intelligence personnel — testified to personal experience with UAP and claimed knowledge of government concealment programmes. The event was widely covered but provoked no official government response. Greer subsequently organised a classified briefing for Congressional staff.

The disclosure movement of the 2000s operated primarily through NGOs, public-records requests, and the cultivation of government and military whistleblowers. It created the cultural and institutional groundwork for the more significant 2017 disclosures.

### Military UAP Encounters Continue

The November 2004 Nimitz encounters — F/A-18 pilot David Fravor's intercept of the "Tic Tac" object off San Diego — occurred during a period of increased naval UAP activity that was not publicly known until 2017. The broader pattern of carrier-based UAP encounters during the 2000s only became visible with the declassification of the AATIP programme and the release of sensor footage.

## 2017–Present: The Sensor-Verified Era

### The 2017 Turning Point

The December 2017 *New York Times* article disclosing the Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP), accompanied by publication of the Nimitz FLIR1 video, constituted the most significant institutional shift in the history of UAP disclosure. For the first time, an officially confirmed, sensor-recorded UAP case — authenticated by the Department of Defense — entered mainstream public and policy discourse.

### Institutional Normalisation

The period from 2017 to the present has seen rapid institutionalisation:
- The UAP Task Force (2020) produced the first official public assessment since Project Blue Book
- The DoD officially released three sensor videos in April 2020
- NASA commissioned an independent scientific study (2022–2023)
- The All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office was established (2022)
- Congressional hearings with military witnesses and whistleblowers (2022–2023)

The contemporary era is distinguished from its predecessors by the presence of officially authenticated sensor data, named credible witnesses with active careers, and formal legislative and executive-branch institutional engagement. The question has shifted, for purposes of institutional analysis, from whether anomalous observations occur to what they represent.

## Interpretive Note

The wave pattern in UAP reporting does not straightforwardly indicate waves in the phenomenon itself. Reporting rates are heavily influenced by media attention, social permission to report, active investigation programmes, and the availability of recording technology. The contemporary increase in reports since 2017 may reflect genuine increases in reporting rates, changes in surveillance sensor density, or both.

What is consistent across the historical record is a residual of well-documented cases that have resisted conventional explanation after serious investigation in each major period. The nature of that residual remains the central unresolved question.
