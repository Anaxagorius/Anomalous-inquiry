UAP research is characterised by a set of foundational open questions that have persisted across decades of investigation and remain unresolved in the current literature. The following survey identifies the principal areas of genuine scientific uncertainty, distinguishing them from questions that have been closed by available evidence and from speculative areas where evidence is insufficient for analytical engagement.

## Nature of Propulsion

The most fundamental physical question in UAP research concerns how the objects — if they are objects — move. Reported flight characteristics including instantaneous acceleration, right-angle direction changes at high velocity, and trans-medium operation (air to water transitions without impact deceleration) appear to require either:

1. **Propulsion physics outside the current scientific consensus**: If the reports are accurate, the objects must interact with their environment in ways that conventional aerospace engineering cannot account for. The physical consequences of known propulsion modes (jet exhaust, rotor wash, sonic boom at supersonic velocity, atmospheric heating at hypersonic velocity) are consistently absent in high-quality UAP cases.

2. **Inertial management or field propulsion**: Some theoretical frameworks — including proposals associated with patents filed by engineer Salvatore Pais under NAVAIR — describe propulsion mechanisms based on manipulation of electromagnetic and gravitational fields that would, if realised, explain the absence of conventional propulsion signatures. These remain theoretical and are not represented in any publicly known tested technology.

3. **Sensor artefacts**: The alternative hypothesis is that the reported flight characteristics are artefacts of how data is collected, processed, and interpreted — radar velocity aliasing, FLIR parallax effects, misinterpretation of angular velocity as linear velocity. Systematic sensor artefact characterisation is a priority of current analytical work.

Determining which of these explanations applies to any given case requires more detailed sensor data, calibrated instrumentation, and multi-source confirmation than is available for most cases in the existing record. Data standardisation and purpose-built sensor arrays (as deployed by the Galileo Project) are the current approach to acquiring adequate data.

## Origin Attribution

If a subset of UAP are of artificial origin — manufactured objects rather than natural phenomena — the question of who or what manufactures them is entirely open. The principal hypotheses are:

### Foreign Adversary Surveillance

The UAPTF Preliminary Assessment and subsequent AARO reports acknowledge that some UAP may represent advanced surveillance platforms from foreign state actors. This hypothesis is taken seriously at the operational level, given the consistent reporting of UAP near U.S. naval operations. The difficulty is that no known foreign platform has been publicly identified that accounts for the most anomalous reported characteristics, particularly the simultaneous radar, visual, and electro-optical detections of objects displaying hypersonic manoeuvrability.

### Classified U.S. Programmes

A systematic alternative is that some reported UAP represent classified U.S. aerospace programmes whose performance has been observed without disclosure. The U-2, SR-71, and F-117 programmes each generated UAP reports before their public disclosure. The structural difficulty is that for this hypothesis to explain all high-quality military UAP cases, it would require programmes operating at performance levels substantially beyond known classified aerospace for many decades — and would require that these programmes were unknown to the military personnel reporting the encounters.

### Non-Human Origin

The non-human origin hypothesis — that some UAP represent technology produced by intelligence not originating in contemporary human civilisation — encompasses extraterrestrial, interdimensional, cryptoterrestrial, and related variants. It remains the most dramatic and least evidentially supported of the active hypotheses for any specific case, while being the most difficult to definitively exclude from the aggregate case record.

No analytical framework currently provides a basis for confident attribution. Intelligence attribution requires evidence of sufficient specificity and quality to rule out all alternative hypotheses — a standard that has not been met for any individual case in the open literature.

## Risk Assessment and Airspace Safety

One of the most practically urgent open questions is the airspace safety implications of UAP. Commander Ryan Graves has testified to near-miss incidents between naval aircraft and unidentified objects in restricted military airspace. The NARCAP technical literature documents multiple civilian aviation near-miss reports.

The specific questions requiring investigation include:

- **Frequency of encounters**: What is the actual rate of pilot UAP observations relative to formal reporting? Survey data suggests formal reports represent a small fraction of actual observations.
- **Encounter geometry and collision risk**: What are the spatial relationships between UAP and manned aircraft during encounter events?
- **System interference risk**: What is the mechanism of reported avionics interference, and how frequently does it occur?
- **Reporting infrastructure**: Is current reporting infrastructure adequate for characterising airspace safety risk, or does stigma-induced underreporting create systematic blind spots?

These questions are specifically amenable to conventional aviation safety methodology and do not require resolution of the broader origin question.

## Information Disclosure Policy

The extent, pace, and framework of official information disclosure about UAP represents an ongoing policy question with significant public interest implications. Several specific issues remain open:

### Classification and Access

A significant fraction of UAP-relevant documentation remains classified for reasons of intelligence source and method protection, operational security, or other national security grounds. The degree to which classification conceals programme-relevant information versus intelligence collection methods is not publicly known.

### Whistleblower Claims

Multiple individuals have made protected disclosures claiming knowledge of classified UAP-related programmes involving recovered technology or biological material. AARO's public reporting has not corroborated these claims. The unresolved tension between whistleblower assertions and official findings represents an open institutional question that has attracted sustained Congressional interest.

### International Coordination

UAP are a global phenomenon observed across national airspaces. The degree to which national investigations coordinate — particularly given the security classification concerns and different national frameworks — is unclear. The precedent of the COMETA Report suggests that multilateral scientific engagement is possible outside direct government coordination.

## Data Standardisation

A persistent problem across UAP research is the absence of consistent data standards. Different reporting systems, different sensor platforms, and different investigation methodologies produce data that is difficult to compare and aggregate. Specific standardisation gaps include:

- **Minimum reportable data fields**: No universal standard defines what information constitutes a complete case report
- **Sensor calibration documentation**: Reports of radar tracks, FLIR footage, or photographic imagery are of limited analytical value without calibration data for the specific sensor systems
- **Confidence and classification methodology**: Different organisations use different criteria for classifying a case as "unexplained" or "anomalous," making cross-study comparison unreliable
- **Multi-source data fusion**: Protocols for integrating radar, visual, electro-optical, acoustic, and other data streams in a single case have not been standardised

AARO's reporting framework and the NASA study's recommendations both address standardisation, but implementation is at an early stage.

## Cross-Domain Correlation

A recurring observation in the UAP literature is the apparent correlation between UAP activity and specific environmental or operational conditions:

- **Nuclear facility proximity**: Claims of elevated UAP activity near nuclear weapons storage, launch, and power generation facilities, documented in the work of Robert Hastings and corroborated by testimony from former military personnel
- **Active military exercise areas**: The Nimitz and Theodore Roosevelt encounters occurred during or near military training exercises; carrier strike group operations have produced a disproportionate fraction of high-quality modern UAP cases
- **Acoustic and seismic conditions**: Some researchers have proposed correlations between geological activity, electromagnetic field variations, and UAP sighting patterns
- **Temporal clustering**: UAP reports tend to cluster in time as well as space, suggesting either genuine concentrated activity or reporting cascade effects (where one report prompts others)

These correlation patterns, if systematically confirmed, would have significant implications for investigation methodology — they would allow deployment of monitoring systems in locations and during periods of elevated expected activity.

## Physical Evidence Recovery and Analysis

A small number of UAP cases in the literature involve physical material that was alleged to be associated with UAP encounters. This category of evidence, if genuine and documented, would be of extraordinary analytical value. Key open questions include:

- **The Sturrock panel's assessment**: The 1998 physical science review found that no physical material in the available evidence base had been definitively linked to UAP activity with adequate chain of custody
- **Alleged government material**: Various claims have been made by whistleblowers and researchers regarding recovered exotic materials in government possession. These claims have not been substantiated in the open literature. AARO has indicated it has not been presented with such material.
- **Isotopic analysis**: Proposed analytical methodology for assessing whether any alleged physical UAP residue — if recovered with proper chain of custody — could be attributed to non-terrestrial manufacturing would involve isotopic ratio analysis. No confirmed material has yet been subjected to peer-reviewed analysis in the open literature.

## The Galileo Project and Future Observational Science

The most significant current response to these open questions is the Galileo Project, founded by Harvard astronomer Avi Loeb in 2021. The project is building a global network of calibrated sensor arrays designed specifically for UAP observation, incorporating:

- Wide-field optical cameras
- Infrared sensors
- Radio receivers
- Seismometers
- Weather instrumentation

The intent is to produce purpose-collected, calibrated, multi-sensor data on UAP rather than to analyse events captured incidentally by sensors not designed for this purpose. This represents a methodological advance over the historical evidence base, which has depended almost entirely on sensor data acquired for other purposes.

The Galileo Project's first observatory was deployed at Harvard in 2023, and its initial results are being prepared for peer-reviewed publication.

## Methodological Priorities

The open questions above converge on a set of methodological priorities for UAP research in the near term:

1. **Standardised data collection**: Implement minimum data standards across reporting systems and sensor platforms
2. **Reduce stigma barriers**: Decrease reporting suppression through formal protections and normalisation of reporting in aviation and military contexts
3. **Purpose-built instrumentation**: Deploy sensor arrays specifically designed for UAP observation rather than relying on incidental capture
4. **Systematic null hypothesis exclusion**: Invest in rigorous sensor artefact characterisation, atmospheric modelling, and conventional object population characterisation to reduce false positive rates
5. **Cross-domain data integration**: Develop protocols for fusion of multi-sensor data streams in UAP case analysis
6. **International cooperation**: Establish frameworks for sharing case data across national programmes while managing classification concerns

The scientific community has sufficient evidence to justify these investments. It does not have sufficient evidence to reach firm conclusions about the nature, origin, or intent of the most anomalous reported phenomena.
