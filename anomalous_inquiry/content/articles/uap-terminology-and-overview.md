Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena (UAP) research concerns the systematic documentation, analysis, and interpretation of reports of objects or phenomena that cannot be identified as conventional aircraft, natural atmospheric events, or known sensor artefacts. The field has evolved substantially over decades of observation, official investigation, and declassification, transitioning from informal public interest to institutionalised scientific and governmental programmes.

## Terminology

The terminology in this domain has shifted across eras and institutional contexts.

**UFO (Unidentified Flying Object)** is the historic term introduced in U.S. Air Force usage during the late 1940s to denote aerial objects that resisted identification after investigation. Despite its popular association with extraterrestrial hypotheses, the term is definitionally neutral — it refers only to an absence of identification.

**UAP (Unidentified Aerial Phenomena)**, later expanded to **Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena**, is the current institutional term preferred by U.S. defence and intelligence agencies. The broader "anomalous" formulation reflects the multi-domain character of many reports — objects observed not only in the atmosphere but underwater, in near-space, and transitioning between domains.

**UAS-adjacent anomalies** refers to objects that may superficially resemble unmanned aerial systems but display performance characteristics inconsistent with any known commercial or military UAS platform.

**Trans-medium objects** describes reported objects observed traversing boundaries between air, water, and space without apparent mechanical or aerodynamic adaptation — a characteristic reported in several recent military sensor cases.

**Unidentified Submersible Objects (USOs)** denotes the underwater analogues of aerial UAP, reported in a subset of naval and coastal cases.

**Multi-domain Anomalous Phenomena (MDAP)** is a contemporary formulation encompassing the full range of aerial, sub-surface, and near-space anomalous observations in a unified analytical category.

## Phenomenological Categories

### Visual and Sensor-Based Observations

UAP are documented across a range of observational modalities:

- **Daylight sightings**: Visual observations in conditions of good visibility, permitting detailed shape and behaviour description
- **Nighttime light phenomena**: Luminous objects observed nocturnally, ranging from single point sources to complex formation lights
- **Radar-only detections**: Returns tracked by ground-based or airborne radar without corresponding visual confirmation
- **Radar-visual correlation**: Cases in which radar returns are simultaneously confirmed by direct visual observation — considered the highest evidential grade in the literature
- **Infrared (FLIR) observations**: Thermal imaging detections, particularly from military aircraft; several officially released videos fall in this category
- **Electro-optical sensor anomalies**: Anomalous returns from cameras, rangefinders, and tracking systems not attributable to standard sensor artefacts
- **Satellite detections**: Ambiguous or contextually anomalous objects recorded by earth observation and surveillance satellites

### Motion and Flight Characteristics

Across a wide variety of cases, reported objects display a coherent set of flight characteristics that distinguish them from conventional aerospace platforms:

- **Hypersonic velocity without sonic boom**: Estimated speeds in excess of Mach 5 in atmospherically dense environments, without associated acoustic signatures
- **Sudden acceleration and deceleration**: Instantaneous velocity changes inconsistent with known propulsion or inertial physics
- **Right-angle turns**: Direction changes without banking or deceleration
- **Hovering without visible propulsion**: Stable position maintenance with no visible rotor, jet exhaust, or control surface activity
- **Trans-medium movement**: Seamless transition from aerial to aquatic environments and back
- **Sudden disappearance**: Dematerialisation from sensor coverage without trajectory extrapolation
- **Formation flight**: Multiple objects maintaining complex relative positions

These characteristics — often grouped as the "five observables" in recent U.S. defence analysis — are not individually unique to UAP, but their combination in single events has so far resisted conventional explanation.

## Methodological Considerations

UAP research has historically suffered from a fragmented evidence base: individual case files without standardised formats, witnesses reluctant to report due to institutional stigma, and a lack of systematic sensor instrumentation at collection sites. Contemporary initiatives — including the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) and the NASA UAP study — aim to address these deficiencies through data standardisation, multi-sensor protocols, and reduced reporting barriers.

The fundamental methodological challenge is distinguishing genuine anomalies from the much larger population of misidentified conventional objects, natural phenomena, and sensor artefacts. Rigorous UAP analysis requires not only positive case documentation but systematic characterisation of the null hypothesis space.

## Classification Note

UAP documentation records unidentified phenomena. Classification as "UAP" reflects an absence of identification, not a determination of origin, intent, or nature.
