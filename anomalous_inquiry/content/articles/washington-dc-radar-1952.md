On the nights of July 19–20 and July 26–27, 1952, unidentified radar returns appeared over restricted airspace above Washington D.C. — including the Capitol, the White House, and the flight path of National Airport. The incidents were simultaneously tracked by multiple independent radar stations, visually observed by pilots and ground personnel, and generated the largest single press conference held by the U.S. Air Force since World War II.

## The July 19–20 Incidents

At approximately 11:40 p.m. on July 19, air traffic controllers at Washington National Airport (now Reagan National) detected a cluster of seven unusual targets on their radar scopes moving at between 100 and 130 mph — within the normal aircraft range — but then accelerating suddenly to speeds estimated at 7,000 to 8,000 mph. The objects manoeuvred in restricted airspace over the Capitol and the White House.

The targets were simultaneously tracked by radar at Andrews Air Force Base. When a controller at National called Andrews to report the anomalous returns, Andrews controllers confirmed they were also tracking unidentified objects in the area.

At approximately 3:00 a.m., an airborne F-94 Starfire interceptor was scrambled from New Castle AFB in Delaware. By the time the aircraft arrived over the capital, the objects had disappeared from radar. They reappeared after the interceptor departed — a pattern that recurred across both weekends.

Visual observations were reported by multiple witnesses on the ground and from commercial airliner crews. Capital Airlines flight Captain Casey Pierman reported six objects over the Herndon, Virginia area, describing them as "like falling stars without trails."

## The July 26–27 Incidents

The second week's events were more intense and more thoroughly documented. At approximately 9:08 p.m. on July 26, National Airport radar again tracked multiple unidentified objects in the restricted zone. Andrews AFB confirmed the targets. Over the following hours, objects were tracked performing rapid direction changes and accelerations, and visual observations were reported from the ground.

Two F-94 interceptors were scrambled. One pilot, Captain John McHugg, reported chasing four lights that he was unable to close with despite full military power. A second pilot reported that the lights "closed in around him" before dispersing.

Radar operators noted that the objects seemed aware of the interceptors' approach, disappearing from the scope when aircraft neared and reappearing after they departed.

## Project Blue Book Investigation

The incidents prompted an immediate Project Blue Book investigation led by Captain Edward Ruppelt, then chief of the USAF's UFO investigation unit. Ruppelt found the cases compelling and noted the unusual behaviour of the radar returns.

The official Air Force explanation, provided at the press conference of July 29 by Brigadier General John Samford, attributed the radar returns to temperature inversions — layers of warm air over cool air that can cause radar waves to refract and create anomalous ground returns. Samford acknowledged the visual observations but attributed them to the same meteorological cause.

The temperature inversion explanation has been critically assessed by subsequent investigators. While anomalous propagation is a documented radar phenomenon, the simultaneous confirmation by multiple independent radar systems at different locations, the corroboration by airborne pilot observations, and the responsiveness of the targets to interceptor activity are not characteristics typical of refraction artefacts.

## Significance

The Washington D.C. incidents produced the first presidential-level attention to the UAP question in the modern era. President Truman's military aide was in contact with Ruppelt during the July 26–27 incidents. The events contributed directly to the CIA's Robertson Panel of January 1953, which recommended a public debunking strategy and increased surveillance of civilian UFO organisations.

The cases are notable for their multi-sensor character — independent radar confirmation, airborne visual, and ground visual observation in simultaneous combination — and for occurring over the most heavily surveilled airspace in the United States.

## Assessment

The Washington D.C. radar incidents remain among the most thoroughly documented UAP cases from the early modern period. The officially proffered explanation has not satisfied a number of independent investigators, and the case file contains substantive evidentiary inconsistencies. The incidents are documented here without adjudication of the contested explanations.
