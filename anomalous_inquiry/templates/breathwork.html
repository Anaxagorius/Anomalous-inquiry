💨 Breathwork States

Physiology of Altered Breathing

Breathing is the only autonomic function routinely subject to voluntary control, a fact that makes it a uniquely accessible lever for altering consciousness. The physiological mechanisms by which altered breathing patterns affect the brain are well characterised: hypocapnia — reduced CO₂ in the blood produced by overbreathing — causes vasoconstriction of cerebral blood vessels, reducing cerebral blood flow. This produces sensory disturbances including tingling in the extremities and face, visual changes, auditory alterations, and at sufficient intensity, altered awareness and hallucination-like phenomena. Simultaneously, the blood pH rises (respiratory alkalosis), altering neuronal excitability and triggering spontaneous electrical activity in sensory cortices.

The physiological account, however, does not fully explain the content of breathwork states. The specific imagery, emotional material, somatic phenomena, and occasional apparently anomalous information arising in breathwork sessions show patterns — biographical specificity, coherence with traditional cosmological schemas, and the recurring entity encounter — that require additional explanatory frameworks beyond simple cerebral hypoperfusion.

Holotropic Breathwork: Grof's Method

Holotropic Breathwork was developed by Stanislav Grof and Christina Grof in the late 1970s, following the scheduling of LSD and subsequent termination of legal psychedelic research. Grof, who had conducted thousands of LSD therapy sessions and developed a comprehensive cartography of non-ordinary consciousness from that data, recognised that sustained faster and deeper breathing could reproduce much of the phenomenological range of the LSD session — including biographical trauma material, perinatal experiences, and transpersonal states — without pharmacological intervention.

The method involves: extended period (typically two to three hours) of accelerated, deeper-than-normal breathing to evocative music in a supported setting; a trained facilitator and individual sitter; bodywork if significant physical material arises; and a mandala drawing and verbal integration process afterwards. The Grof Transpersonal Training (GTT) certifies practitioners worldwide, and the method has been offered in clinical, educational, and retreat contexts globally for over four decades.

COEX Systems and Perinatal Matrices

Grof's empirical mapping of non-ordinary consciousness, derived from over 4,000 LSD sessions and subsequent breathwork facilitation, identified two major experiential domains beyond the ordinary biographical range. The first are COEX systems (systems of condensed experience): organised clusters of biographical memories sharing a common emotional theme and physical feeling tone, which surface as integrated gestalt experiences rather than individual recollections. Resolving a COEX system in a session produces lasting psychological change — suggesting that the altered state provides access to a layer of psychological organisation below the level of ordinary verbal recall.

The second domain is the perinatal matrices (BPM I–IV): four experiential constellations corresponding to the stages of biological birth — undisturbed intrauterine existence (BPM I), onset of contractions with no exit (BPM II), transition through the birth canal (BPM III), and birth completion (BPM IV). Grof found that subjects regularly reached these matrices at sufficient depth of altered state, and that the phenomenology was precisely consistent with the relevant birth stage even when no biographical birth memory was consciously available. BPM II generates the most distressing material: agony, claustrophobia, meaninglessness, and no-exit despair. BPM IV resolution produces the most profound experiences of death-rebirth, cosmic union, and liberation.

LSD versus Breathwork: Phenomenological Parallels

Grof's clinical experience across both modalities convinced him that the phenomenological map of non-ordinary consciousness was essentially identical whether the state was produced by LSD, holotropic breathing, or other means. Subjects who had sessions with both modalities routinely reported that the same unresolved psychological material surfaced in both — the same biographical scenes, the same perinatal sequences, the same transpersonal motifs — differing mainly in intensity and controllability. This cross-modal consistency is one of the strongest arguments that these experiences reflect genuine psychological and potentially ontological structure rather than pharmacological artefact.

The transpersonal domain accessible in deep breathwork — which includes identification with other species, historical periods, mythological figures, and cosmic processes — also shows the same entity encounter phenomenology documented with DMT and in shamanic traditions. Beings of light, guides, deities, and occasionally non-human intelligences appear in the breathwork space with an apparent independence and consistency across unrelated experiencers that Grof found difficult to account for purely in terms of individual unconscious content. He eventually concluded, with characteristic scientific caution, that the transpersonal domain implies a non-local dimension of consciousness that cannot be reduced to individual brain function.

Wim Hof Method and Pranayama

The Wim Hof Method — popularised by Dutch athlete Wim Hof ("The Iceman") and subject to increasing clinical study — involves cycles of 30–40 rapid deep breaths followed by breath retention after exhalation, repeated for multiple rounds. Hof's controlled study at Radboud University Medical Center (Kox et al., 2014, PNAS) demonstrated that trained practitioners could voluntarily regulate their autonomic nervous system and innate immune response — previously considered entirely involuntary — through the combined breathwork, cold exposure, and meditation protocol. Practitioners showed significantly reduced inflammatory cytokine responses and fewer flu symptoms after endotoxin challenge compared to untrained controls.

The altered states produced by Wim Hof breathing in naive practitioners include intense tingling, spontaneous emotional releases, visual phenomena including geometric patterns and brightness, and occasionally profound states of clarity or ego dissolution — effects that significantly overlap with holotropic breathwork phenomenology despite the different cultural framing. The common physiological mechanism of hypocapnia and respiratory alkalosis is the likely mediating pathway, but the specific content and emotional resolution often reported suggest that psychological and possibly transpersonal material is also being processed.

Prāṇāyāma — the systematic breath regulation practices of the yoga tradition, including kumbhaka (breath retention), bhastrikā (bellows breath), nāḍīśodhana (alternate nostril breathing), and kapālabhāti (skull-shining breath) — constitute the oldest systematic breathwork tradition, with roots in texts dating to at least 500 BCE. The Yoga Sūtras describe prāṇāyāma as causing the "inner light" to manifest and as producing fitness for the practice of pratyāhāra (sensory withdrawal) — confirming that altered states were both expected and sought as part of the pranayamic process.

Scientific Studies and Open Questions

Research on holotropic breathwork specifically has been limited by difficulty in obtaining research funding and institutional support for work in this area. Several pilot and observational studies have documented psychological benefits including reduced anxiety, increased emotional well-being, and reduction of PTSD and addiction symptoms. A systematic review by Brewerton et al. (2012) and subsequent studies have found significant improvements in measures of psychological distress. However, randomised controlled trials with appropriate comparator conditions are lacking.

The most important open question — whether breathwork can produce genuinely anomalous information acquisition, as individual case reports suggest — remains unstudied under controlled conditions. Reports of accurate precognitive, telepathic, and remote perception material arising in breathwork sessions appear in Grof's clinical literature and in practitioner accounts, but have not been systematically investigated. The intersection of breathwork phenomenology with the physics of consciousness and the Phenomenon remains, as with other altered state modalities, at the frontier of what science can currently address.
