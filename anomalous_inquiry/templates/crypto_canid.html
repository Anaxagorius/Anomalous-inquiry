🐺 Category II — Canid-Type Cryptids

Canid cryptids are reported wolf- or dog-like entities distinguished from known species by anomalous size, morphology, behaviour, or a combination thereof. The category excludes lycanthropic or paranormal framings, treating all reports zoologically: as potential evidence for unknown canid taxa, relict species, or extreme individual variation. Reports cluster in Western Europe, North America, and parts of Africa, with the most extensively documented cases carrying physical, historical, or institutional evidence beyond eyewitness testimony alone.

Beast of Gévaudan (France, 1764–1767)

The Beast of Gévaudan represents one of the most thoroughly documented cryptozoological cases in history, investigated at the time by the French Crown and generating an institutional record spanning thousands of pages. Between June 1764 and June 1767, an unknown predator killed approximately 88–102 people (estimates vary; at least 60 deaths are confirmed in parish records) in the Margeride mountains of what is now the Lozère department of southern France. The attacks were characterised by a consistent method: decapitation or severe throat wounds with other body parts largely intact — a pattern inconsistent with wolf predation, which typically targets the flanks and abdomen of livestock but rarely achieves the systematic neck-targeting of human victims.

Multiple descriptions of the Beast from surviving witnesses describe an entity larger than a known wolf, reddish-brown in colour with a dark dorsal stripe, a wide head with a blunt muzzle, a long tail with a tufted end, and legs described as unusually long in proportion to the body. Contemporary illustrations, while stylised, are consistent across independent accounts. A wolf killed by hunter Jean Chastel on June 19, 1767, is credited with ending the attacks. This wolf was exhibited to Louis XV's court, but its preserved body was lost after decomposing during the journey to Versailles and no morphological analysis was ever completed. Zoological hypotheses for the Beast include a surviving dire wolf (Aenocyon dirus), a hyena released from a private collection, a wolf-dog hybrid of extraordinary size, or a misidentified striped hyena. The institutional record makes the Gévaudan case a uniquely rigorous historical cryptid investigation.

Quadrupedal Dogman

The Dogman designation refers to a category of North American canid cryptid reports in which the entity is described as a bipedal or transitionally bipedal canid — a wolf-like body carried upright on hindlimbs with forelimbs used for balance or occasionally for quadrupedal movement. Reports concentrate in Michigan, Wisconsin, and the Great Lakes region, though accounts exist from across the continental United States. The term was popularised when radio broadcaster Steve Cook wrote and performed a novelty song about Michigan Dogman sightings in 1987, subsequently receiving hundreds of letters from listeners claiming their own encounters.

The physiological plausibility of a bipedal canid is contested. Canid skeletal structure is not optimised for bipedal locomotion, though several YouTube videos of domestic dogs and foxes walking bipedally due to congenital forelimb absence demonstrate that the gait is not anatomically impossible. Some researchers interpret Dogman reports as misidentification of large upright bears; others, particularly Linda Godfrey — a journalist who has investigated and documented Wisconsin and Michigan reports since the 1991 "Beast of Bray Road" case — argue the consistent detail of canid facial morphology, forward-facing eyes, and articulated ears distinguishes these reports from bear encounters. Godfrey's books (The Beast of Bray Road, 2003; American Monsters, 2014) constitute the primary compiled documentation of the Dogman category.

Shunka Warakin

The Shunka Warakin — a term from the Ioway people meaning roughly "carries off dogs" — is a large wolf- or hyena-like predator reported from the northern Great Plains of North America, notable for the existence of a physical specimen taken in Montana in the 1880s and preserved, with gaps, to the present day. The original animal was killed near Henry Lake, Montana, by Israel Hutchins and reportedly mounted by a taxidermist. The mount passed through several private collections before being rediscovered at the Idaho Museum of Natural History in 1995 by researcher Lance Foster and later documented by Ross MacPhee of the American Museum of Natural History. The mount displays features inconsistent with known wolf, coyote, or hyena — a distinctive sloping spine, large size, and unusual limb proportions — though no formal genetic analysis has been completed. The specimen is widely cited as one of the few alleged cryptid physical specimens with a documented chain of custody.

Onza (Mexico)

The Onza is a large, slender feline-canid hybrid type reported from the Sierra Madre Occidental in northwestern Mexico, historically distinct in indigenous and colonial Spanish accounts from both the mountain lion and the jaguar. A specimen shot in Sinaloa in January 1986 by rancher Aruro Lopez was retrieved by researcher J. Richard Greenwell of the International Society of Cryptozoology and biologist Troy Best. The carcass was photographed and tissue samples taken for genetic analysis by Dr. Howard Quigley. Results published in 1996 identified the specimen as a morphologically unusual puma (Puma concolor), though it displayed limb proportions and skeletal features outside the known range of the species. Whether this represents extreme individual variation or a distinct regional subspecies remains unresolved.

Waheela & Andean Wolf

The Waheela is reported from the Northwest Territories of Canada, particularly the Nahanni Valley (associated with a long history of anomalous deaths and disappearances), described as a large, bear-like wolf with splayed toes and a broad, flattened head unlike the typical conical wolf skull. Researcher Ivan T. Sanderson proposed the Waheela might represent a surviving Amphicyon or amphicyonid "bear-dog" — a group of large Miocene carnivores — though no physical evidence beyond tracks and witness accounts has been recovered. The Andean Wolf (proposed as Dasycyon hagenbecki) is based on a large pelt purchased in Buenos Aires in 1927 by Lorenz Hagenbeck. Zoologist Dr. Ingo Krumbiegel examined the pelt and concluded it belonged to a wolf-type carnivore significantly larger than the known maned wolf (Chrysocyon brachyurus) and morphologically distinct from it. No specimen beyond the pelt has been documented.
