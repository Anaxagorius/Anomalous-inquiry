🐆 Category III — Feline Cryptids

Feline cryptids encompass large or anomalous cats reported from regions where no confirmed wild big cat population exists, as well as alleged survival of extinct felid species. The category is notable for the relative quantity of physical evidence — kill sites, livestock remains, photographs, plaster casts, and in the British case, a substantial institutional record — compared with many other cryptid categories. Several alleged cryptid felids have subsequently been confirmed as known species via genetic analysis of scat or physical specimens, lending the category a degree of zoological credibility.

British Big Cats (ABCs — Alien Big Cats)

The Alien Big Cat (ABC) phenomenon refers to reported large felids — typically black leopard-type animals (Panthera pardus melanistic morph), pumas (Puma concolor), and occasionally lion-type animals — observed and photographed across rural Britain, most densely in Devon, Cornwall, Scotland, and Wales. Britain has no native wild big cat; the last confirmed British large predator was the lynx, which was hunted to extinction approximately 1,300 years ago.

The institutional record for British ABCs is substantial. In 1976, the Dangerous Wild Animals Act was enacted, requiring licences for exotic pet ownership including large cats. It is widely acknowledged that some owners released animals illegally rather than pay licensing or housing fees, providing a credible mechanism for ABC presence in the 1970s–1980s. Whether any such released population has persisted and reproduced across five decades remains the central question. A 2011 analysis by the British Big Cats Society estimated several hundred sightings per year from credible witnesses including farmers, police officers, and wildlife professionals.

Physical evidence includes livestock kills with bite and claw pattern analysis by felid experts, photographed paw casts, and several DNA analyses. A 2014 study by Dr. Ross Barnett at Bangor University analysed a large cat hair sample from Gloucestershire; initial results suggested a leopard match, though subsequent analysis introduced uncertainty. A frozen cat killed in Caithness, Scotland, in 1980 (the "Kellas Cat") was identified as a hybrid between a domestic cat and a Scottish wildcat — an unexpected and significant discovery — raising the possibility that some sightings attributed to ABCs may involve large hybrid felines of a different kind.

Beast of Bodmin Moor & Beast of Exmoor

The Beast of Bodmin Moor (Cornwall) is among the most reported British ABCs, with several thousand claims on record. In 1995, the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food conducted an official investigation concluding there was "no verifiable evidence" of a large wild cat on Bodmin Moor; this conclusion was based primarily on the absence of a confirmed kill attributable to a big cat rather than on absence of evidence generally. The report did not address the photographic and cast evidence. Shortly after the report's release, a boy found a leopard skull on the banks of the River Fowey; examination by the Natural History Museum confirmed it was a genuine leopard skull but that it had been imported as part of a leopard-skin rug and showed signs of having been stored, not recently killed in the wild.

The Beast of Exmoor (Devon/Somerset border) was the subject of a Royal Marines surveillance operation in 1983 following intensive livestock predation on Exmoor farms — over 100 sheep killed over a 12-week period by an unknown predator. The Marines deployed in the area but failed to sight or capture the animal. Farmers reported paw prints considerably larger than a domestic cat and bite marks on carcasses consistent with a large felid's killing bite to the back of the neck. The formal military operation represents an unusual degree of institutional engagement with the ABC phenomenon.

Marozi (Spotted Lion, Kenya)

The Marozi is a reported spotted lion described from the highland forest zone of Kenya, particularly the Aberdare Range, at elevations above 10,000 feet where typical African lions do not range. Unlike the African lion, which is plain-coated in adults, the Marozi is described with spots persisting into adulthood. Four specimens were shot by farmer Michael Trent on the Aberdare slopes in 1931; two skins were preserved and submitted to the British Natural History Museum, where they were examined and described by zoologist R.I. Pocock, who noted the spotted pattern was anomalous and speculated it might represent either a highland subspecies or a lion–leopard hybrid. The skins are still held at the NHM London and have been analysed genetically; results have not resolved whether the Marozi is a distinct subspecies, a hybrid, or individual variation. Field naturalist Kenneth Gandar Dower organised a dedicated expedition in 1936 to locate the Marozi and recorded local accounts but made no sighting.

Queensland Tiger (Australia)

The Queensland Tiger is reported from the Cape York Peninsula and northeastern Queensland rainforests, described as a dog-sized or cat-sized striped carnivore with a feline face, dog-like feet, and a long stiff tail — a morphology unlike any known Australian mammal. Indigenous Australians in the region have long described such an animal. Some researchers have proposed this is a surviving marsupial lion (Thylacoleo carnifex) of a smaller form, a surviving Palorchestes (a large browsing marsupial), or more commonly a surviving thylacinid of a different species from the Tasmanian Tiger. No specimen has been recovered from Queensland.

Sabertooth Survival Hypotheses

Reports of large, long-fanged felids from several regions of South America and central Africa have prompted hypotheses about surviving sabertooth-type cats. In South America, the Yaguaru and Tigre de montaña of Bolivian and Peruvian indigenous accounts describe a large cat with exceptionally long upper canines. Ivan T. Sanderson collected several such accounts and proposed a surviving Smilodon or related machairodont was possible given the limited exploration of Amazonian headwaters. Palaeontologically, Smilodon populator is known from South American fossil sites as recently as approximately 10,000 years ago — not impossibly remote for a relic population in a large, underpopulated region. No physical evidence has been recovered.
