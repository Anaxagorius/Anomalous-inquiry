🪞 Dissociation and Anomalous Experience

Definition and Spectrum

Dissociation describes disruptions in the normal integration of consciousness, memory, identity, emotion, and perception — the ordinarily seamless weave of subjective experience. Dissociative phenomena exist on a wide spectrum:

  • Normal range: highway hypnosis (arriving at a destination with no conscious memory of the drive), absorption in reading or music, daydreaming
  • Moderate: depersonalization (feeling detached from one's own body or thoughts), derealization (feeling that surroundings are unreal)
  • Pathological: Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID, formerly Multiple Personality Disorder), dissociative amnesia, dissociative fugue

The Dissociative Experiences Scale (DES), developed by Eve Carlson and Frank Putnam, provides a validated 28-item measure of dissociative tendencies across the absorption, amnesia, and depersonalization/derealization dimensions. High DES scores are consistently associated with elevated rates of reported anomalous experiences across independent samples.

Historical Foundations

Pierre Janet (1859–1947) was the principal 19th-century theorist of dissociation. Working at the Salpêtrière hospital in the 1880s, Janet proposed that psychological trauma could cause the splitting of the personality into fixed "subconscious" systems operating in parallel with normal waking consciousness. His concept of "disaggregation" anticipated modern dissociation theory. William James engaged extensively with dissociation in Principles of Psychology (1890), coining the concept of the "secondary self" as an autonomous mental system beneath the threshold of ordinary awareness.

Fantasy-Proneness and Anomalous Experience

Psychologists Sheryl Wilson and Theodore Barber (1983) identified a population of "fantasy-prone" individuals — approximately 4% of the general population — characterised by deep absorption in fantasy and imagination, vivid hallucinatory experiences in various contexts, and high rates of reported paranormal, psychic, and anomalous experiences. Fantasy-proneness is moderately correlated with dissociativity and strongly correlated with anomalous belief and experience reporting.

The relationship between fantasy-proneness and genuinely anomalous experience is debated. Keith Basterfield and Robert Bartholomew have argued that much of the alien abduction literature can be explained by fantasy-prone individuals generating vivid, convincing false memories. Others, including John Mack at Harvard, argued that the depth and detail of abduction accounts, and the distress of experiencers, warrant investigation beyond a simple psychological dismissal.

Dissociation and Psi Research

Several research groups have explored whether high dissociativity correlates with enhanced psi performance in laboratory settings. Harrison and Thalbourne found positive correlations between DES scores and performance in forced-choice psi tasks in some studies. Parra and Argibay at the Institute for Psychical Research in Buenos Aires reported that highly dissociative subjects outperformed low-dissociativity subjects in ganzfeld-type sessions. The effect has not been large or consistently replicated, but the trend across independent labs is noted.

Michael Thalbourne's concept of transliminality — a tendency for psychic material to cross the threshold between the unconscious and conscious — predicts both anomalous experience reporting and psi performance. Thalbourne's Transliminality Scale loads on fantasy-proneness, magical ideation, and mystical experience, as well as dissociativity. Highly transliminal individuals report more anomalous experiences of all kinds and show modest psi advantages in some laboratory paradigms.

DID and Anomalous Cognition

Dissociative Identity Disorder presents phenomena of particular interest to consciousness researchers. In some documented DID cases, different alters appear to have distinct physiological profiles — different allergic responses, different visual acuity, different EEG signatures — despite sharing a single body. Some case reports also describe apparent information transfer between alters without normal sensory channels: alter A acquiring information available only to alter B through means that have not been accounted for by standard memory models. These cases, while anecdotal and methodologically difficult, suggest that DID may provide a unique window on the relationship between consciousness and identity.

Temporal Lobe Lability and Boundary Deficit

Neurologist Michael Persinger proposed that anomalous experiences — including sensed presences, mystical states, and apparent psi impressions — are mediated by microseizure-like activity in the temporal lobe, particularly the right hemisphere. Individuals with high "temporal lobe lability" report more anomalous experiences in daily life and show different response patterns to weak transcerebral magnetic fields in laboratory settings. This neurobiological hypothesis is distinct from — but overlapping with — the dissociativity/transliminality framework.

Stanislav Grof argued that depersonalisation and derealization, when arising in non-ordinary states, should be understood as potentially meaningful spiritual emergencies rather than pathology — a conceptual framework that bridges the clinical and transpersonal literature and has influenced how extreme dissociative states are managed in integrative mental health settings.
