🔱 Kundalini & Energy States

Kundalini: Origins and Conceptual Framework

The concept of kundalinī — literally "coiled one" in Sanskrit — originates within the Tantric traditions of Hinduism and has been elaborated across Shaiva Tantra, Shakta traditions, Kashmir Shaivism, and the Nāth siddha lineages. The kundalini is described as a latent potential energy residing at the base of the spine (mūlādhāra chakra), symbolised as a serpent coiled three-and-a-half times around the central channel (suṣumnā nāḍī). When this energy is activated — through practice, grace, or spontaneous event — it ascends through the seven primary chakras (energy centres) aligned along the spinal axis, producing successively higher states of consciousness as it rises. The full ascent to the crown centre (sahasrāra chakra) is identified with samādhi and liberation.

The chakra system — mūlādhāra (root), svādhiṣṭhāna (sacral), maṇipūra (solar plexus), anāhata (heart), viśuddha (throat), ājñā (brow), and sahasrāra (crown) — is not a biological claim in the ordinary anatomical sense but a map of the quality of consciousness associated with different levels of activation and integration. Western practitioners have debated whether chakras have any physical correlate; a small literature suggests correspondences with neurological plexuses and endocrine glands, though these anatomical mappings are post-hoc and contested.

Phenomenology of Awakening

Kundalini awakening — whether gradual or spontaneous — is one of the most physically dramatic of all altered state phenomena. Commonly reported physical experiences include:

  • Internal heat: intense burning sensations moving upward along the spine, sometimes experienced as waves or surges
  • Vibration: buzzing, trembling, or electrical sensations throughout the body, particularly the spine and head
  • Internal light: perceptions of brilliant light inside the head or body, sometimes described as illuminating the visual field even with eyes closed
  • Involuntary movements: spontaneous kriyas — bodily movements, postures, mudras, or vocalizations arising without conscious volition
  • Bliss and terror alternating: states of profound ecstasy and expansion alternating with terror, dissolution of self, and overwhelming sensory intensity
  • Psychic openings: sudden perceptions of others' thoughts, presences, or events beyond ordinary sensory range

Gopi Krishna: First-Person Testimony

The Kashmiri mystic and writer Gopi Krishna (1903–1984) produced the most detailed first-person account of spontaneous kundalini awakening in the modern literature. After years of disciplined daily meditation, he experienced an abrupt and overwhelming awakening at age 34 that was initially terrifying: a roaring current of energy ascending his spine, followed by dissolution of ordinary sensory awareness and replacement by a field of brilliant, vibrating luminosity. He subsequently spent years in an unstable oscillation between states of ecstasy, terror, and physical suffering — requiring dietary adjustments and sustained practice to achieve stable integration.

Krishna's account, published in Kundalini: The Evolutionary Energy in Man (1967) with commentary by physicist James Hillman, was the first major work to bring the phenomenon to Western scientific and psychological attention. He proposed that kundalini represents an actual biological process — the activation of a dormant evolutionary mechanism involving the nervous system — and that the subjective phenomenology is the experiential correlate of this physiological transformation. His later work elaborated a theory of kundalini as the driver of all genuine spiritual experience and creative genius across cultures and history.

Stanislav Grof and Spiritual Emergency

Stanislav Grof, the Czech psychiatrist and consciousness researcher who developed holotropic breathwork, encountered kundalini phenomena repeatedly in his clinical work — both in LSD-assisted therapy sessions and in breathwork contexts — and recognised them as part of a broader category he termed spiritual emergency. A spiritual emergency, as defined by Grof and his wife Christina Grof (who herself experienced a dramatic kundalini awakening during childbirth), is a crisis of spiritual opening that produces dramatic psychological and physical symptomatology — and that is liable to misdiagnosis as psychotic breakdown by a psychiatric system that lacks categories for transformative altered states.

The Spiritual Emergence Network (SEN), founded by Christina Grof in 1980, provides support and referral for individuals undergoing these experiences. The differential diagnosis between kundalini awakening and psychiatric pathology is non-trivial: both can involve perceptual disturbances, intense emotional states, fragmentation of the ordinary self, and confusion about the nature of reality. The key clinical indicators favouring spiritual emergency include: a context of intensive practice or other precipitating catalyst, the presence of bliss and meaning amid the distress, the coherence of the phenomenology with traditional descriptions, and the individual's ability to maintain some metacognitive perspective on the state.

Lee Sannella's Clinical Work

Lee Sannella, a California psychiatrist and ophthalmologist, conducted the first systematic clinical investigation of kundalini cases in Western patients, reported in Kundalini — Psychosis or Transcendence? (1976, revised 1987). Drawing on over 70 case histories, Sannella described a characteristic pattern he called the physio-kundalini cycle: sensations beginning in the feet or spine and ascending, eventually producing alterations in visual and auditory perception, involuntary movements, and resolution in states of expanded awareness. He consistently found that patients whose symptoms were treated as psychiatric illness worsened, while those who were supported through the process with appropriate understanding generally emerged with enhanced psychological integration and functioning.

Kundalini, Psi, and Anomalous Phenomena

The kundalini literature consistently records enhanced anomalous perceptual capacities following awakening. Gopi Krishna reported persistent experiences of clairvoyance, precognition, and what he described as access to a vast reservoir of transpersonal knowledge — experiences he interpreted as the natural epistemological consequences of operating in an expanded state of consciousness. Survey data from the International Association for Near-Death Studies (IANDS) and other experiencer research groups have noted significant overlap between kundalini awakening phenomenology and NDE aftereffects, including increased psi experiences, electromagnetic sensitivity, and heightened intuition.

Particularly striking are documented reports of kundalini-related phenomena appearing in the context of close UAP encounters. Multiple witness accounts describe sensations during or after proximity to anomalous aerial objects that match the kundalini signature precisely: sudden intense heat moving up the spine, brilliant internal light, involuntary bodily movements, and a persistent state of heightened sensory acuity and psychic receptivity in the days and weeks following the encounter. John Keel documented cases in which witnesses to bright orb phenomena in particular reported these somatic effects. Whether this reflects a direct physical or electromagnetic interaction with the body, a psychobiologically induced awakening mediated by proximity to the phenomenon, or a common neurological pathway through which extreme encounters express themselves remains entirely open.

Research into the electromagnetic body — bioelectrical fields measurable at the skin surface and in the surrounding space — has occasionally been proposed as a bridge between the kundalini energy model and neuroscience. A small literature on biophoton emission from biological systems has been cited in this context, though the connections remain speculative. More robustly, the autonomic nervous system and endocrine correlates of kundalini phenomenology — the surges of heat, the altered breathing patterns, the dramatically altered arousal states — are areas where basic neurophysiological measurement is in principle possible, if culturally and methodologically challenging to carry out.
