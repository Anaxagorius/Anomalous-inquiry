🌉 Liminal & Between States

Victor Turner and the Anthropology of Liminality

The concept of liminality — from the Latin limen, threshold — was developed by the Belgian folklorist Arnold van Gennep in his 1909 study of rites of passage, and elaborated into a rich theoretical framework by British anthropologist Victor Turner in his fieldwork among the Ndembu of Zambia and subsequent theoretical works including The Ritual Process (1969). Van Gennep identified three phases in all rites of passage: separation (detachment from ordinary social position), liminality (the threshold period during which the initiate is "betwixt and between" — neither what they were nor what they will become), and reincorporation (return to social life with a new identity and status).

Turner's contributions deepened and expanded this framework. He described the liminal phase as characterised by communitas — an intense, egalitarian social bond among liminal individuals stripped of ordinary status differentials — and by exposure to the sacred, the dangerous, and the transformative. The liminal person inhabits a state outside ordinary social structure and therefore outside ordinary cognitive structure: the categories and norms that organise everyday reality are suspended, creating an openness to new perceptions and new forms of understanding. Turner recognised that liminality was not merely a transitional inconvenience but the generative moment of the ritual process — the moment at which genuine transformation became possible precisely because the ordinary was suspended.

Turner extended the concept beyond formal ritual to identify liminoid spaces in modern industrial societies — art, theatre, carnival, counterculture — as functionally equivalent liminal zones in which the transformative potential of threshold experience continues to operate outside the formal ritual contexts that produced it in traditional societies.

The Phenomenology of the In-Between

As a state of consciousness, liminality is characterised by a specific quality of awareness that is neither fully one thing nor another. The threshold experience — standing in a doorway, at dusk rather than day or night, in the moment between waking and sleeping, in the period between an old identity and a new one — produces a distinctive cognitive and perceptual quality: heightened sensitivity to ambiguity and possibility, reduced defensiveness and increased openness, a sense of the ordinary world's contingency rather than its necessity. The familiar becomes strange; the boundaries between categories soften; the normally invisible joints of reality become perceptible.

This phenomenology of the in-between is not merely metaphorical. Empirical research on attentional broadening has shown that states of positive affect, openness, and reduced self-focus — all features of liminality — are associated with measurably wider attentional scope: more peripheral information reaches conscious awareness, more remote associative connections are accessible, and pattern recognition is enhanced in ways that can produce both creative insight and increased sensitivity to subtle environmental signals. The liminal state is, in neurological terms, a state of expanded receptive field — an expanded aperture on what is available to perception and cognition.

Threshold Beings in World Folklore

The cross-cultural folklore of threshold beings — entities associated specifically with doorways, crossroads, borders, and the in-between places of the world — represents one of the most widespread and internally consistent traditions in world mythological literature. These beings share several characteristics: they inhabit or appear at physical or temporal thresholds; their nature is ambiguous (neither fully good nor fully evil, neither fully present in ordinary reality nor fully absent from it); they have the power to assist or harm those who encounter them at the threshold; and they demand proper acknowledgement and ritual conduct from those who traverse their domain.

  • Crossroads entities: in African-American folklore (Hoodoo), Yoruba tradition (Eshu/Elegba), and elsewhere, the crossroads is the domain of a trickster-guardian whose assistance is sought through midnight ritual
  • Elves and fairies at twilight: Northern European fairy lore consistently places fairy encounters at dusk, dawn, and at physical liminal locations — hedgerows, hawthorn trees, stone circles, the shoreline
  • Angels at doorways: in biblical and later traditions, angels appear at the boundaries between ordinary and sacred space — the mouth of the cave, the gate of the city, the ford of the river
  • Kappa and water spirits: Japanese folklore places numerous dangerous entities at water's edge — the boundary between the human world and the world below
  • Hecate and triple crossroads: Greek-Roman religion identified the goddess of magic and thresholds specifically with the triple crossroads as her sacred space

The researcher Patrick Harpur, in Daimonic Reality (1994), has proposed that these threshold beings across traditions represent encounters with what he calls the daimonic — a realm of autonomous, image-generating intelligence that interpenetrates ordinary reality and becomes accessible at the points where ordinary reality's defences are weakest: the liminal thresholds of place and time. Harpur's framework offers a phenomenological bridge between the folklore of threshold beings and the contemporary anomalous encounter literature.

John Keel's Ultraterrestrial Windows

John Keel (1930–2009), the journalist and researcher whose field investigations of UAP phenomena produced works including UFOs: Operation Trojan Horse (1970), Strange Creatures from Time and Space (1970), and The Mothman Prophecies (1975), developed an empirical framework for what he called window areas: geographical locations and temporal periods associated with anomalously high rates of anomalous phenomena across multiple categories — UFO sightings, monster encounters, poltergeist activity, and other manifestations of what Keel called the ultraterrestrial intelligence. His work predates and foreshadows the current AARO interest in location-specific UAP activity by decades.

Keel's window areas correspond closely to traditional liminal geography: they include mountain ridges and passes, river confluences, ancient earthworks and sacred sites, and coastal zones — all representing physical boundaries between domains. His temporal analysis found that anomalous phenomena peak at certain times of year — solstices, equinoxes, and the cross-quarter days of the traditional Celtic calendar (Samhain, Imbolc, Beltane, Lughnasadh) — and at certain times of day, particularly the hours around dusk and dawn. These patterns align precisely with the traditional folklore of when the threshold between worlds is most permeable, suggesting either that the folklore accurately encodes empirically real temporal patterns, or that cultural expectations generated through folklore are themselves producing the observed anomalous experience distributions.

Liminal Geography and Sacred Sites

The relationship between anomalous phenomena and liminal geography — locations that mark boundaries between domains — has been documented systematically by researchers including John Burke and Kaj Halberg (electromagnetic anomalies at ancient sacred sites), Paul Devereux (earthlights and tectonic stress as a mechanism for anomalous light phenomena at geological fault lines and sacred sites), and Freddy Silva (the placement of megalithic monuments at locations of pre-existing geophysical anomaly). The convergence of human sacred site placement with geophysical anomaly locations suggests that ancient cultures possessed empirical knowledge — presumably acquired through direct experience — of locations where altered states and anomalous perception were facilitated.

Modern measurements at locations including Avebury, Stonehenge, Newgrange, and numerous cairns and stone circles have found elevated infrasound levels, unusual magnetic field fluctuations, and anomalous electromagnetic resonance patterns. Several small-scale studies have found that meditation and altered state induction at these locations produces more extreme results — more intense visual phenomena, higher incidence of anomalous perception reports — than comparable practices at control locations. Whether the geophysical anomalies are the cause of both the ancient sacred site placement and the modern anomalous experience reports, or whether both geophysical anomaly and anomalous experience are expressions of a common underlying property of these locations, remains unknown.

Temporal Liminality and the Phenomenon's Preferences

The statistical clustering of encounter and anomalous experience reports at temporal liminal moments — not only dusk and dawn, but the periods around major seasonal transitions, the hours after midnight, and anniversary dates — is consistent enough in the historical literature to merit serious attention. Whitley Strieber's accounts document encounters that occur with unusual frequency around the winter solstice period; the Rendlesham Forest incident occurred at the winter solstice; the Fátima apparitions occurred on specific dates throughout the summer and concluded at the autumn equinox. These temporal clusters are consistent with the hypothesis that whatever process underlies the Phenomenon is sensitive to, or preferentially expressed through, conditions that correspond to transitional states in natural cycles.

The hypnagogic state itself — detailed elsewhere on this site — is the paradigmatic neurological liminal state: the threshold between waking and sleep, between directed and autonomous cognition, between the social self and the dream self. Its privileged role in the anomalous experience literature may reflect not only its neurological properties (reduced inhibitory processing, high image-generativity, reduced external sensory noise) but its structural identity with the broader liminal condition: it is the experience of being precisely between, of being maximally open precisely because definition has been suspended. This structural resonance — between neurological liminality, physical and temporal liminality, and the conditions of anomalous encounter — suggests that liminality itself may be the fundamental operative variable in a wide range of anomalous phenomena, and that understanding it may be key to understanding the conditions under which the Phenomenon chooses to appear.
