🐟 Nommo

The Nommo are amphibious beings occupying a unique position within the aquatic NHI literature: they are among the oldest and most culturally grounded aquatic entity accounts on record, forming the cosmological foundation of the Dogon people of Mali, West Africa. The Dogon tradition describes the Nommo as fish-like or amphibious creators who descended from the sky associated with the Sirius star system, entering a body of water upon arrival and emerging periodically to communicate with humanity. What makes the Nommo case extraordinary is its convergence with the modern contact and exopolitical literature — the description of beings associated with Sirius, possessing aquatic adaptations, and serving as cosmic educators or civilizational founders aligns with multiple independent threads in the NHI research field. The Nommo are not a marginal entry in this literature but a central reference point for aquatic and Sirian-associated NHI typology.

The Nommo profile sits at the intersection of ancient cosmological testimony, anomalous astronomical knowledge, and the modern contact literature's accounts of amphibious or semi-aquatic beings reported in close encounter contexts. The degree to which the Dogon tradition represents genuine contact-encoded memory, symbolic mythology, or some combination of the two is a matter of ongoing debate; what is not debated is the internal consistency and ethnographic depth of the tradition, and its extraordinary resonance with features of the broader aquatic NHI typology.

The Dogon Tradition — Astronomical and Cosmological Context

The Dogon people of Mali, documented most extensively by French anthropologists Marcel Griaule and Germaine Dieterlen beginning in the 1930s, hold a cosmological system of unusual sophistication. Griaule's 1948 publication of his conversations with Dogon elder Ogotemmêli — later expanded in the co-authored Le Renard Pâle (The Pale Fox, 1965) — revealed that Dogon astronomy included knowledge apparently inconsistent with naked-eye observation:

  • Sirius B (Po Tolo): The Dogon describe a small, dense companion to Sirius — "Po Tolo," the smallest and heaviest of stars — completing a 50-year elliptical orbit around Sirius. The white dwarf Sirius B was not photographed until 1970 and was not detectable to the naked eye. The Dogon tradition describes its orbit, its density (heavier than all earthly iron), and its elliptical rather than circular path.
  • Sirius C (Emme Ya): The Dogon additionally describe a third star in the Sirius system — "Emme Ya" — larger than Sirius B but less bright than Sirius itself. As of the twenty-first century, astronomical evidence for a third body in the Sirius system remains inconclusive; the existence of Sirius C is neither confirmed nor definitively ruled out. The Dogon's description predates any Western awareness of the debate.
  • The moons of Jupiter and the rings of Saturn: Dogon cosmological knowledge also includes descriptions of Jupiter's moons and Saturn's ring system — both requiring telescopic observation that was unavailable to the Dogon in any documented historical period.
  • The origin attribution: The Dogon attribute all of this astronomical knowledge to the Nommo — the amphibious beings from the Sirius system who came to Earth in a vessel that descended with great noise and set fire to the earth, entering an arc of water upon landing. The Nommo taught the Dogon astronomy, agricultural cultivation, and the structure of the cosmos before returning to their origin system.

Skeptical analyses — notably Walter van Beek's 1991 re-examination of Dogon astronomical knowledge — have questioned whether the more precise astronomical details were present in the tradition prior to Western contact or were introduced through colonial-era astronomical interaction. The debate remains unresolved; Griaule's supporters note that his fieldwork predated the photographic confirmation of Sirius B and was conducted with elder knowledge-holders with no documented access to astronomical literature.

Physical Description — Traditional and Contact Accounts

  • Amphibious morphology: Dogon iconography and oral description characterize the Nommo as fish-like in their lower bodies, with humanoid upper bodies — a configuration that appears in multiple ancient cultures (the Babylonian Oannes, the Mesopotamian Apkallu, the Phoenician Dagon) in what researchers propose is a trans-cultural encoding of similar contact experiences. The precise configuration varies between traditions: some describe a smooth fish-tail below a humanoid torso, others describe gill structures and webbed extremities on an otherwise humanoid frame.
  • Aquatic adaptation markers: Descriptions consistently include features indicating water-medium adaptation — smooth, moist integument; gill-like structures at the neck or upper torso; webbed hands; large, wide-set eyes adapted for low-light or underwater vision; and a body plan optimized for aquatic locomotion.
  • Bioluminescence: Several contact accounts that researchers associate with the Nommo or Nommo-adjacent types include descriptions of bioluminescent or light-producing qualities in the being's integument — a feature consistent with deep-ocean aquatic organisms and with USO luminosity reports from the surrounding contact literature.
  • Group or paired appearance: The Dogon tradition describes the Nommo as appearing in complementary pairs — male and female, or in complementary functional roles. Multiple contact accounts describe aquatic humanoids appearing in small groups rather than individually, which some researchers align with this paired-manifestation characteristic.
  • Clothing or garment descriptions: Where modern contact accounts describe Nommo-adjacent beings in garment terms, the descriptions tend toward form-fitting suits with an iridescent or metallic quality — consistent with other aquatic NHI encounter descriptions and distinct from the silvery coveralls reported in many humanoid contact cases.

The Oannes Parallel — Ancient Mesopotamia

The most striking cross-cultural parallel to the Nommo tradition is the Oannes of Babylonian tradition, recorded by the Babylonian priest Berossus in the third century BCE based on much older Sumerian sources. Berossus describes Oannes — a being of fish and humanoid appearance who emerged from the sea — as the civilizational teacher of early Mesopotamia, bringing knowledge of writing, mathematics, astronomy, law, and agriculture before returning to the water. The structural parallel with the Dogon Nommo tradition is complete: an amphibious being from beyond the Earth, associated with water, serving as a civilizational educator, and departing after the transmission of foundational knowledge.

The Sumerian Apkallu — the seven sages who preceded the flood and transmitted civilization to humanity — are depicted in Assyrian iconography as fish-robed humanoids: human in form but wearing or emerging from a fish exterior. Their role as pre-flood knowledge transmitters, their amphibious iconographic identity, and their cosmic origin profile are structurally identical to both the Nommo and Oannes traditions. The convergence of these three traditions across Mali, Babylon, and Sumer — with no documented cultural cross-contamination — is among the most striking features of the Nommo research literature.

Modern Contact Literature — Sirian Connection

Within the modern contact and channeled literature, the Sirius star system is one of the most frequently cited origins for NHI that describe themselves as having a long history of interaction with Earth. The Sirian connection in this literature encompasses multiple being types — some humanoid, some aquatic — but the aquatic or amphibious Sirian type resonates most directly with the Nommo tradition:

  • Dolphin connection: Several researchers and contact experiencers — including Timothy Wyllie in Dolphins, ETs and Angels (1993) — describe a connection between Sirian NHI, dolphins, and cetacean intelligence that echoes the Nommo's traditional association with both water and cosmic transmission of knowledge. The Nommo are sometimes described in contact literature as the beings responsible for establishing cetacean intelligence on Earth as a secondary form of aquatic guardianship.
  • Ancient Egypt and Sirius: The Egyptian goddess Sopdet (Sothis) — associated with Sirius and the annual Nile flood that made Egyptian agriculture possible — connects Sirian influence with water and civilizational sustenance in a way that mirrors the Nommo role in Dogon cosmology. Several researchers propose that ancient Egyptian knowledge of Sirius encodes the same contact tradition as the Dogon record, preserved through a different cultural transmission path.
  • Contact testimonies — Sirian amphibious type: A subset of modern contact accounts — particularly from the 1980s–2000s channeled and experiencer literature — describes beings identifying themselves as Sirian and presenting with amphibious or semi-aquatic characteristics: smooth, slightly moist skin; large, widely spaced eyes; a body temperature cooler than human; and an association with water in the encounter setting. These accounts are distributed across independent experiencers and maintain the key physical differentiators of the aquatic NHI category.

Key Sources & Documentation

Foundational Academic Sources

  • Dieu d'Eau (God of Water) — Marcel Griaule, 1948. The primary ethnographic source for Dogon Nommo cosmology through the testimony of elder Ogotemmêli.
  • Le Renard Pâle (The Pale Fox) — Marcel Griaule & Germaine Dieterlen, 1965. The comprehensive elaboration of Dogon cosmological systems including full Nommo cycle documentation.
  • "A Study of Dogon Astronomical Knowledge" — Robert K. G. Temple analysis drawing on Griaule and Dieterlen's material.

Popular Research Literature

  • The Sirius Mystery — Robert K. G. Temple, 1976. The most comprehensive mainstream analysis of the Nommo tradition, the Sirius astronomical data, and the Oannes/Apkallu parallel traditions.
  • The Sirius Mystery: New Scientific Evidence — Robert K. G. Temple, 1998 (revised edition). Includes response to van Beek critique and updated astronomical data.
  • Invisible Residents — Ivan T. Sanderson, 1970. The foundational USO text that contextualizes aquatic NHI including analysis of the Nommo tradition within a broader underwater intelligence framework.

Contact & Experiencer Literature

  • Dolphins, ETs and Angels — Timothy Wyllie, 1993. Sirian-aquatic connection in contact literature.
  • The Prism of Lyra — Lyssa Royal & Keith Priest, 1992. Galactic history framework including Sirian aquatic types.
  • Bringers of the Dawn — Barbara Marciniak, 1992. Pleiadean channeled material with Sirian context; reference framework for contact literature researchers.
