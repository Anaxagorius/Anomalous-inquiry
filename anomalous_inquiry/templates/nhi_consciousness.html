🧠 Category IX — Consciousness-Based / Non-Physical Entities

Consciousness-based entities are proposed intelligences that exist as patterns of information, awareness, or intentionality without a biological or mechanical substrate — pure mind without body. This category represents the intersection of anomalous phenomena research, parapsychology, and fundamental questions in the philosophy of mind. It draws on out-of-body experience research, near-death accounts, remote viewing programs, and theoretical physics to propose frameworks in which consciousness may operate independently of and between physical substrates.

Pure Consciousness Intelligences and Disembodied Minds

The concept of disembodied intelligence appears across every major religious and philosophical tradition: the Christian Holy Spirit, the Hindu Brahman, the Stoic Logos, Buddhist consciousness streams between incarnations, shamanic spirit entities. Modern research frameworks that examine disembodied intelligence scientifically draw primarily on three bodies of evidence: near-death experiences (NDEs), out-of-body experiences (OBEs), and remote viewing research.

Robert Monroe (1915–1995), a radio broadcasting executive who began spontaneous OBE experiences in 1958, founded the Monroe Institute in Faber, Virginia, in 1974 to research altered states associated with binaural audio technology (Hemi-Sync). In his trilogy — Journeys Out of the Body (1971), Far Journeys (1985), and Ultimate Journey (1994) — Monroe documented encounters during OBE states with what he interpreted as non-physical intelligences: some apparently human spirits, others entities of a fundamentally different nature he called "Inspecs" (Intelligent Species), which appeared to be pure consciousness forms operating in environments beyond ordinary physical reality. Monroe's accounts are notable for their systematic, exploratory character and his efforts to apply verifiable test protocols to OBE experiences.

The Gateway Process, a 1983 U.S. Army Intelligence and Security Command (INSCOM) report prepared by analyst Wayne McDougall and declassified in 2003, evaluated the Monroe Institute's Hemi-Sync technology and out-of-body training for potential intelligence applications. The report, which became widely circulated after its CIA release in 2017, described a theoretical framework in which human consciousness is a resonance pattern of energy capable of operating outside the physical body — a framework the Army analyst considered scientifically plausible on the basis of Monroe Institute research and quantum physics parallels.

Collective Hive Minds and Non-Local Awareness

Collective or hive mind intelligences — distributed consciousness shared across multiple individual nodes without a centralised physical substrate — appear in the contact literature both as descriptions of Grey-type beings (who are often described as having no individual identity, operating as extensions of a group mind) and as distinct entities encountered in altered states.

The concept of non-local consciousness — awareness that operates independently of the constraints of space and time — has been explored theoretically by physicists including Henry Stapp (Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory), whose interpretation of quantum mechanics assigns a fundamental role to the observer's consciousness in wavefunction collapse, and Roger Penrose and Stuart Hameroff, whose orchestrated objective reduction (Orch-OR) theory proposes that consciousness arises from quantum processes in neuronal microtubules. These theories are contested within mainstream physics and neuroscience but represent serious academic attempts to integrate consciousness into physical theory.

Remote viewing research at the Stanford Research Institute (Russell Targ and Hal Puthoff, 1972–1985) and subsequently under the CIA-funded Stargate programme produced statistical evidence for anomalous information transfer that, if genuine, implies consciousness can access spatially remote information independent of known physical channels. The relevance to consciousness-based entities is that if human consciousness exhibits non-local properties to any degree, the existence of purely non-local consciousness forms becomes theoretically less implausible.

Intent-Based and Observer-Dependent Entities

A subset of consciousness-based entity reports describes beings that appear to have no stable independent existence but manifest in response to expectation, intention, or observation. This category overlaps with the psychological construction hypothesis (that all anomalous entities are mind-generated) but is distinguished by the reported ability of such entities to produce physical effects in the external world independent of the observer's immediate psychological state.

The Philip Experiment (1972–1975), conducted by the Toronto Society for Psychical Research under researchers including Dr. A.R.G. Owen and Iris Owen, demonstrated that a group of individuals could apparently generate a communicating "entity" (Philip Aylesford, a fictional 17th-century Englishman whose complete biographical history had been invented by the group) that produced rapping sounds, table movements, and consistent responses to questions — even providing specific answers the group had not pre-agreed. The Philip Experiment suggested that group consciousness could generate or attract an apparently external responding intelligence, though the physical mechanisms remain unexplained.

Theoretical physicist Tom Campbell, a former nuclear physicist who worked with Robert Monroe and subsequently developed a comprehensive "My Big TOE" (Theory of Everything) framework, proposes that physical reality is a subset of a larger consciousness system, and that various anomalous phenomena — including entity encounters — represent interactions with other consciousness systems operating within the same informational substrate. Campbell's model is consistent with the observer-dependent character of many high-strangeness events.

NHI Encounters in Near-Death and Psychedelic Contexts

A consistent feature of both near-death experiences and high-dose DMT (N,N-dimethyltryptamine) experiences is the reported encounter with apparently autonomous, intelligent non-human beings. DMT researchers including Rick Strassman (University of New Mexico, published as DMT: The Spirit Molecule, 2001) found that a significant proportion of research subjects spontaneously reported contact with insectoid beings, robotic entities, or "beings of light" during DMT states — descriptions strikingly similar to UAP encounter reports, produced by subjects with no knowledge of that literature.

Strassman's subsequent theoretical work, including DMT and the Soul of Prophecy (2014), explored the possible relationship between endogenous DMT production in the brain and the phenomenology of prophetic and visionary experiences in ancient texts. The question of whether DMT-induced entities represent internal mental constructs, non-physical intelligences accessible through altered neurology, or something else entirely remains fundamentally open — but the consistency across independent subjects of both the entities' appearance and their apparent intelligence has made the DMT entity phenomenon one of the most intriguing data points in consciousness research.
