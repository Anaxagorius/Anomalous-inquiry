🐜 Insectoid / Arthropod NHI Types

The insectoid and arthropod typology within the NHI research literature represents one of the most morphologically distinctive and internally varied groupings of reported non-human beings. Unlike humanoid NHI categories — Nordics, Greys, Reptilians — whose body plans closely map to familiar terrestrial vertebrate forms, the insectoid types present a fundamentally different structural template: multi-jointed limbs, compound or faceted eye structures, chitinous or armored integument, and in some accounts antennae, mandibles, or multi-part abdomens. This departure from the humanoid form is itself significant from a phenomenological standpoint: the morphological consistency of insectoid descriptions across independent experiencers separated by culture, geography, and decade argues against simple confabulation from shared media templates, as mainstream entertainment has historically underrepresented the insectoid type relative to the Grey or humanoid forms.

Within the insectoid category, researchers have identified at least five meaningfully distinct types based on recurrent morphological, behavioral, and contextual differences across the documented case literature: the iconic Mantids (praying mantis-type beings), the broader generic Insectoid category (beetle, ant, and generalized insect-forms), the hybrid-adjacent Mantoids, and two named species appearing in contact literature and alleged intelligence catalogues — the Itipurians and Klermers. Each presents a distinct profile in terms of physical description, behavioral role in reported encounters, and appearance in cross-cultural mythological parallels.

Typological Overview

The insectoid NHI category presents several features that recur across its sub-types and distinguish it as a coherent phenomenological grouping:

  • Chitinous or armored integument: Across all insectoid sub-types, the skin or body surface is consistently described as harder than mammalian skin — ranging from a flexible, segmented exoskeleton to a smooth, shell-like carapace. This feature distinguishes the insectoid category from all humanoid NHI types without exception.
  • Compound or multi-faceted eye structures: Eyes described as compound, faceted, multifaceted, or deeply concave — structured entirely unlike vertebrate eyes. This feature is present in Mantids, generic Insectoids, and Mantoids; it is modified in Itipurians and absent (replaced by uniformly dark, dome-like ocular structures) in Klermers.
  • Multi-joint limb articulation: Arms, legs, and in some cases additional appendages articulate at angles and positions inconsistent with any vertebrate joint structure. This is a universal marker across all five insectoid sub-types.
  • Telepathic or non-auditory communication: No insectoid type is consistently described as communicating through spoken language. All five types show predominantly telepathic communication in reported encounters, with some types also producing low-frequency vibrations, clicking, or subsonic effects.
  • Hive or colonial intelligence resonance: Multiple insectoid types — particularly the generic Insectoids and Klermers — are described by experiencers as giving the impression of acting within a larger organized collective rather than as fully autonomous individuals. This quality is less prominent in Mantids (who project strong individual presence) and intermediate in the remaining types.
  • Hierarchical positioning: In multi-species encounter settings, insectoid beings as a category are rarely described in subordinate roles; they are overwhelmingly described as supervisory, observational, or senior to other NHI types present in the same setting.

Cross-Cultural Mythological Context

Insectoid beings appear across ancient religious and mythological systems in ways that resonate — sometimes strikingly — with the modern contact literature:

  • Khepri (Egypt): The scarab-headed deity of dawn and self-creation, whose insect-headed form in hieroglyphic art and statue is among the most prominent non-mammalian deity representations in any ancient culture.
  • Ah-Muzen-Cab (Maya): The bee deity of the Maya Tzeltal and related cultures; represented in the Madrid Codex with prominent insectoid features. Beehive cosmology in Mesoamerican tradition positions insect beings as cosmological agents, not merely fauna.
  • Iktomi (Lakota): Spider/insect trickster being of vast intelligence and antiquity in Lakota cosmology; weaves the web of existence and holds knowledge inaccessible to ordinary beings — a profile closely matching the ancient-wisdom quality of modern Mantid encounters.
  • Indra's Net (Hindu/Buddhist): The cosmic network in Vedic cosmology, sometimes represented through spider-web imagery, with nodes corresponding to each being in the universe — a metaphor structurally consistent with the hive-intelligence model attributed to some insectoid NHI types.
  • Mesopotamian insect omens: Cuneiform omen texts (particularly the Šumma ālu series) contain extensive entries on insect appearances as divine messages, suggesting an ancient tradition of insect beings as agents or messengers of higher intelligences.

Documented Insectoid Sub-Types

🦗 Mantids / Mantis Beings

The most widely documented insectoid type. Tall, praying mantis-like beings — 6–7 ft, elongated multi-joint limbs, triangular faceted eyes — appearing almost exclusively in supervisory or observational roles. Associated with ancient wisdom, patient intelligence, and emotional connection across multiple encounters.

🪲 Insectoids (Generic Type)

The broader category of insect-morphology beings beyond the mantis form — including beetle-adjacent, ant-like, and generalized arthropod forms. Appearing across abduction cases, underground facility reports, and indigenous contact traditions. More procedural and less personalized in behavioral profile than Mantids.

🔬 Mantoids

A hybrid-intermediate insectoid type — combining insectoid structural features with a more humanoid body plan than pure Mantids. Reported in multi-species encounter settings, often performing specialized technical or research functions. Some researchers propose a bio-engineered origin.

🌐 Itipurians

A named insectoid type appearing in contact literature and channeled material, described as smaller and more compact than Mantids, with distinctive amber-to-gold coloration, antennae structures, and a role associated with communication and transmission rather than supervision.

📋 Klermers

A named insectoid type appearing in contact literature, documented as analytically oriented observers and record-keepers. Small-to-medium in stature, with smooth carapace rather than visible segmentation, uniformly dark ocular structures, and a methodical behavioral signature distinct from all other insectoid types.

Insectoid Beings in the Research Literature

The following researchers and research frameworks have contributed the most to the documentation of insectoid NHI types:

  • Dr. David Jacobs (Temple University): Jacobs' two-decade hypnotic regression archive — spanning several hundred subjects — contains the most extensive systematic documentation of Mantid beings in supervisory roles. His hierarchical model of the abduction program places insectoid beings at or near the apex of the multi-species structure observed by abductees.
  • Barbara Lamb (certified hypnotherapist): Over 2,000 regression sessions since 1991 provide a substantial insectoid sub-population; Lamb estimates Mantid presence in approximately 15–20% of multi-species encounter cases in her archive. Her work also documents instances of generic insectoid and Mantoid types.
  • Yvonne Smith (CERO): The Close Encounter Research Organization archive, assembled through Smith's long-running hypnotherapy practice, represents one of the largest collections of insectoid encounter descriptions from independent experiencers.
  • Kathleen Marden (MUFON Research Director): Marden's investigative and hypnotherapy work includes multiple insectoid cases across the Mantid, generic insectoid, and Mantoid categories.
  • Dr. John Mack (Harvard Medical School): Mack's 200+ abduction subject interviews, documented in Abduction (1994) and Passport to the Cosmos (1999), include insectoid-adjacent beings described across culturally diverse subjects. Mack noted the cross-cultural consistency of certain insectoid descriptions as among the most difficult features to explain through culturally-derived confabulation.
  • The alleged Russian Alien Races Book: The KGB-attributed species catalogue documents several hive-intelligence species assessed as ancient and numerous, with colonial intelligence structures. The absence of a specific "Mantid" entry — despite the frequency of the type in Western literature — is itself a notable data point; some researchers map the Klermers and Itipurians to entries within this catalogue.
