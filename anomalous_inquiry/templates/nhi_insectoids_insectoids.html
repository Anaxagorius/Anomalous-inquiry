🪲 Insectoids — Generic Type

The generic insectoid category — distinct from the specific Mantid/mantis form, the Mantoid intermediate, and the named species Itipurians and Klermers — encompasses the wide range of insect-form beings reported across the contact, abduction, and encounter literature that do not fit cleanly into these more specific sub-types. These beings share the defining features of the insectoid NHI category (chitinous integument, compound or multi-part eye structures, multi-joint limb articulation, and non-auditory communication) but present morphological templates drawn from the broader order of insects and arthropods: beetle-adjacent, ant-like, wasp-form, and generalized arthropod configurations. The generic insectoid type is, on the whole, less individuated and less emotionally engaged in encounters than Mantids — appearing more frequently in procedural, collective, or industrial contexts — and is among the most widely distributed insectoid sub-types across both geographic regions and research archives.

Physical Description — Morphological Variants

Unlike the Mantid type, which presents a consistent morphological template across independent accounts, the generic insectoid category encompasses several distinct body plans. Researchers have identified the following recurring variants:

  • Beetle/coleoptera-adjacent form: A broad, heavily built variant relative to other insectoid types. The body is more compact and rounded; the carapace is thicker and more dome-like, covering the dorsal surface fully. Head is round or ovoid rather than triangular. Eye structures are large but less dramatically wrapping than Mantid eyes. Height: typically 4–6 ft. Color: dark grey to near-black, occasionally deep iridescent blue-green. This form appears most frequently in underground facility accounts and in contact cases from mining, industrial, or subterranean settings.
  • Ant/hymenoptera-adjacent form: The most frequently cited generic insectoid variant in collective-setting accounts. Head is elongated and ovoid; visible mandible structures reported in a proportion of accounts. Antennae always present. Body is clearly three-segmented (head, thorax, abdomen) relative to the Mantid form's less pronounced segmentation. Height: 4–5 ft. Coloring: tan, ochre, or reddish-brown. Movement is described as rapid, purposeful, and highly coordinated within groups — more resembling coordinated team activity than individual agency. The "ant-like" behavioral profile — precise group coordination without visible communication — is the behavioral signature of this variant.
  • Wasp/vespid-adjacent form: A slender, waist-constricted body plan with yellow-brown or banded coloration reported in a small but consistent subset of accounts. Wings or wing-vestige structures (non-functional in all accounts) may be present on the dorsal thorax. More aggressive or territorial behavioral profile than other generic insectoid variants; this form appears disproportionately in accounts where insectoid beings were perceived as guards or security-function entities within facility settings.
  • Generalized arthropod form: Accounts that do not map clearly to a specific insect order; described as insect-like but not identifiable with any specific family. This category captures a significant proportion of brief, low-observation encounters where sufficient detail was not available for morphological classification.

Across all variants, the following physical features are universal: multi-joint limb articulation, some form of compound or multi-faceted eye structure, chitinous or shell-like integument, and absence of clothing or artifact in most accounts. Body size is consistently smaller than the Mantid type.

Behavioral Profile

  • Collective rather than individual orientation: The most defining behavioral feature of generic insectoid encounters is the impression of collective, coordinated intelligence rather than individual agency. Unlike Mantids — who project strong individual presence and are recognized across encounters — generic insectoids in group settings give the impression of acting from a shared awareness, with individuals functionally interchangeable within the group task.
  • Procedural and technical roles: Generic insectoids appear predominantly in contexts involving physical tasks, material handling, technical operations, or structured procedures. They are rarely described in the wisdom-transmission or emotionally connected roles typical of Mantids. Their interaction with human experiencers tends to be indirect — engagement with the human is incidental to the task being performed rather than the purpose of the encounter.
  • Efficiency and economy: Movement and activity in generic insectoid accounts is described as extremely efficient — no unnecessary movements, no pause between task segments, no apparent hesitation. Several experiencers compare the quality of movement to machinery rather than biological organisms.
  • Subsonic communication: While telepathy is noted in some generic insectoid accounts, a distinctive feature of this sub-type relative to others is the frequency of reports describing low-frequency vibrational or clicking communication within insectoid groups — not always perceived by human experiencers as information-directed toward them, but rather as an intra-group communication modality operating at the periphery of their perception.
  • Neutral affect: Generic insectoids are described as exhibiting neither the warmth and emotional engagement typical of Mantids, nor the cold calculation associated with some Grey types, nor the intimidation factor of Reptilian and Maitre encounters. The prevailing experiential impression is one of complete indifference to the human's emotional state — not hostile, not friendly; simply not oriented toward the human in emotional terms.
  • Subordinate to Mantids in multi-species settings: In encounters where both Mantids and generic insectoids are present, the relationship is consistently described as hierarchical — the generic insectoids perform tasks under the observational presence of the Mantid, with the same deference structure that Grey beings exhibit toward Mantids.

Key Cases & Documentation

Philip Schneider — Dulce Facility Claims (1995–1996)

Philip Schneider, a self-described former government structural engineer and geologist who claimed to have worked on construction of underground facilities for the U.S. government, gave a series of public lectures in 1995–1996 describing encounters with insectoid and Grey beings in an underground facility near Dulce, New Mexico. Schneider described an "alien war" incident in which he claimed his crew encountered large insectoid beings while drilling. His accounts describe generic insectoid beings — not Mantid type — performing non-communicative, operationally focused activities. Schneider died in January 1996 in disputed circumstances (ruled suicide; disputed by some researchers as murder). His testimony remains highly contested; no independent verification of the underground facility claims has been established. It is documented here as a significant data point in the underground-insectoid encounter literature regardless of its ultimate provenance.

Thomas Castello — Alleged Dulce Security Officer Testimony

Thomas Castello is an alleged whistleblower who claimed to have been a security officer at the Dulce underground facility, describing in detail a multi-species population including several insectoid types performing different facility functions. Castello's material — circulated through researcher Branton (Bruce Alan Walton) in the 1990s — describes a hierarchical facility in which different insectoid types perform distinct operational roles: ant-adjacent forms as workers, larger beetle-adjacent forms as guards or laborers. The provenance of the Castello material is unverified; it is presented here as a significant piece of underground-insectoid contact literature.

CERO Archive — Collective Insectoid Cases

Yvonne Smith's CERO (Close Encounter Research Organization) case archive contains a subset of regression-derived accounts in which experiencers describe generic insectoid beings — not Mantids — performing tasks within craft or facility environments. These cases are notable for the consistency of the collective-behavior description across experiencers who had no prior contact with each other or with the underground facility contact literature.

South American Contact Traditions

Multiple South American contact traditions — particularly from the Amazon Basin and Andean regions — include accounts of insect-form beings associated with specific locations, practices, or states of consciousness. Ayahuasca-facilitated visions in the Shipibo and related Amazonian traditions frequently include insect and arthropod being encounters that share features with the generic insectoid NHI profile: compound eyes, multi-joint limbs, non-human body plans, and a quality of operating according to purposes inaccessible to the human observer. Anthropologist Luis Eduardo Luna's systematic documentation of ayahuasca vision content (beginning in the 1980s) provides the most detailed published record of this tradition.

Underground / Subterranean Context

Generic insectoid beings appear disproportionately in accounts involving underground or subterranean settings, relative to other NHI types. This association is present across multiple independent contact traditions and research frameworks:

  • Dulce / underground facility literature: The body of testimony associated with alleged underground bases — spanning Schneider, Castello, Bob Lazar-adjacent claims, and MUFON case files — consistently features generic insectoid beings in facility-worker or facility-guard roles alongside Greys.
  • Native American traditions: Several Pueblo, Hopi, and Navajo traditions include accounts of insect beings (Ant People, Spider Grandmother, etc.) who live underground, emerge to interact with surface-dwelling humans at critical junctures, and possess technical knowledge or cosmological information. The Hopi Ant People tradition specifically describes insectoid beings who sheltered humanity underground during a previous world-cycle.
  • Ancient Sumerian texts: The "Anunnaki who go below" in certain Sumerian textual traditions are described in terms that some researchers interpret as insectoid-adjacent — beings who operate in underground domains, manage human labor, and possess a collective-intelligence organizational structure.
  • Global cave mythology: The association of insect beings with underground realms appears across Mesoamerican (Xibalba), Japanese (Yomi), and Celtic traditions, suggesting a cross-cultural resonance between insect-form and subterranean intelligence that predates the modern contact literature.

Cross-Cultural Parallels

  • Hopi Ant People (Anu Sinom): In Hopi oral tradition, the Ant People (literally "Ant Friends" — Anu Sinom) rescued humanity during the destruction of the First and Second Worlds, sheltering them in underground homes and sharing food during long periods of concealment. The Ant People are described as small, industrious, and insect-form — one of the most structurally specific ancient insectoid being references in any indigenous tradition.
  • Egyptian bee symbolism: The bee hieroglyph (bjt) in ancient Egypt represented Lower Egypt and was incorporated into the title of the Pharaoh (nswt-bjt: "He of the Sedge and Bee"). Some researchers propose that the bee's social structure — a collective intelligence organized around a queen, with workers performing specialized roles — served as a metaphorical template for Egyptian cosmological thinking, possibly reflecting genuine insectoid contact traditions within the priestly class.
  • Mayan wasp/bee cults: The Xunan Kab (sacred bee) in Mayan tradition was associated with the Ah-Muzen-Cab deity and occupied a place of cosmological significance beyond simple agricultural or economic importance. The Mayan beekeeping tradition — among the world's oldest — appears intertwined with a religious-contact dimension that some researchers interpret as reflecting insectoid NHI contact.
  • Vedic Gandharva insect parallels: Some Vedic texts describe categories of non-human beings whose social organization and physical description contain insectoid features — collective organization, specialized caste roles, non-mammalian form — that researchers in the ancient astronaut tradition map to the generic insectoid NHI category.
