🏛️ Anunnaki

The Anunnaki are among the most prominent divine beings in ancient Mesopotamian mythology — attested in thousands of cuneiform tablets from Sumer, Akkad, Babylon, and Assyria, representing some of the earliest written records in human history. The NHI reinterpretation — most comprehensively developed by Zecharia Sitchin — proposes that the Anunnaki were not mythological gods but extraterrestrial beings whose activities, including the creation of humanity, are recorded as literal historical events in the ancient texts. This page documents both the authenticated archaeological record and the NHI interpretive framework, maintaining phenomenological neutrality throughout.

The Authentic Archaeological Record

The Anunnaki appear extensively in cuneiform texts — the world's oldest writing system, developed in Sumer (southern Mesopotamia, modern Iraq) around 3200 BCE. The texts are genuine, extensively documented, and translated by multiple independent scholars from the 19th century onward.

Key primary sources:

  • Enuma Elish (Babylonian Creation Epic, c. 1100 BCE): Describes the Anunnaki as divine beings associated with heaven and earth; the creation of humanity from the blood of a slain god mixed with clay.
  • Atrahasis Epic (c. 1700 BCE, Akkadian): Most directly relevant to the "created humanity" narrative — the Igigi (lesser gods) complain to the Anunnaki about their labor, and humanity is created to perform the work instead. The Atrahasis also contains the Mesopotamian flood narrative (predating Genesis by at least 1,000 years).
  • Sumerian King List: Lists kings of Sumer before and after the flood; pre-flood kings are assigned reigns of tens of thousands of years — interpreted by mainstream scholars as mythological exaggeration, by Sitchin and others as literal records of Anunnaki lifespans.
  • Descent of Inanna: Goddess Inanna descends to the underworld through seven gates; the Anunnaki serve as judges of the dead in the underworld realm.

These texts are held in the British Museum (London), the Louvre (Paris), the Oriental Institute (University of Chicago), the Vorderasiatisches Museum (Berlin), and the Iraqi National Museum (Baghdad), among others. Standard academic translation and interpretation describes the Anunnaki as mythological divine beings, their stories as religious and cosmological narrative, not historical record.

Zecharia Sitchin's Framework

Zecharia Sitchin (1920–2010), a Russian-born American author who studied at the London School of Economics and claimed to read ancient Sumerian, Akkadian, and Hebrew, proposed in his Earth Chronicles series (beginning with The 12th Planet in 1976) a radically literal reinterpretation of Mesopotamian texts:

  • Nibiru: A 12th planet (counting Sun, Moon, and the 9 then-known planets as the Babylonians did) with a highly elliptical 3,600-year orbit that periodically brings it into the inner solar system. Sitchin identified Nibiru with the "planet of the crossing" in Babylonian astronomy.
  • Anunnaki origin: The Anunnaki are Nibiru's inhabitants, described as coming to Earth approximately 450,000 years ago. Their primary mission: mining gold to repair Nibiru's damaged atmosphere (gold particulates as atmospheric protection).
  • Human creation: After 200,000 years of Anunnaki mining labor (performed by the Igigi — lesser Anunnaki workers), rebellion led the Anunnaki geneticist Enki and his sister Ninhursag to create a "Lulu Amelu" (primitive worker) by genetically modifying Homo erectus with Anunnaki DNA — explaining the otherwise unaccounted gap in human evolution. This is Sitchin's interpretation of the Atrahasis creation narrative.
  • Gold mining in Africa: Sitchin placed early gold mining operations in southern Africa; researcher Michael Tellinger subsequently documented ancient stone circle ruins in South Africa's Mpumalanga region that he argues are consistent with ancient mining communities.
  • Departure: The Anunnaki departed — or a significant portion departed — when Nibiru's orbit moved it beyond the inner solar system; some remained as the "gods" of early civilization.

Academic critique of Sitchin is substantial: professional Sumerologists (notably Michael Heiser) argue that Sitchin's translations contain numerous errors, that Nibiru in Babylonian texts refers to a specific planet or position (not a 12th planet on an unusual orbit), and that his interpretations require reading literal meaning into texts scholars understand as mythological. Sitchin responded to these critiques in later works. The debate continues; Sitchin's framework remains enormously popular despite the scholarly objections.

Physical Description

From the ancient texts and Sitchin's interpretation:

  • Stature: Described in texts as tall, powerful beings; Sumerian artworks depict them as human in form but often larger than the humans in the same scenes. Interpretations range from "impressively large" to "giant" (several texts use terms that could be translated as giant, though this translation is contested).
  • Appearance: Humanoid; bearded males in the texts; wearing distinctive garments. Certain Sumerian reliefs show Anunnaki wearing what Sitchin describes as pressure suits or protective armor.
  • The "handbag": A distinctive object — appearing like a structured bag or bucket — is carried by Anunnaki figures in Sumerian relief sculpture, notably at Nimrud. Also appears in Mesoamerican art (held by beings in the Olmec and Toltec artistic tradition) and in other widely separated cultures. Researchers have variously interpreted this as a portable life-support device, a genetic sampling kit, or a time-keeping/navigation instrument.
  • The "shem": Sitchin translates the Sumerian word typically rendered as "memorial stone" or "name" as "rocket" or "flying vehicle" — a translation disputed by mainstream scholars but central to his framework for the Anunnaki's aerospace capability.

The Oannes / Apkallu Parallel

Berossus, a 3rd-century BCE Babylonian priest, wrote a history of Babylon in Greek in which he described the Oannes — a being described as having the body of a fish but with a human head beneath the fish head, and human feet beneath the fish tail. Oannes reportedly emerged from the Persian Gulf and spent days teaching humanity agriculture, writing, law, geometry, and astronomy, then returned to the sea at night. Berossus said this being's instruction gave humanity all the arts of civilization at once.

Sumerian art depicts the Apkallu — seven sages who served as advisors to the antediluvian kings — wearing fish-skin cloaks and carrying the distinctive "handbag." Some researchers interpret the fish-cloak as either literal amphibious NHI physiology or as an environmental suit; others interpret it as the visual representation of ancient astronomical and astrological knowledge encoded in the fish-priest imagery.

Physical Evidence Claims

  • Baalbek, Lebanon: Three megalithic stones (the Trilithon) each weighing approximately 800 tonnes, incorporated into the base of the Roman Temple of Jupiter. The quarried "Stone of the Pregnant Woman" (approximately 1,200 tonnes) remains partially in its quarry. No ancient technology identified by mainstream archaeology can account for their transport and placement; ancient astronaut proponents cite this as requiring Anunnaki technology.
  • Puma Punku, Bolivia: Stone blocks of andesite and red sandstone cut with precision rivaling modern machine tools — precise angles, drilled holes, H-blocks interlocking with extraordinary tolerances. Part of the Tiwanaku complex (c. 500–900 CE mainstream dating; disputed by some researchers). Cited by ancient astronaut proponents as beyond ancient human hand-tool capability.
  • Adam's Calendar, South Africa: Michael Tellinger's interpretation of stone circles in the Mpumalanga region as the oldest stone calendar in the world (claimed at 75,000 years or older; mainstream dating is disputed). Tellinger connects these to Sitchin's African gold mining narrative.
