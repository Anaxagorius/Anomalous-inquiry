🌍 Category II — Ultra-Terrestrial / Intra-Terrestrial Beings

The Ultra-Terrestrial or Intra-Terrestrial hypothesis proposes that some or all reported non-human intelligences are not extraterrestrial visitors but Earth-native beings — intelligences that evolved on this planet either alongside or prior to Homo sapiens and have maintained a hidden coexistence. This hypothesis has been advanced by serious researchers as an alternative to the extraterrestrial interpretation, particularly in response to the behavioural and phenomenological features of UAP encounters that fit poorly with naive models of spacefaring visitors.

The Cryptoterrestrials Hypothesis

Mac Tonnies (1975–2009) was a writer, futurist, and anomalist whose posthumously published The Cryptoterrestrials (2010) provided the most developed modern statement of the intra-terrestrial hypothesis. Tonnies argued that the UAP phenomenon is better explained by an indigenous terrestrial intelligence — long pre-human or evolved in parallel with human civilisation — than by visitors from other star systems. Key arguments included:

  • The reported beings' preoccupation with human biology, genetics, and reproduction makes more sense for a species sharing our biosphere than for interstellar visitors.
  • The phenomenon's cultural responsiveness — adapting its apparent form to match the technological and mythological idiom of each era — suggests proximity rather than distance.
  • An advanced terrestrial species that predated Homo sapiens by millions of years would have ample time to develop concealment technologies and underground or deep-ocean habitats completely unknown to surface humans.
  • The apparent scarcity and vulnerability of some reported beings could reflect a remnant population using deception and misdirection as survival strategies against a dominant human civilisation.

Tonnies died suddenly in 2009 at age 34, before completing the final manuscript. The book was assembled from his notes and drafts by his estate and editor.

Inner Earth / Agartha

The concept of a habitable inner Earth inhabited by advanced beings draws on both ancient myth and modern fringe science. The name Agartha (also Aghartha or Shambhala in some traditions) appears in nineteenth-century Theosophical literature, particularly the writings of Helena Blavatsky and later Alexandre Saint-Yves d'Alveydre, whose Mission de l'Inde (1886) described a hidden underground kingdom governed by a spiritual hierarchy.

The Tibetan Buddhist tradition of Shambhala — a hidden kingdom described in the Kalachakra Tantra and other texts — is distinct from the Western Agartha concept but has been repeatedly conflated with it. Genuine Tibetan scholars treat Shambhala as a spiritual rather than physical realm; the conflation with a literal subterranean civilisation is a Western esoteric overlay.

The Shaver Mystery (1943–1948) represents the first mass-media engagement with the intra-terrestrial concept. Richard Shaver, a welding worker in Michigan, submitted manuscripts to Amazing Stories editor Ray Palmer claiming that Earth is inhabited by ancient, degenerated beings he called "Deros" (Detrimental Robots) and "Teros" (Integrative Robots) who live in vast cavern systems and use ancient machines to torment surface humanity. Palmer published Shaver's material as science fiction but received thousands of letters from readers claiming personal experience of the phenomena described. The Shaver Mystery generated unprecedented reader correspondence for a pulp magazine and established the intra-terrestrial concept in American popular culture decades before Tonnies formalised it theoretically.

Olaf Jansen's The Smoky God (1908), written by Willis George Emerson based on a purported Norwegian sailor's memoir, described a voyage through polar openings into a warm inner world lit by a central sun. Though almost certainly fiction, it was among the inspirations for the later Hollow Earth movement.

Hollow Earth Theory and Admiral Byrd

John Cleves Symmes Jr. (1780–1829), an American army officer, promoted the idea in the early nineteenth century that Earth is hollow with large openings at the poles, and petitioned Congress in 1822 for an expedition to find them. While his physics were wholly unfounded, Symmes inspired a persistent tradition of polar-opening mythology.

The most enduring modern Hollow Earth narrative involves Admiral Richard E. Byrd and his polar expeditions. Advocates claim that Byrd's diary from his 1947 Operation High Jump Antarctic expedition contains references to flying beyond the Pole into a warm, inhabited inner land. These claims were published in a book purportedly based on Byrd's diary by anonymous authors in the 1970s; the document is regarded by mainstream historians as a fabrication. Operation High Jump was a legitimate U.S. Navy expedition involving 13 ships and 4,700 personnel, formally tasked with establishing a research station and conducting aerial surveys. The expedition did encounter unexpectedly difficult conditions and was curtailed; this ambiguity has fed speculation.

Subterranean Humanoids and Deep-Ocean NHIs

Researcher Richard Sauder documented in Underground Bases and Tunnels (1995) and subsequent books extensive evidence for a vast network of classified underground military facilities in the United States, drawing on declassified documents and contractor records. Sauder's thesis is limited to human underground infrastructure, but his work is often cited by advocates of non-human subterranean presence as evidence that the subsurface environment is more accessible than commonly assumed.

The alleged Dulce Base — a claimed joint human-alien underground facility beneath Archuleta Mesa near Dulce, New Mexico — is the most prominent specific location in the intra-terrestrial literature. The story originated primarily with accounts attributed to Thomas Edwin Castello, a claimed security officer at the facility, whose manuscripts circulated in UFO research circles from the 1980s. Independent verification of Castello's identity or claims has not been established. The Dulce area has had significant cattle mutilation incidents and repeated UAP sighting reports, documented by researcher Gabe Valdez, a New Mexico state trooper who conducted official investigations.

Deep-ocean NHI hypotheses draw on USO (Unidentified Submerged Object) reports to suggest that some intelligences have established habitats in the ocean depths, which cover more than 70% of Earth's surface and whose abyssal regions remain largely unexplored. This category overlaps with Category XI (Aquatic/Transmedium NHIs) and is addressed more fully there.
