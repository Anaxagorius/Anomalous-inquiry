✝️ Exorcism Throughout History & Cultures

← Paranormal

A comparative historical and ethnographic survey of exorcism traditions spanning Mesopotamian antiquity to the contemporary era. Entries draw from cuneiform tablets, scriptural canons, ecclesiastical records, ethnographic fieldwork, clinical case literature, and comparative religion scholarship. The practice of ritual expulsion of malevolent spirits or intrusive entities from persons, places, and objects appears in virtually every documented human culture.

I. Ancient Near East & Mesopotamia

Babylonian & Assyrian Exorcism

The oldest systematised exorcism corpus in the archaeological record is the Mesopotamian Maqlu ("Burning") series — a collection of eight cuneiform tablets assembled c. 1000 BCE but drawing on Sumerian antecedents. Maqlu comprised incantations, prayers, and ritual acts (burning figurines, purification with fire and water) designed to counter witchcraft and drive out evil. A complementary series, Shurpu ("Incineration"), addressed taboo violations that invited demonic affliction. The specialist who performed these rites was the āšipu (exorcist-priest), a figure distinct from the asû (physician), though the two often worked in tandem: the āšipu expelled spiritual causes while the asû treated physical symptoms. The demon Lamashtu — depicted as a lion-headed female predator of pregnant women and infants — required specific counter-rites involving the god Pazuzu, whose image was worn as protective amulet.

Sumerian Precedents & the Udug-hul Series

The Udug-hul ("Evil Demons") series, composed in Sumerian but preserved in bilingual Sumerian-Akkadian recensions from the first millennium BCE, catalogues evil spirits and the incantations required to expel them. The udug, ala, gidim, and dim-me constitute a taxonomy of afflicting entities corresponding broadly to the disease categories the texts address: epilepsy, mental illness, fever, and infant mortality. The role of the god Marduk, patron of Babylon, as the archetypal divine exorcist — acting on behalf of his father Enki/Ea, god of wisdom and magic — established a theological model in which exorcism derived its efficacy from divine authority, a framework that would persist through Abrahamic traditions.

Ancient Egypt

Egyptian exorcism was embedded within a comprehensive medical and magical system in which disease was attributed to gods, the dead, or demons (bau) violating the order of maat. The Ebers Papyrus (c. 1550 BCE) and the London Medical Papyrus contain incantations to be recited over patients alongside pharmacological prescriptions, with deities invoked to withdraw afflictions. Sekhmet, the lion-headed goddess of plague and destruction, was both cause and cure of epidemics; her priests served as healers and ritual specialists. The concept of demonic possession by the dead (mut, restless spirits) required the intervention of specialised priest-magicians (lector priests or kher-hebet) who recited protective and expulsive texts on behalf of afflicted individuals.

Ancient Greek & Roman Traditions

Classical Mediterranean exorcism drew on both indigenous Greek traditions and Eastern imports. The goês (sorcerer-exorcist) and the magos (magician, often of Persian attributed origin) were figures who trafficked with spirits. Platonic philosophy distinguished between daimones — potentially beneficent intermediary spirits — and kakodaimones (evil demons), anticipating later demonological distinctions. In Rome, the defixiones (binding tablets) tradition involved both afflicting and releasing persons from demonic bonds. The Greek Magical Papyri (PGM) — an Egyptian corpus from the 2nd century BCE to 5th century CE, mixing Egyptian, Jewish, and Greek elements — contains some of the earliest surviving explicit exorcism instructions, including formulae invoking Hebrew divine names, demonstrating the syncretism that would produce late antique exorcism practice.

II. Jewish & Early Christian Traditions

Second Temple Judaism & the Dead Sea Scrolls

The Dead Sea Scrolls contain multiple exorcism-related texts, including the Genesis Apocryphon (which depicts Abraham exorcising Pharaoh by prayer and the laying on of hands) and the Prayer of Nabonidus (portraying the Babylonian king healed of a skin disease by a Jewish exorcist). The Book of Tobit, preserved in the Septuagint, presents a detailed exorcism narrative: the demon Asmodeus is driven from Sara by burning the liver and heart of a fish at the instruction of the angel Raphael — ritual technology attributed divine prescription. The figure of King Solomon as master exorcist was prominent in Second Temple literature; the Testament of Solomon (2nd–5th century CE) presents Solomon binding and interrogating demons by divine seal. This Solomonic tradition fed directly into medieval grimoire culture.

New Testament Exorcism

The Synoptic Gospels present exorcism as central to Jesus's ministry: the Gerasene demoniac episode (Mark 5:1–20) — in which Jesus expels a self-named legion of demons into swine — is among the most detailed exorcism narratives in antiquity. Jesus's method was notably direct: no lengthy incantations, no ritual materials, only command in his own name, which New Testament scholarship identifies as theologically distinctive. Graham Twelftree's Jesus the Exorcist (1993) and John Meier's A Marginal Jew treat the exorcism tradition as among the most historically secure elements of the Gospel material. The disciples were commissioned to exorcise; Acts records performed exorcisms by the apostles and the unauthorized use of Jesus's name by itinerant exorcists (Acts 19:13–16), suggesting broad cultural currency.

Early Church & the Exorcist Order

The early Christian church formalised exorcism within its institutional structure. Tertullian (c. 200 CE) cites the exorcism of demons from the mentally ill as a public apologetic demonstration of Christian spiritual authority. By the third century, the office of exorcista was one of the minor orders of the Western church — a formally ordained role distinct from lay charismatic exorcism. Origen described the expulsion of demons as ordinary Christian practice requiring only invocation of Jesus's name. The catechumenate — the process of preparation for baptism — involved ritual exorcisms (the scrutinies) intended to free candidates from demonic influence before baptism. Patristic writers including Irenaeus, Justin Martyr, and Cyril of Jerusalem treat exorcism as a routine pastoral and apologetic practice.

The Dybbuk and Kabbalistic Exorcism

The concept of the dybbuk — a clinging spirit of a deceased person who has invaded a living body — appears in Jewish popular tradition from the 16th century, notably in Safed (northern Israel/Palestine), the centre of Lurianic Kabbalah. Rabbi Isaac Luria (the Ari, 1534–1572) and his disciple Hayyim Vital developed elaborate exorcism procedures drawing on Kabbalistic theory: the possessing spirit was a soul unable to complete its cosmic rectification (tikkun), and exorcism involved addressing the spirit, determining its unfinished business, and assisting its departure through prayer, incantation, and the blowing of the shofar at specific bodily points. J.H. Chajes's Between Worlds: Dybbuks, Exorcists and Early Modern Judaism (2003) provides the most thorough academic treatment of the tradition.

III. Christian Exorcism — Medieval to Modern

Medieval Catholic Practice

Medieval exorcism theology was systematised by Thomas Aquinas (Summa Theologica), who distinguished between the church's solemn exorcism (requiring episcopal authority) and simple blessings. The Malleus Maleficarum (1487), authored by Dominican inquisitor Heinrich Kramer, while primarily a witch-hunting manual, extensively addressed demonic possession and its remedies, contributing to a conflation of witchcraft prosecution with exorcism theology that characterised late medieval and early modern practice. Notable mass possession epidemics — Loudun (1632–1638), Louviers (1642–1647), Auxonne (1658–1663) — occupied both ecclesiastical tribunals and emergent medical opinion, generating extensive documentation reviewed by later historians including Michel de Certeau (The Possession at Loudun, 1970).

The Rituale Romanum (1614 & 1999)

The Rituale Romanum of 1614, issued under Pope Paul V, codified Catholic exorcism rite into a standardised liturgical form used for over 350 years. The rite requires: episcopal authorisation for the exorcist; preliminary investigation to exclude natural causes; a structured sequence of prayers, psalms, Gospel readings, and direct commands to the demon; and physical signs confirming completion. The 1999 revision (De Exorcismis et Supplicationibus Quibusdam) reinforced the requirement of psychiatric evaluation prior to proceedings. The Vatican's International Association of Exorcists, founded 1990 and recognised by the Holy See in 2014, reported membership of approximately 250 Catholic priests across 30 countries as of 2014, with demand for exorcism reportedly increasing in Europe and Latin America.

Protestant Exorcism Traditions

Early Protestant reformers generally distanced their movements from formal exorcism ritual, interpreting Catholic exorcism as superstition. Nevertheless, charismatic and Pentecostal traditions recovered robust exorcism practice from the 19th century onward. John Wimber's "power evangelism" and the Third Wave Charismatic movement institutionalised "deliverance ministry" — the expulsion of demons through prayer and command — as a normative element of Christian discipleship rather than exceptional clerical activity. Contemporary deliverance ministries operate across Pentecostal, charismatic, Baptist, and independent Protestant denominations globally, with extensive published methodology (Derek Prince, Frank Hammond, Neil Anderson). The World Health Organization has documented cases of harm resulting from unregulated exorcism practices in deliverance contexts.

Eastern Orthodox Practice

Eastern Orthodox Christianity maintains exorcism prayers as part of the baptismal rite and as a distinct pastoral practice. The Euchologion contains prayers of exorcism attributed to Basil the Great and John Chrysostom. Orthodox tradition distinguishes between prelest (spiritual delusion — a form of self-deception sometimes presenting as demonic) and genuine possession, with discernment by experienced spiritual fathers (staretz) considered essential. Russian Orthodox and Greek Orthodox traditions each retain active exorcism practice. The Holy Mountain of Athos has historically been associated with exorcism and demonic encounter narratives, documented in collections such as the Gerontikon.

IV. Islamic & Middle Eastern Traditions

Ruqyah — Islamic Exorcism

Ruqyah (from Arabic: incantation, recitation) refers to Quranic recitation and supplication as a healing and exorcistic practice. Quranic verses — most prominently Al-Fatiha, Ayat al-Kursi (2:255), and the Mu'awwidhatayn (Suras 113–114) — are recited over or upon an afflicted person to expel possessing jinn. Ruqyah is distinguished within Islamic jurisprudence from prohibited sorcery (sihr): licit ruqyah uses only Quranic verses, divine names, and hadith-authenticated prayers. Ruqyah practitioners (raqi) operate across the Muslim world and in diaspora communities; debates within Islamic scholarly tradition address the conditions for licit practice and the boundaries distinguishing religious healing from prohibited occultism. Cases of harm from prolonged or coercive ruqyah procedures have been documented by human rights organisations.

Zar Possession & Exorcism (Northeast Africa & Arabian Peninsula)

The zar tradition — a spirit possession and exorcism complex found across Ethiopia, Egypt, Sudan, Somalia, and the Arabian Peninsula — involves affliction by zar spirits (typically understood as neither fully demonic nor fully divine), ceremonial negotiation with the possessing spirit through music, dance, incense, and animal sacrifice, and eventual resolution through the spirit's adoption rather than expulsion. The zar cult is primarily practised by women; its ritual specialists (kudiya) are often women who were themselves formerly possessed. Janice Boddy's Wombs and Alien Spirits (1989) and Luc Thorsen-Enemark's fieldwork provide anthropological analysis. The tradition is condemned by mainstream Islamic clerics as bidʿah (forbidden innovation) but persists due to its social and therapeutic functions.

V. South & East Asian Traditions

Hindu Exorcism (Ojha, Jhad-Phunk, Tantric Practice)

Hindu exorcism encompasses multiple traditions operating across different social strata. The ojha (village exorcist-healer) employs spirit-coercion techniques involving trance, mantras, and physical interventions to expel bhuta/preta (spirits of the dead) or improperly propitiated deities. Jhad-phunk ("sweeping and blowing") involves ritual sweeping of the body with neem branches and blowing of mantras. Tantric exorcism draws on the tradition of the Atharva Veda — the fourth Veda, heavily oriented toward apotropaic and exorcistic magic — and employs elaborate ritual structures, yantras, and mantras from Shakta and Shaiva traditions. Sudhir Kakar's Shamans, Mystics and Doctors (1982) and Frederick Smith's The Self Possessed (2006) provide ethnographic documentation of living Hindu possession and exorcism traditions.

Buddhist Exorcism Traditions

Theravada, Mahayana, and Vajrayana Buddhist traditions each contain distinct but related exorcism practices. In Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia, Pirit (protective Pali chanting) serves both preventive and remedial functions against demonic affliction; elaborate Tovil ceremonies (all-night exorcism rituals) involve costumed dancers impersonating and appeasing demon figures. In Tibetan Buddhism, the tradition of Dorje Drolö (fierce wrathful deity) and the use of the Phurba (ritual dagger) in ritual context serve exorcistic functions; the Chöd practice — offering one's body to demons as a means of transcending fear — inverts conventional exorcism by voluntary invitation rather than expulsion. Tenzin Wangyal Rinpoche and other Bon/Buddhist teachers have written on possession and exorcism within the Tibetan framework.

Taoist Exorcism (China)

Taoist ritual specialists — the Zhengyi (Orthodox Unity) and Quanzhen (Complete Perfection) orders — maintain extensive exorcism liturgies dating to the Celestial Masters movement founded by Zhang Daoling (c. 142 CE). The exorcism complex includes the Bugang (pacing the Big Dipper), the deployment of talismans (fu) against malevolent spirits, invocation of divine generals (shen bing) to compel and expel demons, and the theatrical exorcism performances of the Nuo tradition. The Judge Zhong Kui, a classical Chinese icon, is among the most recognised exorcistic figures in East Asian culture. Kristofer Schipper's The Taoist Body and Robert Hymes's research document ritual exorcism in medieval and early modern Chinese contexts.

Japanese Traditions (Harae, Chinkon, Onmyōdō)

Japanese exorcism traditions operate within both Shinto and Buddhist frameworks, often syncretised in the pre-Meiji shinbutsu-shūgō (amalgamation) tradition. Harae (purification rite) and Misogi (lustral purification by water) are Shinto cleansing rites that remove kegare (ritual pollution) attributed to malevolent spirits or impurity. Chinkon (spirit pacification) involves inviting the spirit into a medium and then calming or dismissing it. The Onmyōji — specialists in Onmyōdō (the Way of Yin-Yang), exemplified historically by Abe no Seimei (921–1005 CE) — performed exorcisms at the Heian imperial court, expelling mono no ke (malevolent spirits) from afflicted aristocrats through elaborate ritual procedure documented in the Genji Monogatari and the Pillow Book.

VI. African, Indigenous & Shamanic Traditions

Sub-Saharan African Traditions

Across sub-Saharan Africa, spiritual affliction by malevolent entities, ancestral spirits, or witchcraft is addressed by specialist practitioners — Sangoma (Zulu/Nguni diviner-healer), Nganga (Bantu ritual specialist), Babalawo (Yoruba Ifá divination priest) — who diagnose the spirit cause and prescribe or perform remediation. The Bori spirit possession and exorcism complex of the Hausa (Nigeria/Niger) shares structural features with the East African zar tradition: afflicting spirits are identified through divination, ritually placated through dance and sacrifice, and managed through ongoing relational negotiation rather than simple expulsion. Sheila Walker's anthropological research documented the persistence and transformation of these traditions in both African and diaspora contexts.

Shamanic Traditions (Siberia, Central Asia, Americas)

The shaman — the specialist in controlled spirit interaction across Siberian, Central Asian, and many indigenous American traditions — occupies a position that encompasses both spirit-invitation and spirit-expulsion functions. In Siberian and Mongolian shamanism as documented by Mircea Eliade (Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy, 1951) and more recent ethnographers, illness was understood as soul-loss (requiring soul retrieval) or spirit intrusion (requiring extraction). The shaman's trance journey to spirit realms to negotiate with or combat afflicting entities constitutes the oldest documented exorcism technology in the ethnographic record. Michael Harner's Core Shamanism formulated a cross-cultural extraction technique based on comparative analysis of indigenous traditions; contemporary neo-shamanic practitioners operate this framework in clinical and spiritual healing contexts globally.

Indigenous American Traditions

Indigenous North American exorcism and spirit-extraction practices vary by nation and tradition. Navajo chantways — multi-day ceremonial complexes performed by trained hataalii (singers/medicine men) using sand paintings, prayer bundles, and song sequences — address spirit-caused illness through narrative re-enactment of mythological healing events. The Iroquois False Face Society performs masquerade ceremonies to drive away disease spirits. In Mesoamerican traditions, the curandera/curandero tradition (which persists across Mexico, Central America, and the US Southwest) incorporates limpia (spiritual cleansing using eggs, herbs, or candles) and confrontational exorcistic prayer to expel malevolent entities or counteract brujería (witchcraft). Ester Hernandez and Elena Avila's ethnographic work document living curanderismo practice.

Afro-Caribbean & Diaspora Traditions

Afro-Caribbean religions that developed under conditions of the transatlantic slave trade preserve West and Central African spirit-management traditions within syncretic frameworks. In Haitian Vodou, malevolent spirits (baka) may afflict individuals; remediation involves ritual offerings, ceremony, and in serious cases the intervention of a houngan or mambo (priest/priestess). Cuban Palo Monte — deriving from Kongo traditions — involves the palero working with ancestral spirits (nfumbi) both to afflict and to heal; exorcism of malevolent spirits uses specific ritual actions involving the nganga (consecrated vessel). Brazilian Umbanda and Quimbanda traditions both address spirit intrusion and employ mediumistic exorcism in therapeutic contexts, documented by researchers including David Hess and Diana deG. Brown.

VII. Contemporary Practice & Clinical Intersection

Psychiatry, Dissociation, & Differential Diagnosis

The clinical relationship between exorcism and psychiatry has evolved from outright dismissal to cautious engagement. The DSM-5 Cultural Formulation Interview and associated guidelines explicitly acknowledge possession trance as a culturally shaped presentation requiring contextual interpretation rather than automatic pathologisation. Richard Gallagher, a psychiatrist and professor at New York Medical College, has published clinical papers (2008, 2016) presenting cases he assessed as consistent with genuine possession after exclusion of known psychiatric and neurological causes. The primary differential diagnoses — dissociative identity disorder, temporal lobe epilepsy, schizophrenia spectrum disorder, and conversion disorder — do not fully explain all reported phenomenology across clinical case series, according to proponents of a genuine possession hypothesis.

Notable Modern Cases

The Anneliese Michel case (Klingenberg, Germany, 1976) remains the most extensively documented modern exorcism case: 67 audio-recorded sessions over 10 months, conducted by two priests under episcopal authorisation, preceded Michel's death from dehydration and malnutrition. Court proceedings convicted the priests and Michel's parents of negligent homicide; the case prompted revision of German Catholic exorcism guidelines. Father Gabriele Amorth, the Vatican's chief exorcist from 1986 until his death in 2016, claimed over 70,000 exorcisms performed and published detailed case typologies emphasising the need for psychiatric collaboration. The 2005 death of Tae Yeon-nam in Korea during a church exorcism ritual, and multiple cases from the UK and Australia involving children subjected to exorcism practices, demonstrate the continuing intersection of exorcism belief with safeguarding concerns.

Anthropological & Cognitive Perspectives

Anthropologist Erika Bourguignon's cross-cultural survey found trance and possession states institutionalised in 74% of 488 sampled societies, establishing the near-universality of possession belief as a fundamental datum for any theory. Cognitive scientists of religion — including Pascal Boyer, Justin Barrett, and Harvey Whitehouse — have proposed that possession and exorcism beliefs exploit evolved cognitive mechanisms: agency detection, mind-body dualism intuitions, and the social transmission of minimally counter-intuitive concepts. Etzel Cardeña's review of dissociative states in shamanism and possession notes that the phenomenological overlap between culturally structured possession experiences and clinical dissociative disorders does not determine whether the experiences share underlying mechanisms — the question remains methodologically open.

Parapsychological Research

William Roll's poltergeist research, the Rhine Research Center's case protocols, and the work of investigators including Barry Taff and Loyd Auerbach have attempted to apply empirical methods to claims of possession and exorcism. Roll's concept of recurrent spontaneous psychokinesis (RSPK) centred on a living agent rather than an invading entity — reframing the exorcism target as a psychodynamic process rather than an external spirit. The Society for Psychical Research (SPR) archives contain a substantial case file collection on possession and exorcism reports reviewed against the standards of evidence applied to mediumship and other psi claims. Divergences between parapsychological, theological, and psychiatric frameworks for the same reported phenomena reflect unresolved foundational questions about the nature of consciousness, personal identity, and the possible existence of discarnate agency.

All traditions and cases are presented from published theological, historical, ethnographic, and clinical sources. No conclusions about the efficacy or metaphysical validity of any exorcism tradition are asserted. Differential diagnosis frameworks, anthropological context, and scholarly references are provided throughout to support critical evaluation of reported phenomena.
