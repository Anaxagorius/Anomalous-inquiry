🌆 Phoenix Lights (1997)

Overview: Two Distinct Events

The events of March 13, 1997 over Nevada, Arizona, and the state of Sonora in Mexico constitute one of the most extensively witnessed UAP events in recorded history. Thousands of eyewitnesses reported unusual aerial phenomena across a corridor spanning hundreds of miles, and the events were captured on numerous home video cameras. However, careful analysis of witness reports and timelines reveals that two distinct and probably unrelated events occurred on the same evening — a distinction that has been repeatedly obscured in popular accounts.

The first event, beginning at approximately 8:15 pm MST, involved the passage of a large, structured, V-shaped or boomerang-shaped formation of lights tracking silently from northwest to southeast across Nevada and into Arizona. The second event, at approximately 10:00 pm MST, involved a stationary arc of reddish-orange lights that appeared to hover over the Estrella Mountains southwest of Phoenix before disappearing. These two events differ sharply in their character, timing, and plausible explanations, and conflating them has generated considerable confusion in subsequent analyses.

The V-Formation: First Event (8:15 pm)

The first event was tracked across a path beginning near Henderson, Nevada, proceeding through Kingman, Prescott, and Dewey in Arizona, through the Phoenix metropolitan area, and continuing south to Tucson, covering roughly 300 miles. The formation was consistently described as a triangular or V-shaped arrangement of five to seven large, non-blinking lights, attached to or part of a single dark, solid structure of enormous apparent size — estimates from witnesses placing it between 1/4 mile and 1 mile across.

The object or formation moved silently, at low altitude (estimated at 1,000–2,000 feet), and at a relatively slow speed for an aircraft — estimated by witnesses at between 30 and 60 mph. There was no engine noise whatsoever despite the proximity of many witnesses who were outside. The lights were described as a warm white, sometimes amber, and were arranged in a fixed geometric configuration that maintained position relative to one another throughout the transit. Multiple witnesses including pilots, police officers, and trained observers filed independent reports consistent in their core elements.

The object was tracked by amateur astronomers and sky-watchers throughout its transit. Witness Tim Ley, a former municipal employee in Phoenix, described watching it approach from miles away as it passed directly overhead at low altitude, obscuring stars as it passed — indicating a dark, solid structure rather than a loose formation of separate aircraft. His entire family, including his son and grandson, provided corroborating accounts.

Governor Fife Symington and Official Response

Among the witnesses to the first event was Fife Symington, then serving as Governor of Arizona, who was in Scottsdale at the time and observed the V-formation passing overhead. Symington's initial public response, in 1997, was to call a press conference to announce that the "culprit" had been caught — and then produce a staff member dressed in an alien costume, mocking public concern about the sightings. The stunt was widely criticised as dismissive of thousands of witnesses and deflected serious investigation.

A full decade later, in 2007, Symington publicly reversed his position. In a statement to CNN and other outlets he confirmed that he had personally witnessed the V-formation on March 13, 1997, that it was enormous, silent, and unlike any aircraft he had ever seen in his capacity as a former Air Force pilot, and that he had staged the press conference alien stunt to "lighten the mood" of a frightened public rather than because he doubted what he had seen. He subsequently advocated publicly for a congressional investigation into the Phoenix Lights, calling it a genuine unknown of national security significance.

Multiple attempts at congressional inquiry were made by Arizona representatives, including a push for a formal investigation by then-Congressman Ed Pastor. The Air Force declined to comment on the V-formation, acknowledging only the second event — the flares — as the explanation for the Phoenix Lights.

The 10 pm Lights: Second Event

The second event, beginning at approximately 10:00 pm, was visually distinct from the V-formation. A series of bright, reddish-orange lights appeared in a roughly horizontal arc to the southwest of Phoenix, appeared stationary or slowly moving, and then extinguished sequentially from left to right. The lights were captured on multiple home video recordings, several of which were broadcast nationally on CNN and other networks in the days following the event.

The Arizona Air National Guard subsequently identified this second event as the dropping of LUU-2B/B illumination flares by A-10 aircraft from the 104th Fighter Squadron during a training exercise over the Barry M. Goldwater Range southwest of Phoenix. The flares were released at approximately 15,000 feet altitude and burned for several minutes before being occluded by the Estrella Mountains from the perspective of Phoenix observers, creating the visual impression of lights appearing and then disappearing in place. This explanation is broadly accepted as accurate for the 10 pm event by most investigators.

The flare explanation has been applied inappropriately by many media outlets and government spokespersons to the entire Phoenix Lights phenomenon, including the 8:15 pm V-formation — despite the fact that flares could not account for a slow-moving, silent, structured object that transited 300 miles across two states and was observed from directly below by hundreds of witnesses over a period of approximately 45 minutes. The two events require separate analyses.

Dr. Lynne Kitei's Documentation

Physician Dr. Lynne Kitei, a prominent Phoenix-area doctor, documented unusual lights over Phoenix beginning as early as 1995 — two years before the mass sighting. On March 13, 1997 she captured the lights on video from her home in the Phoenix Mountain Preserve area. Kitei subsequently dedicated years to collecting and analysing witness testimonies and became the primary civilian researcher on the Phoenix Lights phenomenon, publishing The Phoenix Lights: A Skeptic's Discovery That We Are Not Alone (2004) and directing a documentary film on the subject. Her photographic and video evidence predating the 1997 event suggests a possible pattern of UAP activity in the Phoenix area that predates the mass sighting.

Scale, Context, and Significance

The Phoenix Lights are consistently cited as one of the largest mass UAP sightings on record by witness count. Estimates of the number of witnesses to the V-formation event range from several hundred to several thousand across Arizona and Nevada, with the subsequent media coverage prompting thousands more to come forward in the following days and weeks. The National UFO Reporting Center received hundreds of reports on the night of March 13 alone.

Researchers have drawn comparisons between the Phoenix V-formation and the Belgian Wave of 1989–1991, during which thousands of witnesses across Belgium reported large, slow-moving, silent triangular craft with lights at the vertices — a description remarkably similar to the Phoenix accounts. The Belgian craft were tracked on radar and scrambled Belgian Air Force F-16s, providing an independent and military-corroborated sighting of a similarly described object. Whether these events involve the same phenomenon remains an open research question.

What distinguishes the Phoenix Lights as a research case is the combination of mass witness testimony across a wide geographic area, a credible high-status witness (a sitting governor and former Air Force pilot) who later reversed his dismissive public stance, photographic and video documentation, and the existence of an official explanation that — while adequate for one of the two events — wholly fails to account for the other. The V-formation of March 13, 1997 remains unexplained by any known aircraft, formation flying, or atmospheric phenomenon, and continues to be investigated by civilian researchers.

Ongoing Witness Accounts and Legacy

Annual commemorations of the Phoenix Lights are held in Arizona, drawing witnesses who wish to share their accounts and researchers who continue to compile the evidentiary record. Many original witnesses report that the event had a profound and lasting impact on their worldview, comparable in some accounts to religious experiences. The event generated substantial cultural output including documentaries, books, and media coverage that has kept it in public consciousness for nearly three decades.

The Phoenix Lights hold a notable place in the broader UAP disclosure conversation. When the U.S. government's UAP Task Force reports and subsequent AARO (All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office) processes began producing official acknowledgements of unexplained aerial phenomena in the 2020s, many analysts noted that the Phoenix Lights — one of the most witnessed and documented events of the 20th century — had never been officially explained beyond the inadequate flare attribution. Symington's reversal, the scale of the V-formation sighting, and the case's evidentiary richness continue to make it a cornerstone reference in serious UAP research.
