👁️ Possession & Overshadowing States

Phenomenology and Cross-Cultural Distribution

Possession states — experiences in which a person's ordinary consciousness is partially or wholly replaced, supplemented, or co-occupied by a distinct presence, entity, or identity — are among the most widely distributed phenomena in the entire cross-cultural literature of anomalous consciousness. Anthropologist Erika Bourguignon's 1968 survey of 488 societies found that 74% had institutionalised forms of possession or trance, making it one of the most universal human institutions. This distribution indicates that whatever the ultimate nature of these states, they arise from or exploit capacities deeply embedded in human neurobiology and social organisation.

Phenomenologically, possession states vary along several dimensions. The primary distinction is between full possession — in which the ordinary personality is completely displaced and the host has no memory of the episode — and co-consciousness or overshadowing, in which the ordinary self remains partially present and aware but perceives itself as accompanied, influenced, or partially overridden by another presence. Within these categories, the nature of the possessing entity, the mode of induction, the social and ritual context, and the purposes served vary enormously.

Vodou and Candomblé

In Haitian Vodou, the possession of initiates (chevaux, "horses") by lwa (divine spirits) is the central ritual event and theological reality of the tradition. The lwa "mount" their horses during ceremony, producing characteristic personality transformations consistent with the identity of the possessing spirit: the warrior Ogou displays militaristic aggression and demands rum; the old and infirm Gede speaks of death and sexuality with ribald humour; the regal Ezili Freda is elegant and perfumed. Witnesses describe the possessed individual's voice, gait, vocabulary, facial expressions, and body posture changing profoundly and characteristically — changes that are culturally standardised for each lwa, yet not reducible to conscious performance given the amnesia often reported by horses after the event.

In Candomblé — an Afro-Brazilian tradition with Yoruba roots — Orixás (divine beings associated with natural forces) possess initiated practitioners (filhos de santo, "children of the saint") during rituals. As in Vodou, the possession is characterised by radical personality transformation, specific physical manifestations for each Orixá, and often partial or complete amnesia for the episode. Both traditions understand the medium not as fabricating a performance but as genuinely hosting a distinct non-human intelligence whose nature and identity transcends the individual medium.

Spiritism and Western Traditions

Spiritism, the tradition founded by Hippolyte Léon Denizard Rivail (Allan Kardec) in France following the publication of Le Livre des Esprits (The Spirits' Book) in 1857, developed an elaborate theoretical and practical framework for understanding and engaging with mediumistic phenomena, including possession-like channelling states. Kardec held that spirits of the deceased and other non-physical beings could and did communicate through mediums, and that this constituted a scientifically investigable reality. Brazilian Kardecism (Espírito) became a major religious movement with millions of adherents, incorporating healing practices in which mediums are temporarily inhabited by healing spirits.

Victorian Spiritualism produced extensive documentation of trance mediumship — states in which a medium ostensibly yields control of their vocal apparatus, hand, or entire body to a communicating spirit. The physical phenomena associated with certain trance mediums (D.D. Home, Eusapia Palladino, Leonora Piper) were investigated by the Society for Psychical Research with methodological rigour unusual for the era. Leonora Piper in particular was studied by William James, Richard Hodgson, and others over many years; James, characteristically cautious, concluded she was his "white crow" — the one case sufficient to establish that the possibility of genuine mediumistic information transmission could not simply be dismissed.

Pierre Janet and the Psychology of Dissociation

The French psychologist and neurologist Pierre Janet (1859–1947) was the first to provide a systematic psychological analysis of possession states, which he situated within his theory of psychological dissociation. Janet's concept of désagrégation mentale — the splitting of mental systems that should operate in integrated coordination — provided a framework in which possession could be understood as the emergence of a dissociated part of the personality to executive control, rather than as literal spirit occupation. His work with hysterical patients who displayed secondary personalities he could communicate with separately from the primary self laid the foundation for all subsequent dissociation research.

Janet's framework remains valuable not as a denial of the experiential reality of possession but as an account of the psychological mechanism through which such states are possible — a mechanism that does not, in itself, settle the question of whether the possessing entity has an existence independent of the host's psychology. The co-consciousness model — in which a secondary self with distinct memories, personality, and capacities operates in parallel with the primary self — is now well established in DID research and provides a model for possession phenomenology that requires no commitment on the ontological question.

Xenoglossy and Information Infusion

Xenoglossy — the apparent production of a language not learned in the ordinary biographical sense — has been reported in possession and trance states across multiple traditions and investigated by researchers including Ian Stevenson of the University of Virginia Division of Perceptual Studies. Stevenson's case studies, including the detailed examination of two American women who, under hypnosis or in trance states, spoke fluently in Swedish and in a now-rare German dialect respectively, are among the most carefully documented anomalous information acquisition cases in the literature. The xenoglossy cases are relevant to possession research because they suggest that, in at least some instances, the possessing identity brings genuine information — linguistic competence — that is not explicable by the host's documented learning history.

A parallel phenomenon is knowledge infusion: possession states in which the medium or horse demonstrates access to information — biographical details of the deceased, historical facts, medical knowledge — not available through normal channels. The Spiritist healing tradition specifically relies on this phenomenon: mediums channelling deceased physicians are held to produce accurate medical diagnoses and treatment recommendations. The evidential quality of such cases varies enormously, but a subset has been investigated carefully enough to establish that some form of anomalous information access is occurring.

Walk-In States and NHI Interfacing Claims

The walk-in concept — the claim that a non-human or post-human consciousness has taken up residence in a human body, either temporarily displacing or cohabiting with the original personality — appears in New Age and experiencer literature from the 1970s onward and has been mapped by researchers including Ruth Montgomery. In experiencer communities connected to UAP contact, a distinct variant has been reported: accounts of a temporary but profound consciousness displacement or co-occupation during or following contact events, in which the experiencer's ordinary identity recedes and a different quality of awareness — described as vast, non-local, and non-human — occupies their perception and volition for a period.

These contact-related possession-adjacent states typically involve characteristic features that distinguish them from spontaneous DID-type dissociation: the clear sense of an external intelligence with a coherent agenda; the infusion of specific information or perspectives; physical effects on the experiencer's body (heat, vibration, electrical phenomena); and in some cases, accurate transmission of verifiable information. The phenomenological overlap between these accounts and the traditional possession states of Vodou and Candomblé is striking from an anthropological perspective — suggesting either a common neurological mechanism producing culturally inflected experiences, or a genuine class of contact events that cultures variously interpret as spirit possession, divine inspiration, or alien interfacing.
