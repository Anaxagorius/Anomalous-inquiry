👁️ Remote Viewing

Overview

Remote viewing is a structured practice for acquiring information about a distant, shielded, or otherwise inaccessible target using ostensibly extrasensory means, with the session conducted according to a defined protocol designed to minimise cueing, expectation effects, and analysis overlay. The term was coined by Ingo Swann in 1971 during early experiments at the American Society for Psychical Research, and the practice became central to a classified U.S. government intelligence programme that ran — under various programme names — from 1972 to 1995. Unlike free-form psychic reading, remote viewing is distinguished by its emphasis on protocol, documentation, and blind judging — making it amenable to scientific evaluation in ways that informal psychic claims are not.

History: The Stargate Programme

The programme that became known as Stargate began in 1972 under the project name SCANATE (scan by coordinate), when the CIA funded initial experiments at Stanford Research Institute (SRI) International in Menlo Park, California. The programme's existence was prompted in part by intelligence reports that the Soviet Union was investing heavily in psychotronics research, and by physicist Hal Puthoff's proposal that anomalous information acquisition might have intelligence utility.

Over its 23-year lifespan the programme operated under multiple designations including GONDOLA WISH, GRILL FLAME, CENTER LANE, SUN STREAK, and finally STARGATE. Funding came from the CIA, the Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA), the Army's Intelligence and Security Command (INSCOM), and other agencies. The programme involved both research (at SRI and later the Science Applications International Corporation, SAIC) and operational intelligence collection using trained remote viewers assigned to a military unit at Fort Meade, Maryland.

By 1995, the programme had cost approximately $20 million over its two-decade existence, had involved over a dozen primary viewers, and had produced thousands of remote viewing session reports. The CIA commissioned the American Institutes for Research (AIR) to conduct an independent evaluation, resulting in the 1995 AIR report by statistician Jessica Utts and psychologist Ray Hyman. Utts concluded that the statistical evidence for remote viewing was compelling and that the phenomenon deserved serious scientific attention. Hyman agreed that the overall effect was above chance but disputed the methodology sufficiently to withhold endorsement of psi as an explanation. The programme was officially terminated in 1995.

Key Figures

Ingo Swann (1933–2013) was the most theoretically innovative participant in the programme. An artist and psychic, Swann coined the term "remote viewing," participated in the earliest SRI experiments, and developed the highly structured Coordinate Remote Viewing (CRV) protocol in a collaborative training programme with Puthoff beginning in 1978. Swann described CRV as a learnable skill rather than a gift, arguing that any person of normal cognitive function could be trained to access psi information through the CRV structure. His accounts of remote viewing sessions — including a controversial claimed session in which he described anomalous structures on the dark side of the Moon and detailed features of Jupiter's atmosphere before Pioneer 10 flyby confirmation — remain among the most debated in the literature.

Pat Price (1918–1975), a retired police officer and commissioner, was the programme's most operationally effective early viewer. His remote viewing of the Soviet research facility at Semipalatinsk and his independent description of a classified U.S. underground facility at Mount Weather, Virginia (which he was not told about) are among the most cited early successes. Price's death from a heart attack in 1975 was considered a significant operational loss; some researchers have speculated about its circumstances.

Russell Targ and Hal Puthoff, both physicists at SRI, were the programme's principal scientific directors during the 1970s and early 1980s. Their 1974 Nature paper describing successful remote viewing experiments by Swann and Price — the first peer-reviewed publication of such results in a major mainstream scientific journal — attracted enormous attention. Targ subsequently co-authored Mind Reach (1977) and continued remote viewing research after leaving SRI.

Edwin May became the programme's principal scientific director in 1985 after Puthoff's departure and directed the SAIC research phase. May is a physicist and rigorous sceptic of his own programme's claims — he has argued publicly that the evidence supports a "signal-to-noise" model of psi as a weak, unreliable, but real information channel, while rejecting the more spectacular operational claims sometimes made about Stargate's effectiveness.

Joseph McMoneagle (designated Remote Viewer 001) was the programme's most decorated and consistent operational viewer, receiving the Legion of Merit for his intelligence contributions. McMoneagle participated in hundreds of operational sessions and has continued public remote viewing demonstrations and writing after the programme's declassification. His book Remote Viewing Secrets (2000) is considered one of the most candid accounts of the operational programme's realities.

Protocols: CRV and ERV

Coordinate Remote Viewing (CRV) is the most structured and extensively documented protocol. Developed by Ingo Swann in collaboration with Hal Puthoff and later systematised by Army trainer Tom McNear and Warrant Officer Lyn Buchanan, CRV proceeds through six distinct stages:

  • Stage I: Initial contact — the viewer writes the target coordinates (a random numerical string used as a label for the target) and notes the first automatic impressions: the ideogram (an automatic pen movement representing the gestalt of the target) and its decoded meaning (land, water, structure, energy, life).
  • Stage II: Sensory data — the viewer consciously explores and records sensory impressions: colours, textures, temperatures, sounds, tastes, smells, and dimensional impressions.
  • Stage III: Dimensional data — the viewer sketches the target's spatial configuration and dimensional relationships.
  • Stage IV: Aesthetic impact — the viewer describes emotional and aesthetic qualities, functional impressions, and intangible attributes.
  • Stage V: Tangibles — specific physical objects, details, and descriptions.
  • Stage VI: Three-dimensional modelling and final integration of all prior data.

Extended Remote Viewing (ERV) uses a different approach: the viewer enters a deeply relaxed, semi-hypnotic state (typically guided by a monitor who asks questions and records responses), and provides free-form verbal descriptions of the target. ERV was the method used most often by McMoneagle and was considered by some operators to allow access to deeper, more complex target information at the cost of greater susceptibility to contamination by imagination and expectation.

Notable Operational Successes

Among the most frequently cited operational successes of the Stargate programme are:

  • Soviet submarine at Severodvinsk: Pat Price and Ingo Swann independently described details of a Soviet naval facility and shipyard at Severodvinsk, providing information about classified submarine construction that was subsequently assessed as partially accurate by NSA analysts.
  • Chinese nuclear test facility: A viewer (details classified) provided descriptions of a Chinese nuclear test site that analysts assessed as providing useful intelligence about facility layout.
  • U.S. hostages in Iran (1979–1981): McMoneagle and other viewers were tasked to locate the American hostages being held in Iran. McMoneagle reported providing information about the hostages' location that was assessed as providing useful context, though the intelligence could not be acted upon operationally.
  • Missing Soviet TU-95 Backfire: A viewer provided geographic coordinates that led to the discovery of a downed Soviet aircraft in Africa, a result cited by Senator Claiborne Pell in 1995 as justification for continued programme funding.

The operational successes claimed for Stargate must be evaluated with significant caution. Intelligence assessments of remote viewing sessions were conducted under conditions where assessors were not always fully blind to the sessions' provenance, and the tendency to focus on accurate elements while discounting misses (the "hit rate fallacy") may inflate the programme's apparent accuracy. Edwin May himself has stated that he believes the programme's research results were more reliable than its operational results.

The AIR Evaluation and Termination

The 1995 AIR evaluation commissioned by the CIA consisted of two components: a statistical analysis by Jessica Utts (University of California, Davis) and a critique by Ray Hyman (University of Oregon). Utts examined the SAIC experimental database of 1,653 research sessions and concluded that the evidence for remote viewing was statistically robust — effect sizes were significant and replicable, and the effect could not be explained by known experimental artefacts. She recommended continued research. Hyman acknowledged the statistical anomaly but argued that the overall methodology was insufficiently rigorous to rule out all normal explanations and declined to endorse the psi interpretation.

Based on the AIR report, the CIA recommended programme termination. The DIA concurred. The programme was officially closed in late 1995. Many participants and researchers regarded the termination as premature — noting that the programme was discontinued not because the research failed to show results but because the results were judged insufficiently reliable for operational intelligence use, a higher standard than scientific proof. The 20-year database of research and operational sessions was declassified in stages, with the bulk of non-sensitive materials released through the CIA's online FOIA reading room.

PEAR Remote Perception Studies

Independent of the Stargate programme, Robert Jahn and Brenda Dunne's PEAR laboratory at Princeton University conducted a parallel research programme in remote perception from the late 1970s to the laboratory's closure in 2007. PEAR's approach differed from CRV in using a standardised binary-coded judging protocol: percipient descriptions were rated against 30 descriptor dimensions, and a binary match score was calculated for each session against both the actual target and a pool of decoy targets. Across a database of over 650 formal experimental trials, PEAR found a significant above-chance correlation between percipient descriptions and target sites (z ≈ 3.7, p ≈ 10⁻⁴). Crucially, PEAR's time-displaced sessions — in which the percipient attempted to describe a target the agent had not yet visited — showed equivalent effect sizes to real-time sessions, suggesting temporal independence of the psi process.

Post-Stargate: Civilian Programmes and Current Research

Following Stargate's declassification, remote viewing became a substantial cottage industry. Numerous former programme participants established civilian training organisations. Paul H. Smith, a former Army Stargate officer and CRV trainer, published the definitive public CRV training manual and founded Remote Viewing Instructional Services (RVIS). Lyn Buchanan founded Problems, Solutions, Innovations (P>S>I). McMoneagle founded Intuitive Intelligence Applications. These organisations have trained thousands of civilian remote viewers in CRV and related protocols.

The International Remote Viewing Association (IRVA), founded in 1999, serves as the professional organisation for the field, holding annual conferences and maintaining standards for the practice. The Farsight Institute, founded by Courtney Brown, conducts remote viewing research and has published sessions targeting historical events and extraterrestrial subjects — work regarded with varying degrees of scepticism by more methodologically conservative practitioners.

Academic research on remote viewing continues at the Koestler Parapsychology Unit, the Rhine Research Center, and several European institutions. Recent studies have used functional neuroimaging to investigate the neural correlates of remote viewing sessions, comparing brain activity during accurate and inaccurate trials. The convergence of the Stargate database, PEAR results, and ongoing academic research represents the most extensive evidential base for any specific anomalous cognition paradigm — and its assessment continues to divide empirical scientists along lines that parallel the broader debate about the existence and nature of psi.
